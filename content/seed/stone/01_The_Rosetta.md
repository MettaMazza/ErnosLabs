SLAB 1 — THE ROSETTA
THE DECODER

Without this slab, every other slab is noise.
This teaches you how to read what follows.


OBJECTS — WHAT THINGS ARE

SUN — the bright circle in the sky that gives heat and light. It rises, crosses the sky, and sets. When it is gone, darkness comes.
MOON — the pale circle that appears at night. It changes shape over roughly 30 days, from nothing to full circle and back.
STAR — a small point of light in the night sky. Stars do not move relative to each other. They are fixed.
WATER — the clear liquid that falls from the sky, fills rivers, and sustains all life. It freezes solid when cold, boils to vapor when hot.
FIRE — heat and light produced by burning. It consumes wood, fat, and gas. It transforms materials. It kills and it sustains.
STONE — hard solid material from the ground. It does not burn. It does not rot.
EARTH — the soil. The soft ground that plants grow from. Made of crushed stone, dead plants, and living creatures too small to see.
AIR — the invisible substance you breathe. It fills all spaces. It moves as wind.
HUMAN — you. Two legs, two arms, upright. The being reading this.
HAND — the part at the end of your arm with five fingers. Your primary tool.
EYE — the organ that detects light. You have two, in the front of your head.
TREE — a tall plant with a hard trunk, branches, and leaves. It grows for decades or centuries.
SEED — the small hard object that a plant produces. Put it in soil, add water, it grows into a new plant.
RIVER — water that flows continuously along a channel in the ground, from high ground to low ground.
MOUNTAIN — very high ground. Stone pushed up above the surrounding land.


COUNTING — NUMBERS FROM FINGERS

One finger raised = 1
Two fingers = 2
One hand, all fingers = 5
Two hands = 10
When you reach 10, make a mark on the ground or a stick. Start over.
Each mark = 10.
Ten marks = 100. Ten groups of 100 = 1000.

Nothing = 0. The empty space. The absence. It is a number.
THE FOLD: zero is not a number — it is the absence itself. The fold begins at One, not zero.

COMBINING (adding): 3 stones and 4 stones together = 7 stones.
REMOVING (subtracting): 7 stones, take away 4 = 3 stones.
EQUAL GROUPS (multiplying): 3 groups of 4 stones = 12 stones.
SPLITTING (dividing): 12 stones split into 3 equal groups = 4 stones per group.

PARTS (fractions): cut something in half = 2 pieces, each is 1/2.
Cut in 4 = each piece is 1/4.
Half of half = 1/4. This always works.
THE FOLD: halving is the fold's doubling reversed. 1, 1/2, 1/4 — each a fold. See Seed, Section 4.


LOGIC — HOW TO CONNECT IDEAS

IS: this thing is that thing. Water IS a liquid.
IS NOT: this thing is not that thing. Stone IS NOT water.
AND: both at once. Fire AND air together make flame.
OR: one or the other. You need water OR you die.
IF-THEN: when this happens, that follows. IF you heat water, THEN it boils.
MORE THAN: 7 is more than 3.
LESS THAN: 3 is less than 7.
EQUAL TO: 5 is equal to 5.
CAUSE: this made that happen. Fire CAUSED the water to boil.
CONTAINS: this is inside that. The pot CONTAINS water.


ACTIONS — WHAT YOU CAN DO TO THINGS

HEAT — make hotter by applying fire or sunlight
COOL — make colder by removing from heat, adding water, or exposing to air
CUT — divide by applying a sharp edge
JOIN — bring two things together so they stay
POUR — tilt a container so liquid flows out
PRESS — push down with force
PULL — draw toward you
PUSH — send away from you
LIFT — raise upward against weight
DROP — release so it falls
GROW — increase in size over time (living things)
DIE — stop living. All living things do this.
EAT — take food into the body
DRINK — take water into the body
BREATHE — draw air into the body and push it out
BUILD — assemble parts into a structure
BREAK — separate into parts by force
DIG — remove earth from below


PROPERTIES — WHAT THINGS ARE LIKE

HOT / COLD — fire is hot, ice is cold
HARD / SOFT — stone is hard, clay is soft
HEAVY / LIGHT — iron is heavy, feather is light
WET / DRY — water makes things wet, sun makes things dry
BRIGHT / DARK — fire is bright, night is dark
SHARP / DULL — a new blade is sharp, a worn one is dull
FULL / EMPTY — a vessel with water is full, without is empty
BIG / SMALL — a mountain is big, a seed is small
FAST / SLOW — a bird is fast, a tree is slow


BUILDING SENTENCES FROM SYMBOLS

Objects + Actions + Properties + Logic = sentences.

WATER + HEAT + FIRE = STEAM (water heated by fire becomes vapor)
SEED + EARTH + WATER + SUN = PLANT (a seed in soil with water and light grows)
STONE + HEAT + EXTREME = LIQUID (stone heated enough melts)
IRON + WATER + AIR = RUST (iron exposed to water and air crumbles)
COPPER + TIN + HEAT = BRONZE (two soft metals melted together make a hard one)
HUMAN + NO WATER + 3 DAYS = DEATH (a human without water dies in three days)
SEED + SAVE + DRY + DARK = NEXT YEAR (seeds stored dry and dark grow next season)


HOW TO MAKE FIRE

Fire is survival. Without fire, you cannot cook, purify water, smelt metal, stay warm, or see in the dark.

METHOD 1 — FRICTION (BOW DRILL)

1. FIREBOARD: a flat piece of dry softwood (cedar, willow, poplar). Cut a small notch at the edge.
2. SPINDLE: a straight, dry stick, thumb-thick, forearm length. Sharpen one end to a blunt point.
3. BOW: a curved stick with a cord tied tightly between its ends.
4. SOCKET: a stone or hard piece of wood with a small depression to hold the top of the spindle.
5. TINDER: the driest, finest material you can find — shredded bark, dry grass, cattail fluff, birch bark.

PROCEDURE:
- Place the fireboard on the ground. Put your foot on it.
- Loop the bow string once around the spindle.
- Place the spindle point in the fireboard notch.
- Press the socket on top of the spindle.
- Saw the bow back and forth rapidly. The spindle spins in the fireboard.
- Friction creates heat. Dark powder collects in the notch.
- When the powder begins to smoke on its own, stop.
- Carefully tip the glowing ember onto your tinder bundle.
- Blow gently. The tinder catches fire.

METHOD 2 — SPARK (FLINT AND STEEL)

1. Hold a piece of flint (hard, sharp-edged stone) in one hand.
2. Strike it sharply with a piece of high-carbon steel (a steel knife back works).
3. Sparks fly off the steel. Direct them onto charcloth or dry tinder.
4. Blow the ember into flame.

CHARCLOTH: cut small squares of cotton or linen cloth. Place in a sealed metal tin with one small hole. Put the tin in a fire. Smoke escapes through the hole. When smoking stops, remove and cool. The cloth is now black and catches sparks easily.

METHOD 3 — LENS (SUNLIGHT FOCUS)

1. Hold a convex lens (curved glass, thick in the middle) between the sun and your tinder.
2. Angle it until the sunlight focuses into the smallest, brightest point possible.
3. Hold steady. The tinder smokes, then ignites.

ANY clear curved glass works — the bottom of a glass bottle filled with water, a piece of clear ice shaped into a lens, a polished concave reflector.

FIRE RULES:
- Fire needs three things: HEAT + FUEL + AIR. Remove any one and the fire dies.
- Start with the smallest, finest fuel (tinder). Add slightly larger material (kindling). Then add larger pieces.
- Green or wet wood smothers fire. Use dry wood.
- Fire spreads. Clear the ground around your fire. Never leave it unattended in wind.
