SLAB 7 — THE BUILD
SHELTER AND INFRASTRUCTURE


FOUNDATIONS

Dig below the frost line (the depth where ground freezes in winter). A foundation that sits in freezing ground heaves and cracks.
Level the bottom. Make the foundation WIDER than the wall it carries. The wider the base, the more weight it can bear.
Stone or brick foundations last longest. Set in mortar for permanence.


WALLS

STONE: stack stones with their flattest faces out. Stagger the joints (never align vertical gaps). Fill gaps with mortar. Thick walls are stronger and insulate better.

BRICK: shape clay into uniform blocks. Dry. Fire in a kiln until hard. Lay in staggered rows with mortar between. Brick walls are strong, fireproof, and uniform.

TIMBER FRAME: vertical posts (driven into ground or set on stone footings), horizontal beams, diagonal braces for rigidity. Fill the spaces between timbers with clay, wattle (woven sticks), or brick.


THE ARCH

The strongest shape in building. Wedge-shaped stones (or bricks) arranged in a curve. Each stone is compressed by its neighbors. The forces flow outward and downward to the ground. The center stone (keystone) locks the arch. An arch can span a gap no single beam could cross. The same principle extends to domes (arches rotated in a circle) and vaults (arches extended in a line).
THE FOLD: each stone carries a fraction of the total load. Force redistributed as exact shares summing to one.


ROOFING

All roofs must slope for drainage. Steeper slopes shed water and snow faster.
THATCH: bundles of reeds or straw tied to a frame. Cheap, good insulation. Burns.
CLAY TILES: fired clay, overlapped. Fireproof, waterproof, heavy.
WOOD SHINGLES: thin split wood, overlapped. Durable but burns.


THE FORGE

Fire bed (heat-resistant stone or brick) + bellows (two boards hinged together with leather sides, a nozzle, and an intake valve — boards apart pulls air in, boards together forces air through nozzle into fire) + tuyere (pipe from bellows to fire) + chimney (draws smoke) + anvil (heavy flat metal or stone block) + quench trough (water or oil for rapid cooling).

Bellows are what separate a campfire from a forge. Without forced air, you cannot reach iron-smelting temperature.


THE KILN

Enclosed oven for sustained high heat. Firebox at the bottom, ware chamber above (separated by perforated floor), chimney at the top. Thick insulating walls. Load items, seal the door with clay, fire for 8-12 hours (pottery) or 24+ hours (calcium stone). Cool slowly — rapid cooling cracks. Temperature control: more fuel and wider flue = hotter; less fuel and narrower flue = cooler.


WATER

WELL: dig straight down to the water table. Line walls with stone or brick to prevent collapse.
AQUEDUCT: a channel carrying water from source to destination. Must slope continuously downhill. Even a very slight slope works.
DAM: a wall across a stream. Stores water above. Controls flow below.


SANITATION

THE LATRINE: a pit, downhill and downwind from living areas and water sources. At least 30 paces from any well, spring, or stream. Cover deposits with earth after each use. When full, fill with earth, dig a new one.

WASTE WATER: channel to a gravel-filled pit (soakaway). Gravel filters, earth absorbs.

WASH HANDS after using the latrine. Every time. With soap if available.

Contaminated water has killed more people than any weapon in history. Separate drinking water from waste water. Build the latrine before you build the house.


KNOTS

SQUARE KNOT — joins two equal ropes. Left over right, then right over left.
BOWLINE — a fixed loop that will not slip under load and can always be untied. The rescue knot.
CLOVE HITCH — quick attachment to a post. Wrap, cross over, wrap again, tuck under.
TIMBER HITCH — for dragging logs. Wrap, twist the end around itself three times. Self-tightens under load.
TRUCKERS HITCH — creates mechanical advantage for tensioning. A loop partway along the rope acts as a pulley.
THE FOLD: every machine trades force for distance at a rational fraction — half the force, twice the distance.

LASHING — binding poles together: wrap rope alternately over and under the two poles at the crossing, then wrap between them to tighten (frapping turns). This builds frames, shelters, scaffolding from poles without nails.


THE WHEEL

SOLID DISC: three planks edge to edge, held by two cross-battens. Cut a circle. Bore a center hole. Heavy but strong.
SPOKED: hub (thick disc with center hole) + spokes (rods radiating out) + rim (outer ring). Lighter, equally strong.
AXLE: straight smooth rod through the center hole. Grease with animal fat.
The wheel enables transport, grinding, pottery, pulleys, and water power.


BRIDGES

BEAM: a strong plank or log laid across a gap. Limited by the length and strength of a single piece.
ARCH: the strongest bridge form. Same principle as the building arch.
SUSPENSION: two ropes anchored on each side, planks laid across for the deck. Additional support ropes above. Spans great distances with flexible materials.


BOATS

RAFT: logs bound together. Floats. Stable. Slow. Cannot sail upwind.
DUGOUT: a hollowed log. Faster. Less stable. Requires a large tree.
SAIL: a surface that catches wind. By angling the sail relative to the wind, you can travel in almost any direction — not just downwind.
