SLAB 8 — THE SIGNAL
SENDING AND STORING INFORMATION


WRITING

Marks on a surface = stored speech. Any mark that a group agrees represents a sound, a word, or an idea is writing. The marks can be scratched in clay, carved in stone, painted on bark, or inked on paper. What matters is that the agreement is shared and consistent.

Writing is the technology that allows one mind to speak to another across time and distance. Without it, every generation starts from nothing.


PAPER

1. Gather plant fiber: inner bark, hemp, flax, cotton, reeds, straw.
2. Chop fine. Soak in water for days until soft.
3. Pound or grind into a mush (pulp) until individual fibers separate.
4. Suspend the pulp in a large vessel of water. Stir.
5. Dip a flat screen (a frame with cloth or fine mesh stretched across it) into the water. Lift. A thin layer of fibers settles on the screen.
6. Press the wet sheet to squeeze out water.
7. Dry flat in air or sun.
8. Result: paper. Repeat the process for each sheet.


INK

BASIC: grind charcoal to fine powder. Mix with water and a binder (tree sap, egg white, hide glue). The binder makes the ink stick to the surface instead of washing away.

IRON-BASED: soak crushed oak galls (round growths on oak trees) in water. Mix with iron filings or rust and tree sap. Produces a dark, permanent ink that resists fading.


PRINTING

1. Carve a design or text in REVERSE on a flat block of wood (cut away everything that should NOT print).
2. Apply ink to the raised surface.
3. Press paper onto the inked block.
4. Lift. The image transfers to the paper.
5. Repeat. One block produces hundreds of identical copies.

MOVABLE TYPE: carve individual letters as separate small blocks. Arrange them into words and lines. Ink and press. Rearrange for the next page. One set of letter blocks can print any text.


TELEGRAPH

Electricity through a wire carries a signal instantly over any distance the wire reaches.

At one end: a switch (press to connect, release to disconnect).
At the other end: a device that clicks or marks when current flows.

Short press = dot. Long press = dash. Assign patterns of dots and dashes to each letter. Now you can send any message at the speed of electricity.
THE FOLD: two symbols — dot and dash — like the fold's two generators, 2 and 3. All messages built from two.


TELEPHONE

Sound makes a thin membrane vibrate. Attach the membrane to a device that converts vibration to electrical signal. Send the signal through a wire. At the other end, convert the electrical signal back to vibration through another membrane. The second membrane reproduces the original sound. Voice travels through wire.


RADIO

An oscillating electrical current in a wire radiates invisible energy outward in all directions. Another wire (antenna) at a distance picks up this energy and converts it back to electrical current. No physical connection needed. Sound can be encoded in the signal (by varying its strength or frequency) and decoded at the receiver.
THE FOLD: signal strength falls by the inverse square. Each doubling of distance applies the fold twice — 1/4, 1/16, 1/64.


LENSES

Curved glass bends light.
CONVEX (thick in the middle, thin at the edges) = magnifies. This is a magnifying glass.
CONCAVE (thin in the middle, thick at the edges) = reduces and spreads.

TELESCOPE: two convex lenses in a tube, separated by distance. The far lens gathers light, the near one magnifies. Distant objects appear close.

MICROSCOPE: two convex lenses close together, stronger curvature. Very small objects appear large. Reveals a world invisible to the naked eye — the world where disease lives.


MAPS

A map stores information about space on a surface.

SCALE: everything shrunk by the same ratio. Mark the ratio on the map.
THE FOLD: map scale is a constant rational fraction — the territory divided into the map.
ORIENTATION: mark north on every map.
SURVEYING: measure a baseline between two known points. From each end, measure the angle to a landmark. Draw those angles on your map from the scaled baseline. Where the lines cross = the landmark's position. Repeat to fix more positions.
LEGEND: consistent symbols for water, elevation, vegetation, structures, hazards. Mark the symbols on the map.

A community with maps can plan. One without maps rediscovers its own land every generation.


INFORMATION STORAGE — DURABILITY

SPEECH — seconds. Gone when the air stops vibrating.
INK ON PAPER — decades to centuries. Destroyed by fire, water, rot.
FIRED CLAY TABLETS — thousands of years. Broken only by physical force.
CARVED STONE — tens of thousands of years. Eroded only by weather over ages.

If you want information to outlast your civilization, carve it in stone.
