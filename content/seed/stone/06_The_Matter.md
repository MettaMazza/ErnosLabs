SLAB 6 — THE MATTER
MATERIALS AND HOW TO WORK THEM


IDENTIFYING MATERIALS

HARDNESS — what scratches what. Line up materials from softest to hardest. Fingernail scratches talc and gypsum. Copper coin scratches calcium stone. Iron knife scratches copper. Quartz scratches steel. Diamond scratches everything.

STREAK — scrape the material across rough white stone. The powder color is more reliable than surface color. Iron ore leaves a red-brown streak even when the rock looks black.

DENSITY — heavy things that are small are dense. Iron is denser than wood. Gold is denser than everything common. If a yellow metal feels too light for its size, it is not gold.

HEAT — does it melt? Tin melts over a cooking fire. Copper needs a forge. Iron needs sustained high heat with bellows. Does it burn? Wood burns. Stone does not.

MAGNETISM — hold a lodestone near it. Only iron and steel are pulled.

MALLEABILITY — hit it with a hammer. Malleable materials flatten (copper, gold, hot iron). Brittle materials shatter (glass, cold cast iron, stone).


COPPER

Green or blue-green stained rock = copper ore. Crush the ore. Heat with charcoal and forced air (bellows) in a clay crucible. Copper melts out and pools at the bottom. Pour into a mold. Reddish-orange metal. Conducts heat and electricity well. Soft alone but essential for bronze.

BRONZE = COPPER + TIN

Melt copper. Add one part tin to nine parts copper. Stir. Pour into mold. Much harder than copper alone. Holds an edge. The first great alloy.
THE FOLD: 1 part in 10 = 1/10, an exact fraction in the domain (0,1]. Every alloy ratio is a fold fraction.

IRON

Red, brown, or black heavy rock with reddish streak = iron ore. Extremely common. Build a tall clay furnace. Fill with alternating layers of charcoal and crushed ore. Force air in from the bottom with bellows. Maintain for hours. Iron collects as a spongy mass at the bottom. Remove hot. Hammer repeatedly to squeeze out impurities. This is wrought iron. Strong. Magnetic. RUSTS — keep it dry and oiled.

STEEL = IRON + CARBON

Heat iron in direct contact with charcoal for extended periods. Carbon absorbs into the iron. Quench — plunge hot metal into water or oil. Much harder than iron. Holds a sharper edge. TEMPERING: after hardening, reheat gently to a straw-yellow or blue color. Cool slowly. Reduces brittleness while keeping hardness.

CALCIUM STONE + HIGH HEAT = MORTAR

Burn calcium stone in a kiln until it crumbles to white powder (burned calcium). This powder + water = violent reaction, intense heat, produces a paste. Paste + sand + water = mortar. Hardens like stone. Holds bricks and stones together. Paste + sand + gravel + water = concrete.

CHARCOAL

Pile dry hardwood in a mound. Cover with earth, leaving small air holes at the base and a chimney at the top. Light from the center. Seal the chimney when smoke turns blue. Maintain 24-48 hours. Cool completely before opening. Black, light, rings when tapped = good charcoal. Burns hotter and cleaner than wood. Essential for metalwork, water filtration, ink.

ROPE

Harvest plant fiber (inner bark, nettle stems, hemp, flax). Soak and dry. Split a bundle of fibers in half. Twist each half the SAME direction. Wrap the two halves around each other the OPPOSITE direction. The opposing twists lock together. Extend by overlapping new fibers while twisting. Strengthen by twisting two finished cords together (plying).

GLUE

Animal hide scraps, tendons, or hooves. Chop. Soak in water overnight. Heat gently (do not boil) for hours until dissolved into thick liquid. Use hot — sets as it cools. Reheatable. Bonds wood, leather, many surfaces.

PITCH: tree resin + crushed charcoal, heated. Waterproof sealant and adhesive.

GLASS

Sand + plant ash + extreme heat. Mix roughly 3 parts sand to 1 part ash. Heat in a crucible inside a furnace for hours until it melts to glowing orange liquid. Shape while hot: pour into flat molds (windows), blow through a hollow tube (vessels), or draw into rods. Pure white sand = clear glass. Iron in the sand = green glass. Glass gives you lenses, vessels, windows, thermometers.
THE FOLD: 3 parts in 4 = 3/4, exact fraction. Mixing is division of the whole into shares.

SOAP = FAT + LYE + HEAT

Pour water through hardwood ash to produce lye. Mix lye with rendered animal fat. Heat gently. Thickens and hardens into soap.

SALT — evaporate sea water in shallow pans. Essential for preserving food and the human body.


DANGEROUS MATERIALS

LEAD — soft grey metal. Very heavy. POISONOUS. Accumulates in the body. Causes madness, organ failure, death. Do not eat or drink from lead vessels.
MERCURY — silvery liquid metal. Do not touch. Do not breathe near it. Destroys brain and nerves.
ARSENIC — white tasteless powder. Deadly poison.
ASBESTOS — fibrous stone that does not burn. Do NOT crush or breathe the dust. Destroys lungs slowly, irreversibly.
RADIOACTIVE ORES — heavy dark stones that cause unexplained sickness, hair loss, bleeding in those who work near them. If you observe this pattern, LEAVE. Mark the area. Do not dig there.
