SLAB 9 — THE SKY
NAVIGATION, TIME, AND WEATHER


THE SUN

Rises in the EAST. Sets in the WEST. Highest point = MIDDAY.

SHADOW STICK: plant a straight stick upright in open ground. The shortest shadow of the day points toward the sun's highest point.
- Northern half of the world: shortest shadow points NORTH.
- Southern half: shortest shadow points SOUTH.

EAST-WEST: mark the shadow tip in morning. Mark again when the shadow is the same length in afternoon. The line between marks = east-west.


THE MOON

The moon changes shape in a cycle of approximately 29.5 days:
NEW (invisible) to CRESCENT to HALF to FULL to HALF to CRESCENT to NEW.

The moon does not produce its own light. It reflects the sun. The changing shape = different amounts of the sunlit side visible from earth.

TIDES: the sea rises and falls roughly twice per day. The moon's pull causes this. Highest tides occur at new and full moons.


FINDING NORTH — POLE STAR

Look toward the northern sky. Find seven bright stars shaped like a ladle or plow — four form a rectangular cup, three form a curving handle. The pattern is visible year-round but rotates through the sky with the seasons (high in spring evenings, low in autumn).

The two stars at the outer edge of the cup are the pointer stars. Draw a line through them, extending away from the cup's opening, about five times the distance between them. You reach a moderately bright star sitting nearly alone. This is the POLE STAR. It marks NORTH.

VERIFICATION: watch for an hour. All stars visibly shift position. The pole star does not. It stays fixed while the sky wheels around it.

The angle of the pole star above the horizon equals your LATITUDE — your position between the equator (pole star on the horizon = 0 degrees) and the pole (pole star directly overhead = 90 degrees). A fist held at arm's length = approximately 10 degrees.
THE FOLD: latitude is a fraction of the quarter-turn. Every position on earth = an exact share of 90.


FINDING SOUTH — SOUTHERN CROSS

Look toward the southern sky. Find four bright stars in a compact cross or kite shape. It is SMALL — roughly three finger-widths at arm's length. The true Southern Cross is accompanied by two very bright pointer stars to its left. If you see the pointer stars, you have the right cross.

Extend the longer axis of the cross about four and a half times its length. That point near the horizon = approximately SOUTH.


COMPASS

A magnetized needle (iron needle rubbed on a lodestone) floating on water or suspended from a thread points approximately north. Works in fog, clouds, darkness, at sea.

Magnetic north and true north (pole star) differ slightly. Compare them at your location and note the offset.


SEASONS

The shadow length at midday changes through the year.
LONGEST shadow = WINTER. The sun is low. Days are short. Cold.
SHORTEST shadow = SUMMER. The sun is high. Days are long. Warm.

The two days when shadow length is midway between extremes = EQUINOXES. Day and night are equal. These mark the transitions between warm and cold seasons.
THE FOLD: equinox = the 1/2 point. Day and night split the cycle into equal shares.

YEAR: count full moon cycles between two identical shadow-length days = approximately 12.4 moon cycles = one year.
THE FOLD: 12 moons in a sun-year is a rational approximation problem. 354/365 is close but never exact — the remainder demands intercalation.


WEATHER PREDICTION

CLOUDS:
- HIGH THIN WISPY clouds = fair weather likely for 1-2 days.
- DARK TOWERING clouds (growing upward rapidly) = storm approaching. Lightning, heavy rain, strong wind. Seek shelter.
- LOW GREY uniform cover = rain likely. Extended but usually not severe.

WIND:
- STEADY wind from one direction = stable conditions.
- WIND SHIFTING direction = weather change coming.
- SUDDEN CALM after wind = storm may be imminent.

PRESSURE:
- FALLING pressure = storm approaching. Observable: smoke from a fire drifts downward and lingers instead of rising cleanly. Clouds lower.
- RISING pressure = clearing weather.

ANIMALS: birds flying low and sheltering, insects swarming close to the ground, cattle lying down = low pressure = rain likely. These are observable patterns, not certainties.


ECLIPSES

SOLAR ECLIPSE: the moon passes between the sun and the earth, casting a shadow. The sun appears to darken or disappear briefly. This is not supernatural. It is the moon's shadow. It is predictable.

LUNAR ECLIPSE: the earth passes between the sun and the moon. The earth's shadow falls on the moon. The moon turns dark or red. Also predictable. Also not supernatural.

Both demonstrate the relative positions and motions of three objects — the sun, the earth, and the moon. They are geometry, not omens.
