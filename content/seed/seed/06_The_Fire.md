# 6. The Fire

> **The fold at work.** Energy is action — every machine in this section trades force for distance at an exact ratio, and that ratio is always a fraction. Gear ratios are exact fractions: a 12-tooth gear driving a 36-tooth gear gives 3/1, and the product of force × distance is conserved through the trade. Pulleys give you dyadic rationals — 1, 1/2, 1/4 — each added pulley applies one fold, halving the force and doubling the rope. A transformer's turn ratio is a rational fraction: 100 turns to 10 turns is 10/1, no free parameters, no approximation. And the generator and motor are the same machine pointed in opposite directions — spin the coil to get current, send current to spin the coil. The mathematics behind all of these ratios lives in Section 4 (The Count).

Fire was the first technology. Everything that follows — metal, glass, pottery, engines, electricity — traces back to controlling heat.

This section covers every form of energy a human can directly harness, from burning wood to generating electrical current. Each step builds on the last.

---

## Part 1: Fire — The First Energy

Fire requires three things. Remove any one and the fire dies.

1. **Fuel**: something that burns. Wood, grass, charcoal, coal, oil, fat, dried dung.
2. **Air**: fire consumes something in air. Without air, fire smothers.
3. **Heat**: enough starting heat to ignite the fuel. A spark, friction, focused sunlight.

This is the fire triangle. It is directly observable. Smother a fire with dirt (removing air) — it dies. Remove the fuel — it dies. Cool it below ignition temperature — it dies.

### Fuel Types, Ranked

Not all fuels are equal. Ranked by heat output and burn duration:

| Fuel | Heat | Duration | Notes |
|------|------|----------|-------|
| Dry grass, paper, bark | Low | Very short | Good for starting fires, useless for sustained heat |
| Wood | Medium | Medium | The standard fuel. Hardwood burns longer than softwood |
| Charcoal | High | Long | Burns much hotter than wood, with less smoke. Essential for metalwork |
| Coal | Very high | Very long | Found underground in seams. Dense, black, heavy. The most energy-dense common fuel |
| Oil/Fat | High | Medium | Animal fat, plant oil, petroleum. Burns hot. Can be used in lamps |

### Controlling Fire

**Airflow controls temperature.** More air = hotter fire. Restricted air = slower, cooler burn.

- Open fire in wind: burns hot and fast.
- Fire in a pit with limited opening: burns slow and steady.
- Fire with forced air (bellows): burns extremely hot. This is how you reach metal-melting temperatures.

**The Bellows**: two flat boards with a leather bag between them. Squeeze the boards together — air rushes out through a nozzle pointed at the fire. Release — air fills the bag through a one-way flap. Squeeze again. This simple device is the difference between a cooking fire and a forge.

### The Kiln

An enclosed chamber that traps heat. Load your material inside (clay pots, ore, wood for charcoal), seal it mostly closed, and maintain a fire. The enclosure raises the temperature far above what an open fire reaches.

- **For pottery**: sustained medium-high heat for hours. Clay transforms permanently into hard, waterproof ceramic.
- **For charcoal**: sealed with minimal air. Wood converts to carbon without fully burning.
- **For smelting ore**: maximum heat with forced air. Metal separates from rock.

Build a kiln from clay bricks or stones mortared together. Line the inside with clay. Include an air inlet at the bottom and a vent at the top.

### The Forge

A forge is a contained fire with forced air, specifically for heating metal.

Components:
1. A fire pit or raised bed of heat-resistant material (dense stone, thick clay brick)
2. A bellows or air tube delivering air to the base of the fire
3. Charcoal or coal as fuel
4. An anvil (a large, flat, hard piece of metal or dense stone) nearby for shaping
5. A water or oil trough for quenching (rapid cooling)

With a forge and bellows, you can reach temperatures sufficient to soften iron and melt copper. This is the foundation of all metalwork.

## Part 2: Water Power — Gravity Does the Work

Moving water has force. Stand in a stream and you feel it push against your legs. That force can be captured and converted to useful work.

### The Water Wheel

A wheel with paddles or buckets mounted around its rim, fixed to an axle. The axle sits in bearings (smooth supports that allow it to spin). Place the wheel in a stream or under a controlled flow of water.

Water pushes the paddles. The wheel turns. The axle turns. That rotation is mechanical power — it can grind grain, saw wood, hammer metal, or spin anything connected to it.

**Two types:**

**Undershot wheel**: the water flows beneath the wheel, pushing the bottom paddles. Simple to build. Works in flat rivers. Less efficient — you capture only the push of the current.

**Overshot wheel**: water is delivered to the TOP of the wheel via a channel (called a flume or millrace). Water fills buckets at the top. The weight of the water pulls the wheel around. Far more efficient — you capture the weight of the water falling, not just the flow.

An overshot wheel requires the water source to be higher than the top of the wheel. Build a dam or divert water from upstream to a raised channel.

### The Mill

Connect a water wheel's axle to a grinding stone via gears. Water turns the wheel. The wheel turns the gears. The gears turn the stone. Grain is poured between two stones — one fixed, one rotating. The grain is ground to flour.

One water mill does the work of dozens of people grinding by hand. This is the first step toward a civilization that has time for anything other than food production.

### The Dam

Block a stream with stone, earth, and timber. Water accumulates behind the dam, rising to a higher level. Control the outflow with a gate.

A dam stores energy in the form of height. The higher the water behind the dam, the more force it delivers when released. This stored energy can be released steadily through a controlled channel to a water wheel, providing reliable power even when the stream varies.

## Part 3: Wind Power

Moving air has force, just as moving water does. The same principle applies: capture the force with a surface, convert it to rotation.

### The Windmill

Blades (sails) mounted on a horizontal axle, positioned to catch the wind. The wind pushes the blades; the axle rotates; the rotation drives a millstone, a pump, or any other mechanism.

Key difference from water power: wind is unreliable. It shifts direction and strength. A windmill may work well one day and be useless the next.

Windmills are supplementary power — valuable when the wind blows, but you cannot depend on them alone.

### The Sail

A large surface of cloth mounted on a mast. Wind pushes the sail and moves a boat through water. By angling the sail, you can travel in directions other than directly downwind. This is the foundation of long-distance water travel.

## Part 4: Steam — Trapped Water Wants to Expand

This is the single most important energy technology between fire and electricity.

### The Observation

Boil water in a sealed vessel. Watch what happens. Pressure builds. The lid pushes upward. Steam escapes with force from any gap.

Water, when heated to boiling, expands into steam. Steam takes up roughly 1,700 times more space than the liquid water it came from. In a sealed container, this expansion creates enormous pressure.

If you give the steam only one exit, it rushes through that exit with force. That force can do work.

### The Piston

A cylinder — a tube sealed at one end — with a tight-fitting plug inside that can slide back and forth. This plug is the piston.

1. Inject steam into the cylinder through a valve.
2. Steam pressure pushes the piston.
3. The piston pushes a connecting rod.
4. The rod pushes whatever is attached to it — a wheel, a hammer, a pump.
5. Open a second valve to release the spent steam.
6. The piston returns (by gravity, a spring, or steam pressure from the other side).
7. Repeat.

This is the steam engine. It converts heat from any burning fuel into mechanical motion.

### The Boiler

A sealed metal vessel over a fire. Water inside is heated to boiling. Steam collects in the space above the water and is piped to the piston.

**DANGER**: a sealed boiler with no pressure relief will explode. The explosion is violent enough to kill everyone nearby and destroy structures.

**Always include a pressure relief valve** — a fitting that opens automatically when pressure exceeds a safe level, venting steam before the boiler fails. This is not optional. It is the difference between a machine and a bomb.

### What Steam Engines Can Do

A steam engine can power anything that currently relies on muscle, water wheels, or wind:
- Pumping water out of mines or wells
- Driving mills and factories
- Moving vehicles on rails or roads
- Powering boats against the current or wind
- Hammering metal
- Sawing timber

The fuel can be anything that burns: wood, coal, charcoal, dried plant matter, oil.

## Part 5: Magnetism — An Invisible Force You Can Observe

### Lodestone

Some naturally occurring iron-rich rocks attract other pieces of iron. These rocks are lodestones.

Hold a lodestone near iron filings or small iron nails. They leap toward the stone and cling to it. Release them and they fall. The force is real, invisible, and requires no contact.

### The Compass

Hang a lodestone from a thread so it can turn freely. It rotates and settles pointing roughly north-south. Every time. Move it to a different location. It points north-south again.

You can make a compass without a lodestone: stroke an iron needle along a lodestone, always in the same direction, many times. The needle becomes magnetized. Float it on a leaf on still water, or hang it on a thread. It points north-south.

### Poles

Every magnet has two ends — call them the north-seeking end and the south-seeking end.

- Bring two north-seeking ends together: they push apart.
- Bring two south-seeking ends together: they push apart.
- Bring a north-seeking end toward a south-seeking end: they pull together.

Opposites attract. Likes repel. Test this with two magnetized needles. The behavior is consistent and reliable.

### Magnetism and Iron

You can magnetize iron by stroking it with a magnet. You can demagnetize it by heating it or striking it repeatedly. Magnetism in iron is temporary and controllable.

This matters enormously for Part 6 of this section — electricity.

## Part 6: Electricity — Movement and Magnetism Are Linked

This is the most consequential observation in this section. Everything in modern power generation, communication, and lighting comes from this single relationship.

### The Core Observation

**If you move a copper wire through a magnetic field, a force appears in the wire that pushes on charges inside it. This force is called current.**

**If you send current through a wire, the wire generates a magnetic field around it.**

Movement creates current. Current creates magnetism. They are linked.

### How to Verify

**Experiment 1 — Current creates magnetism:**
1. Obtain a compass (magnetized needle on a pivot).
2. Run a straight copper wire directly over the compass, parallel to the needle.
3. Connect the wire to a battery (see below).
4. The compass needle deflects — it swings away from north.
5. Disconnect the battery. The needle returns to north.

Current in the wire created a magnetic field that overpowered the earth's natural magnetism at short range.

**Experiment 2 — The electromagnet:**
1. Wrap copper wire tightly around an iron nail, many turns.
2. Connect both ends of the wire to a battery.
3. The nail becomes a magnet — it attracts iron filings and small nails.
4. Disconnect the battery. The magnetism stops. The filings fall.

You have made magnetism that you can turn on and off. This is an electromagnet.

### The Battery — Making Current Without Movement

**The Voltaic Pile**: stack alternating discs of two different metals — copper and zinc work well. Between each pair of metal discs, place a piece of cloth soaked in salt water or vinegar.

Stack: copper, wet cloth, zinc, copper, wet cloth, zinc... continue for 10-20 layers.

Connect a wire from the top copper disc to the bottom zinc disc. Current flows through the wire. You can verify this by placing a compass near the wire — it deflects.

This is a battery. It converts a chemical reaction between the metals and the acid into electrical current.

- More layers = more force pushing the current (voltage).
- Larger discs = more current available.
- The battery will eventually stop working as the zinc is consumed. Replace the zinc and the wet cloth to restart it.

### The Generator — Making Current with Rotation

This is where everything connects.

1. Make a coil of copper wire — many turns wound around a form.
2. Place the coil between two magnets, with north and south facing each other.
3. Spin the coil. As the wire moves through the magnetic field, current flows in the wire.

This is a generator. It converts rotation (mechanical energy) into current (electrical energy).

Where does the rotation come from? Anything from the earlier parts of this section:
- A water wheel turns the generator. You have hydroelectric power.
- A windmill turns the generator. You have wind power.
- A steam engine turns the generator. You have thermal power.
- A hand crank turns the generator. You have manual power.

**This is how all electrical generation works.** Every power source ultimately spins a coil of wire inside a magnetic field.

### What Current Does

Once you have current flowing through wire, you can do the following:

**Heat**: force current through a thin wire. The wire resists the flow and heats up. Thin enough wire glows — this is how you make light. This is also how you make an electric heater.

**Magnetism**: wrap current-carrying wire around iron to make controllable magnets. This enables motors, speakers, and communication devices (Section 11).

**Chemical reactions**: pass current through certain liquids and it separates them into components. Current through salt water produces a harsh gas and a metal residue. Current through aluminum-bearing molten salts separates aluminum metal. This is how you extract aluminum (Section 5).

**The Motor**: a generator in reverse. Send current through a coil between magnets, and the coil spins. Current in, rotation out. This is an electric motor. It converts electricity back into mechanical motion.

### The Circuit

Current must flow in a complete loop — from one terminal of the battery (or generator), through the wire, through whatever device you are powering, and back to the other terminal. Break the loop anywhere and current stops everywhere.

This is a circuit. Every switch, every on/off control, works by opening or closing a gap in the circuit.

### Wire

Current flows through metals. Copper is the best common conductor — current flows through it with the least resistance. Iron and steel conduct but with more resistance (more heat loss). Gold and silver conduct excellently but are rare.

To transmit power over distance, use wire — drawn (stretched through progressively smaller holes) copper or iron. The thicker the wire, the less energy lost as heat.

## Part 7: Light and Heat from the Sun

The sun delivers energy to everything it touches. This is the most directly observable energy source that exists.

### Direct Heating

Stand in sunlight: you warm up. Stand in shade: you cool. The sun delivers heat.

- **Dark surfaces** absorb more heat than light surfaces. A black pot in sunlight gets hotter than a white one.
- **Enclosed spaces**: a container sealed with glass lets sunlight in but traps heat inside. The air and contents get hotter than the outside temperature. This is the principle behind every greenhouse and solar heater.

### Concentrating Sunlight

**Curved mirrors**: a concave (bowl-shaped) polished metal surface reflects sunlight and focuses it to a point. At that point, the temperature is high enough to ignite dry wood, boil water, or melt soft metals.

**Lenses**: a curved piece of glass bends light as it passes through. A convex (outward-curving) glass lens focuses sunlight to a point, just like a curved mirror. You can start a fire with a glass lens and sunlight.

### Solar Water Heating

1. Fill a dark-colored container with water.
2. Place it in direct sunlight.
3. Wait. The water heats.
4. Enclose the container behind glass (place it inside a box with a glass top facing the sun). The water gets significantly hotter because the glass traps heat.

This produces hot water for washing, cooking preparation, or heating a shelter — using no fuel at all.

## Part 8: Power Transmission

You have made power. Now you need to move it to where it is needed.

### Mechanical Transmission

**Gears**: toothed wheels that mesh together. When one turns, the other turns.
- A small gear driving a large gear: the large gear turns slower but with more force (torque).
- A large gear driving a small gear: the small gear turns faster but with less force.
- This lets you trade speed for power or power for speed.

**Belts and pulleys**: a flexible belt wraps around two wheels. When one wheel turns, the belt drives the other. Simpler than gears but can slip.

**Chains**: a linked chain wraps around toothed wheels (sprockets). More reliable than belts. Does not slip.

**The lever**: a rigid bar on a pivot (see Section 10 for full detail). Push down on the long end to lift a heavy load on the short end. Trades distance for force.

**The pulley**: a wheel with a groove for rope (see Section 10). Multiple pulleys in combination reduce the force needed — at the cost of pulling through more rope.

### Electrical Transmission

Current flows through wire over long distances. But the longer the wire, the more energy is lost as heat in the wire itself.

To reduce loss:
- Use thicker wire (lower resistance).
- Increase voltage (the pushing force) and decrease current. High voltage with low current loses less energy over distance. This is why long-distance electrical transmission uses high voltage.

To increase or decrease voltage: use a transformer — two coils of wire wound around a shared iron core. Current in one coil creates changing magnetism in the core, which creates current in the other coil. Different numbers of turns in each coil produce different voltages.

- More turns in the second coil = higher voltage out (step-up).
- Fewer turns in the second coil = lower voltage out (step-down).

Transformers only work with alternating current — current that reverses direction many times per second. A spinning generator naturally produces alternating current. This is convenient.

## Summary

| Energy Source | How It Works | What You Need |
|---------------|-------------|---------------|
| Fire | Fuel + air + heat = combustion | Wood, charcoal, coal, bellows |
| Water | Gravity moves water, water turns wheel | Stream, dam, wheel, axle |
| Wind | Air movement turns blades | Windmill, axle |
| Steam | Boiled water expands, pushes piston | Boiler, fuel, water, piston, pressure valve |
| Magnetism + Motion | Wire moving through magnetic field = current | Copper wire, magnets, rotation source |
| Battery | Chemical reaction between metals produces current | Two different metals, acid/salt solution |
| Sun | Direct heating, concentration with mirrors/lenses | Dark surfaces, glass, curved reflectors |

Everything connects. A water wheel turns a generator. The generator produces current. Current powers a motor. The motor drives a tool. Fire heats a boiler. Steam drives a piston. The piston turns a generator.

Energy transforms. It does not appear from nothing and it does not disappear. It changes form — from heat to motion, from motion to current, from current to light. You can observe this in every transformation listed above.

---

*Fire was the first step out of darkness. Electricity was the second. Both require only materials from the ground and the will to experiment.*
