# 3. The Measure

> *Every measurement you will ever make is a ratio — a fraction of the One. You pick a unit, call it "one," and everything else is some fraction or multiple of it. The fold lives here: exact fractions in (0,1], no more, no less.*

You need to measure things: how long, how heavy, how hot, how far, how much time has passed. 

You have no instruments. You have your body, water, sticks, rope, and the sky.

That is enough.

---

## Part 1: Length — Your Body Is Your First Ruler

Your body does not change size from day to day. Use it.

- **Finger width**: the width of your index finger. The smallest common unit.
- **Hand span**: spread your fingers wide. The distance from thumb tip to little finger tip. Roughly eight finger widths.
- **Forearm (cubit)**: elbow to fingertip. Roughly two hand spans.
- **Arm span**: both arms stretched wide, fingertip to fingertip. Roughly equal to your height.
- **Pace**: one full step, measured from where one heel lifts to where it lands again. 
- **Height**: stand against a wall, mark the top of your head. This is your largest body unit.

### Making a Standard

If ten people all measure with their forearms, you get ten different answers. To build anything together, you need a shared standard.

1. Pick one straight stick, roughly one forearm long.
2. Mark it carefully. This is your standard cubit.
3. Copy it. Make many identical sticks. Distribute them.
4. Everyone measures from the same stick.

The length itself does not matter. The agreement to use the same one does.

### Straight Lines

Stretch a cord tight between two stakes driven into the ground. The cord is a straight line. Every building, every measurement of distance, every right angle starts here.

### Right Angles — The 3-4-5 Method

A right angle is the corner of a square — the angle between a flat floor and a vertical wall.

To make one perfectly:

1. Take a rope. Tie twelve equally spaced knots in it (or mark twelve equal segments).
2. Pin one end to the ground.
3. Stretch three segments along one direction and pin.
4. From that pin, stretch four segments at roughly a right angle and pin.
5. The remaining five segments should stretch exactly back to the starting pin.

If they do, the corner between the three-segment side and the four-segment side is a perfect right angle.

This works because 3² + 4² = 5² (nine plus sixteen equals twenty-five). This relationship is a property of right triangles. It does not change. It never has. You can verify it with rope and stakes right now. Notice: 3, 4, 5 are whole numbers, and their squares lock together exactly. This isn't luck — it's forced by the structure of whole-number squares. The fold's two generators, 2 and 3, are at the root of it.

## Part 2: Weight — Balance and Water

### The Balance Scale

The simplest weighing device:

1. Find a straight, rigid stick (or bar).
2. Find its exact center — the point where it balances level on a finger or a thin edge.
3. Hang a pan (or bag, or basket) from each end, at equal distances from the center.
4. Place the thing you want to weigh in one pan.
5. Add known weights to the other pan until the stick is level.

The object weighs the same as whatever you put in the other pan.

### Making Standard Weights

1. Find a stone of convenient size. Call it "one."
2. Break an identical stone as close to half as you can. Verify by balancing the two halves against each other, then each half against the original. Adjust until each half balances. Each half is "one-half."
3. Continue halving. You now have: one, one-half, one-quarter, one-eighth. These are dyadic rationals — powers of 1/2. Each halving is the fold's doubling in reverse: you started at the One and folded down.
4. Duplicate your standards. Store the originals safely. Use copies for daily work.

### Volume — Water Displacement

How to measure the volume of an irregular object (a stone, a tool, a piece of metal):

1. Fill a vessel with water to the brim.
2. Place the vessel inside a larger empty vessel, or over a channel that catches overflow.
3. Submerge the object completely in the water.
4. Collect the water that overflows.
5. The amount of overflow equals the volume of the object.

This works for any shape. A sphere, a rough rock, a tool — anything that sinks.

### Density — Heavy for Its Size

Some things are heavy for their size. Some are light for their size. This is density.

- A piece of iron feels far heavier than a piece of wood the same size. Iron is dense.
- A piece of pumice (volcanic stone) floats on water. It is less dense than water.
- Gold is extremely dense — a small piece is surprisingly heavy.

Density matters because it helps identify materials. If someone hands you a yellow metal and claims it is gold, weigh it and measure its volume by water displacement. If the weight-to-volume ratio does not match gold, it is not gold. This test has exposed fakes for thousands of years.

## Part 3: Time — The Sky Is Your Clock

### The Shadow Stick (Gnomon)

1. Find or make a straight stick roughly as long as your forearm.
2. Plant it upright in flat, open ground. Pack earth around the base so it stays vertical.
3. Throughout the day, the sun casts a shadow. The shadow rotates around the stick as the sun moves across the sky.
4. The shadow is longest at sunrise and sunset. It is shortest at midday.

**Midday**: when the shadow is shortest. At this moment, the shadow points due north (if you are in the northern half of the world) or due south (if you are in the southern half).

Mark the shadow tip at regular intervals throughout the day. These marks become your hours.

### The Day

One complete cycle of light and dark. Sunrise to sunrise, or midday to midday. This is your base unit of time. All timekeeping rests on periodic phenomena — things that repeat. The fold's cyclic structure is here: a process that returns to where it started, every time.

### The Moon Cycle (Month)

Watch the moon each night.

- It starts invisible (new moon).
- A thin crescent appears and grows.
- After roughly 7 days: half lit (first quarter).
- After roughly 14 days: fully lit (full moon).
- Then it shrinks. After roughly 22 days: half lit again (last quarter).
- After roughly 29.5 days: invisible again (new moon).

This cycle is the month. It never changes by more than a fraction of a day. Twelve moon cycles total roughly 354 days — about 11 short of the solar year. The mismatch is a rational approximation problem: two cycles whose ratio is not a clean fraction, so no whole number of months ever fits a whole number of years exactly.

### The Year

The year is the time between two identical seasonal points — two midwinter days, or two midsummer days.

How to find it:

1. Each day at midday, mark the tip of your shadow stick's shadow.
2. Through summer, the midday shadow gets shorter (sun is higher).
3. Through winter, the midday shadow gets longer (sun is lower).
4. The shortest midday shadow of the year is midsummer (summer solstice).
5. The longest midday shadow of the year is midwinter (winter solstice).
6. Count the days between two midsummer (or two midwinter) markers. This is one year.

It will be approximately 365 days. More precisely, approximately 365 and one-quarter days. The quarter matters — see below.

### The Quarter-Day Problem

If you count exactly 365 days as one year, you will drift by one day every four years. After forty years, you are ten days off. Your planting calendar becomes useless.

Fix: every four years, add one extra day to your count. This keeps your calendar aligned with the actual seasons.

### Water Clock

For measuring shorter intervals when the sun is not visible:

1. Take a vessel with a small hole near the bottom.
2. Fill it with water.
3. Water drips out at a roughly constant rate.
4. Mark the water level inside the vessel at equal intervals.
5. As the water drops past each mark, one interval has passed.

Smaller hole = slower drip = finer time divisions.

### The Pendulum

A weight hanging on a string, free to swing.

Observation: a pendulum of a given string length takes the same amount of time for each swing, regardless of how far you pull it to start. A wide swing takes the same time as a narrow swing.

This is directly testable. Try it now.

A longer string swings slower. A shorter string swings faster.

If you find the string length that produces exactly one swing per heartbeat (or any convenient interval), you have a precise, portable timer.

## Part 4: Temperature — Phase Changes as Anchors

You cannot see temperature. But you can observe what it does to water.

### The Two Fixed Points

**Water freezes**: below a certain temperature, water becomes solid (ice). This temperature is your cold anchor point. It never changes at the same location.

**Water boils**: above a certain temperature, water becomes gas (steam), bubbling violently. This temperature is your hot anchor point.

Everything between these two points can be divided into equal steps. If you divide the range into one hundred equal steps, you have a temperature scale.

### How to Observe

Place a thin tube of colored water (or alcohol — it works at colder temperatures) in a sealed glass vial with a narrow neck. As temperature rises, the liquid expands and rises in the neck. As temperature drops, it contracts and falls. Mark the level at freezing. Mark the level at boiling. Divide between them.

You have built a thermometer.

### Altitude Changes Boiling

On top of a mountain, water boils at a lower temperature than at sea level. The higher you go, the lower the boiling point.

Freezing does not change with altitude.

If you are calibrating, do it at the same altitude each time.

## Part 5: Direction — Finding North

### Shadow Method

1. In the morning, plant a stick upright. Mark the tip of its shadow with a stone.
2. Wait several hours. The shadow will move and change length.
3. In the afternoon, when the shadow is the same length as your morning mark, mark the tip again.
4. Draw a straight line between the two marks. This line runs east-west.
5. Stand on the line facing the stick. North is to your left (in the northern half of the world).

### Star Method — Northern Sky

At night, find a group of seven bright stars that resembles a ladle or a plow. The two stars at the outer edge of the "cup" point toward a moderately bright star that sits nearly motionless while everything else rotates around it.

That motionless star marks north. Everything else in the sky wheels around it through the night.

### Star Method — Southern Sky

Find four bright stars arranged in a cross pattern. Extend an imaginary line along the longer axis of the cross, continuing it about four and a half times the length of that axis toward the horizon.

The point on the horizon where this line ends is approximately south.

### Magnetic Method

A lodestone is a naturally occurring stone that attracts iron. If you hang one freely on a thread, it rotates and settles pointing roughly north-south.

You can magnetize an iron needle by stroking it repeatedly along a lodestone in one direction. Float the needle on a leaf in still water, or hang it on a thread. It will point north-south.

This is a compass. It works on cloudy nights, in fog, underground, at sea. It is one of the most important instruments you can make.

Note: the magnetic north is not exactly the same as the star north. The difference is small in most places but it exists. Use both methods and note the gap in your area.

## Part 6: Distance — Measuring What You Cannot Reach

### Pace Counting

1. Measure your pace length (one full step) using your body ruler or standard stick.
2. Walk and count paces.
3. Multiply: paces × pace length = distance.

This gives you the distance along any path you can walk.

### Triangulation — Measuring Without Touching

You can find the distance to something you cannot reach — across a river, to a far tree, to a mountain.

1. Pick two positions on your side, separated by a known distance (measure it by pacing). Call them A and B.
2. From A, point at the distant object. Note the direction precisely.
3. From B, point at the same object. Note the direction.
4. The two directions and the known distance between A and B form a triangle.
5. The wider the angle between the two pointings, the closer the object. The narrower the angle, the further away.

With the geometry from Section 4 (The Count), you can calculate the actual distance. The 3-4-5 relationship and the properties of triangles make this precise.

### The Shadow Method for Height

To measure the height of a tree, a cliff, or a building without climbing it:

1. Plant a stick of known length upright in the ground near the tall object.
2. Measure the stick's shadow length.
3. Measure the tall object's shadow length.
4. The ratio of the stick to its shadow equals the ratio of the tall object to its shadow.

If your stick is 1 cubit tall and casts a shadow 2 cubits long, and the tree's shadow is 20 cubits long, then the tree is 10 cubits tall.

This works because the sun hits both at the same angle at the same time.

## Part 7: Circles and the Ratio

Take any round object. A bowl, a wheel, a tree trunk, a drawn circle.

1. Wrap a cord snugly around its outside edge (circumference).
2. Straighten the cord and measure it.
3. Measure the distance straight across the widest point of the circle (diameter).
4. Divide the circumference by the diameter.

The answer is always approximately 3.1416.

Try it with a plate. Try it with a barrel. Try it with a circle you drew in the dirt. The number does not change. It is not about the object — it is a property of circles themselves. This number — π — is irrational. It sits outside the fold's domain of exact fractions. You can never write it as one whole number over another. But you don't need to. Approximations like 22/7 or 355/113 are rational and close enough for any rope, any wheel, any building you will ever make.

This ratio matters for:
- Calculating how much rope wraps around a wheel
- Building round structures
- Understanding anything that rotates
- Computing areas of circles (the area equals the radius × the radius × 3.1416)

The radius is half the diameter — the distance from the center of the circle to the edge.

## Part 8: Angles — Measuring Turns

You can measure length, weight, time, and distance. But many tasks require measuring the space between two directions — the angle.

### What an Angle Is

Stand facing north. Turn to face east. You have turned through a quarter of a full circle. That quarter-turn is a right angle — the same corner you built with the 3-4-5 rope method.

Divide a full turn into 360 equal parts. Each part is one degree.
- A right angle = 90 degrees (quarter turn).
- A straight line = 180 degrees (half turn).
- A full circle = 360 degrees.

Why 360? Because it divides evenly by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 15, 18, 20, 24, 30, 36, 40, 45, 60, 72, 90, 120, and 180. No other number near it divides so usefully. This is not arbitrary — it is practical.

### Building a Protractor

A protractor is a half-circle marked with degree divisions. It lets you measure or draw any angle.

1. On a flat piece of wood, bone, or metal, draw a circle using a cord and stake.
2. Draw a straight line through the center — this is your baseline (0 to 180 degrees).
3. Mark the right angle (90 degrees) using the 3-4-5 method from Part 1.
4. You now have 0, 90, and 180. Bisect each section:
   - Bisect 0-90 to get 45.
   - Bisect 90-180 to get 135.
5. Bisect again: 0, 22.5, 45, 67.5, 90, 112.5, 135, 157.5, 180.
6. Continue halving for finer divisions. To get single degrees, divide each smallest section by eye into equal parts.

Notice: each bisection is repeated halving — the fold reversed. You start with the whole angle and fold it down, each step dividing by 2. The precision of your protractor is exactly the number of times you fold.

The more carefully you subdivide, the more precise your protractor. A rough version (marked every 10-15 degrees) is sufficient for most construction. Navigation and star measurement benefit from finer divisions.

### Measuring Angles in the Sky

You need to measure how high a star or the sun is above the horizon. This is the angle between the horizon (flat ground extending to the distance) and the line from your eye to the star.

**Simple method — your hand at arm's length:**
- A closed fist held at arm's length covers approximately 10 degrees of sky.
- A spread hand (thumb tip to little finger tip) covers approximately 20 degrees.
- The width of a single finger covers approximately 2 degrees.

This is rough but useful for quick navigation. The north star (pole star) is at an angle above the horizon that equals your latitude — your position between the equator and the pole (see Section 9).

**Precise method — the cross-staff:**

1. Take a straight rod (the staff) as long as your arm.
2. Attach a shorter crosspiece that slides freely along the staff.
3. Hold one end of the staff to your eye.
4. Slide the crosspiece along the staff until the bottom edge lines up with the horizon and the top edge lines up with the star.
5. Read the angle from marks inscribed along the staff.

Calibrate by measuring known angles (the fist/hand method, or by sighting the pole star and comparing with your protractor).

### Measuring Angles on the Ground

**For construction** (roof pitch, ramp angle, slope):

1. Hang a plumb line (a weight on a string) from a straight stick.
2. Hold the stick against the slope.
3. The angle between the string (which hangs perfectly vertical) and the stick = the angle of the slope from vertical.
4. Subtract from 90 to get the angle from horizontal.

Or: place your protractor at the base of the slope. Align the baseline with the ground. The slope angle reads directly from the protractor edge.

## Summary

You now have tools to measure:

| What | How |
|------|-----|
| Length | Your body, standard sticks, stretched cord |
| Weight | Balance scale and standard stones |
| Volume | Water displacement |
| Time | Shadow stick, moon, water clock, pendulum |
| Temperature | Water phase changes, liquid-in-tube |
| Direction | Shadows, stars, magnetism |
| Distance | Pacing, triangulation, shadow ratios |
| Angles | Protractor, hand-at-arm's-length, cross-staff, plumb line |
| Circles | Cord and the constant ratio 3.1416 |

None of these require any instrument you cannot build from materials in the ground. None of them require anyone else to tell you they are true. Measure it yourself. If the answer is the same every time, it is reliable.

That is the only standard that matters.
