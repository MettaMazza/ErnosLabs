# 10. The Build

> **The fold at work.** Every structure is a balance of forces, and every machine in this section trades force for distance at a ratio — always an exact fraction. Mechanical advantage gives you rational ratios: a 4-pulley block and tackle takes 1/4 the force at 4 times the rope, no remainder, no approximation. An arch redistributes force through the curve — each stone carries a share, and those shares partition the total load exactly, the way fractions partition the One. The mathematics behind these ratios lives in Section 4 (The Count).

Every structure solves the same problem: keeping weight up and weather out.

This section covers the principles and techniques of building — from a shelter that lasts one night to a bridge that lasts a century. Every technique here has been used, tested, and refined by hands and observation.

---

## Part 1: The Six Simple Machines

Every complex mechanism is built from combinations of these six. Each one trades force for distance, or distance for force.

### The Lever

A rigid bar resting on a pivot point (fulcrum).

- Push down on the long end. The short end pushes up with greater force.
- The longer the effort side relative to the load side, the less force you need to move a heavier load.
- A pry bar under a boulder is a lever. A seesaw is a lever. A wheelbarrow is a lever.

**Directly testable**: place a strong stick over a rock (fulcrum). Put a heavy stone on the short end. Push down on the long end. The stone lifts. Move the fulcrum closer to the stone — it lifts with less effort.

### The Inclined Plane (Ramp)

A flat surface at an angle. Instead of lifting a load straight up, you push it along the slope. The longer and shallower the ramp, the less force required — but you push over a greater distance.

Every road over a hill is an inclined plane. Every ramp into a building is an inclined plane.

### The Wedge

Two inclined planes back to back, forming a point. Push the thin end into a material, and the sides push the material apart.

An axe is a wedge. A knife blade is a wedge. A nail is a wedge. A door stop is a wedge.

### The Wheel and Axle

A large wheel fixed to a smaller axle. When the wheel turns, the axle turns with greater force (but less distance). When the axle turns, the wheel turns with greater distance (but less force).

A door knob is a wheel and axle. A crank handle is a wheel and axle. Every cart, wagon, and vehicle uses the wheel to convert rotation into forward movement with reduced friction.

### The Pulley

A wheel with a groove for rope. The rope wraps around the wheel.

- **Single fixed pulley**: changes the direction of force. Pull down on the rope; the load goes up. No reduction in effort, but you can use your body weight to lift.
- **Movable pulley**: the pulley itself moves with the load. Halves the effort needed but you must pull twice the distance.
- **Multiple pulleys (block and tackle)**: combine fixed and movable pulleys. Each additional movable pulley halves the effort again but doubles the rope distance. Four pulleys = one-quarter the force, but four times the rope.

### The Screw

An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. Turning the screw converts rotation into forward thrust.

A wood screw pulls two pieces of wood together. A press (wine press, olive press) uses a large screw to apply enormous force slowly. A jack lifts heavy loads by turning a screw.

## Part 2: Foundations — Where Everything Starts

Every structure begins with the ground it sits on.

### Principles

- Build on solid ground. Rock is ideal. Compacted earth is acceptable. Soft, wet, or sandy ground will shift and the structure will crack or collapse.
- If solid ground is deep, dig down to it and build the foundation from there.
- Spread the load. A wide foundation distributes weight over more ground. A narrow foundation concentrates weight and sinks.
- Keep the foundation dry. Water weakens earth. Dig drainage channels around foundations to carry water away.

### Stone Foundation

Dig a trench wider than your wall. Fill with large, flat stones laid in courses (layers) and mortared together (see Section 5 — mortar from burned calcium stone). Build up to ground level or above. Then build your wall on top.

### Post Foundation

For lighter structures: dig deep holes, set vertical posts in them, pack earth tightly around the posts. The posts carry the weight of the structure to the ground. This is faster than a stone foundation but less durable.

## Part 3: Walls — Bearing the Load

Walls do two things: hold up the roof and keep the weather out.

### Stone Walls

Stack stones in courses, mortar between them. Stagger the joints (each stone should bridge the gap between two stones below). Vertical joints aligned between courses are weak points — staggering eliminates them.

- **Dry stone** (no mortar): skilled fitting of stones that lock together by shape and gravity. Remarkably strong if done well. No mortar means no mortar failure.
- **Mortared stone**: stronger bond, fills gaps, blocks wind and water. More durable long-term.

### Brick Walls

Bricks are uniform blocks of fired clay (see Section 5). Their regularity makes building faster and more predictable than stone.

- Make bricks: form wet clay into uniform blocks using a wooden mold. Let dry. Fire in a kiln.
- Lay in courses with mortar. Stagger joints. A wall two bricks thick is far stronger than one brick thick.

### Wood Walls

A frame of vertical posts and horizontal beams. Fill between the frame with whatever is available: mud and straw (wattle and daub), planks, bark, woven branches.

Wood rots. Keep it off the ground (set it on stone foundations). Treat with fire (light charring of the surface resists rot) or coat with oil or tar.

### Earth Walls (Adobe / Rammed Earth)

Mix clay-rich soil with water and straw. Form into blocks (adobe) and sun-dry, OR pack wet earth between temporary boards (forms) and let it dry in place (rammed earth).

Strong, cheap, excellent insulation. Weak against prolonged rain — coat exterior with plaster or whitewash.

## Part 4: The Arch — The Strongest Shape

An arch distributes weight outward and downward through its curve. Each stone in an arch pushes against its neighbors, and the whole structure locks together under load.

### How to Build an Arch

1. Build a temporary wooden frame (centering) in the shape of the arch.
2. Lay stones or bricks along the frame, starting from both sides and working toward the top.
3. The final stone at the top (keystone) locks everything in place.
4. Remove the wooden frame. The arch stands on its own.

An arch can span a doorway, a window, a bridge, or a ceiling.

### The Vault

Extend an arch in depth and it becomes a vault — an arched ceiling. Vaults can span rooms too wide for flat beams.

### The Dome

Rotate an arch around its center point and it becomes a dome — the strongest roof shape. A dome distributes load in all directions. It can cover very large spaces without internal supports.

## Part 5: Roofs

The roof keeps rain, snow, and sun out.

### Principles

- Slope: a sloped roof sheds water. The steeper the slope, the faster water runs off. Flat roofs accumulate water and leak. In heavy snow regions, steep roofs prevent dangerous weight accumulation.
- Overlap: roofing materials (shingles, tiles, thatch) must overlap so water runs down and off, never under. Each piece covers the gap between the pieces below.
- Waterproofing: even overlapping material can leak. Coat wood with tar or pitch. Use fired clay tiles that do not absorb water. Thatch (thick bundles of dry reeds or straw) sheds water naturally if thick enough and steeply angled.

### Common Roofing Materials

- **Thatch**: bundles of reeds, straw, or grass, tied tightly to a frame. Cheap, excellent insulation, surprisingly durable (20-40 years). Combustible — a serious fire risk.
- **Clay tiles**: fired clay shaped into overlapping curves or flat pieces with flanges. Fireproof, waterproof, long-lasting. Heavy — requires a strong frame.
- **Wood shingles**: thin split or sawn pieces of wood, overlapped like fish scales. Durable hardwood shingles last decades. Combustible.
- **Stone slabs**: thin flat stones. Extremely heavy but nearly permanent. Requires massive frame and walls.

## Part 6: The Forge and the Kiln — Specialized Structures

### The Forge

A permanent forge for metalwork (see Section 6) needs:

1. **Fire bed**: a raised surface of heat-resistant material (dense stone, thick clay brick) that holds the fire.
2. **Bellows**: the device that forces air into the fire. Without bellows, you cannot reach the temperatures needed to smelt iron or melt copper. A wood fire in open air does not get hot enough. Forced air is the difference between a campfire and a forge.
3. **Tuyere**: a pipe (clay or metal) connecting the bellows to the base of the fire.
4. **Hood and chimney**: an overhead structure that captures smoke and draws it upward and out. Without this, the workspace fills with smoke and is unusable.
5. **Anvil**: a heavy block of iron or dense stone with a flat top, mounted on a wooden stump. All shaping is done on the anvil.
6. **Quench trough**: a vessel of water or oil near the anvil for rapid cooling of hot metal.

### Building Bellows

**Simple bag bellows** (earliest form):

1. Take a leather bag or animal skin, sewn watertight.
2. Attach a narrow nozzle (a tube of clay, bone, or metal) to one end.
3. Open the bag wide to fill it with air.
4. Squeeze the bag to force air through the nozzle and into the fire.
5. Repeat. Each squeeze is one blast of air.

Limitation: intermittent airflow. The fire surges and drops.

**Hinged bellows** (more effective):

1. Two flat boards (paddles), each roughly the length of your forearm and a hand-span wide.
2. Hinge them together at one end — a leather strap or a wooden pin joint.
3. Connect the two boards with flexible leather sides, forming an airtight chamber that expands and contracts as the boards open and close.
4. At the hinge end, attach a nozzle — a narrow tube that points into the forge fire.
5. On one board, cut a hole and cover it with a flap of leather, hinged so it opens inward only. This is the intake valve.
6. When you pull the boards apart: the valve opens, air rushes in.
7. When you push the boards together: the valve closes (pressure holds it shut), and air is forced out through the nozzle.

**Double bellows** (continuous airflow):

Build two hinged bellows side by side, feeding into the same nozzle through a Y-junction. Operate them alternately — one inhales while the other exhales. The fire receives continuous airflow with no gaps. This is what enables sustained forge temperatures for iron smelting.

### The Kiln

A kiln is an enclosed oven for sustained, controlled high heat. You need it for: firing pottery, making bricks, burning calcium stone to produce calcium paste for mortar, producing charcoal (in smaller batches than the mound method), and making glass.

A kiln for pottery or brick-making needs:

1. **Fire chamber (firebox)**: the lower section where fuel burns. Must have an opening for loading fuel and a grate or perforated floor above it.
2. **Ware chamber**: the upper section where items are placed for firing. Separated from the fire chamber by the perforated floor — heat rises through the holes, but items stay out of direct flame.
3. **Flue/chimney**: openings at the top that allow heat and smoke to exit. The chimney creates draft — hot air rises and exits, pulling fresh air into the firebox continuously. A taller chimney creates stronger draft.
4. **Walls**: thick, insulating. The kiln must retain heat. Multiple layers of dense clay or brick. Thicker walls mean less heat lost and higher achievable temperatures.
5. **Door**: a sealable opening in the ware chamber for loading and unloading. Seal with clay during firing.

**Operation:**

1. Load the ware chamber with items to be fired. Leave space between items for heat to circulate.
2. Seal the door with wet clay.
3. Build a fire in the firebox. Start small and increase gradually over hours — rapid temperature change cracks pottery.
4. Maintain high heat for 8 to 12 hours for basic pottery. Stoneware requires longer and hotter.
5. Let the kiln cool slowly (12-24 hours). Do not open the door while hot — cold air causes cracking.
6. Unseal and unload.

**Temperature control**: adjust by varying fuel (more fuel = more heat), adjusting the flue opening (open wider = more draft = hotter fire; close partially = less draft = cooler), and adjusting the firebox air intake.

## Part 7: Water Engineering

### The Well

Dig straight down until you reach the water table (where soil is saturated with water). Line the walls with stone, brick, or timber to prevent collapse. The water level in the well matches the local water table.

In dry seasons, the water table drops — your well may need to be deeper.

### The Aqueduct

A channel that carries water from a source (spring, stream, reservoir) to where it is needed, over long distances.

**Key principle**: water flows downhill. An aqueduct must slope continuously downward from source to destination. Even a very slight slope works — water does not need a steep grade, just a consistent one.

Where the ground dips below the required slope, build the channel on top of an elevated structure (an arcade of arches). Where the ground rises, tunnel through.

### The Dam

A wall across a stream or river that holds water back, creating a reservoir.

- **Earth dam**: a broad mound of compacted earth with a clay core to prevent seepage. The safest shape — wide at the base, narrow at the top.
- **Stone dam**: mortared stone wall across the stream. Stronger but more difficult to build.
- **Spillway**: CRITICAL. Every dam must have a controlled overflow path for excess water. Without a spillway, water rises over the dam during heavy rain and destroys it. The spillway directs overflow safely around the dam.

### Drainage

Dig trenches that carry water away from foundations, fields, and living areas to a safe discharge point (a stream, a pond, away from structures). Line with stone or gravel to prevent collapse and maintain flow.

## Part 8: Rope and Cordage

Rope is as fundamental as any building material. Without rope you cannot lift, bind, rig, or anchor.

### Making Rope

1. Gather strong plant fibers — from hemp, flax, sisal, coconut husk, inner bark of certain trees, or any long, strong plant fiber.
2. Dry the fibers.
3. Twist a bundle of fibers tightly in one direction (clockwise). This is a yarn.
4. Take two or three yarns and twist them together in the OPPOSITE direction (counterclockwise). This is a strand.
5. Take two or three strands and twist them together in the original direction. This is rope.

The alternating twist directions lock the fibers against each other. Each level of twist tries to unwind, but the opposite twist of the next level prevents it. The result is strong, flexible, and durable.

### Knots

- **Overhand knot**: the simplest stopper knot. Prevents rope from pulling through a hole.
- **Square knot (reef knot)**: joins two ropes of equal thickness. Right over left, then left over right.
- **Bowline**: creates a fixed loop that does not tighten under load. Essential for rescue, mooring, and lifting.
- **Clove hitch**: attaches rope to a post or pole. Quick to tie and untie.
- **Timber hitch**: grips a log or pipe for dragging. Tightens under load.
- **Figure-eight knot**: a stopper knot that is easier to untie after loading than an overhand knot.

## Part 9: Tools

### The Axe

A wedge (metal or sharp stone) fixed to a handle. The handle provides leverage. The wedge splits material. An axe clears land, processes wood, and can be used as a weapon.

### The Saw

A thin blade with teeth. Each tooth is a tiny chisel that removes a small amount of material. Many teeth in sequence cut through wood, bone, or soft metal.

### The Hammer

A heavy head on a handle. Concentrates force at a point. Drives nails, shapes metal on an anvil, breaks stone.

### The Chisel

A sharp, flat blade struck with a hammer. Removes precise amounts of material. Essential for shaping stone, wood, and metal.

### The Drill

A pointed tool rotated under pressure to make holes. Rotate by hand, by bow (wrap a string around the shaft and pull the bow back and forth), or by gear mechanism.

### The Lathe

A device that spins a workpiece while a cutting tool is held against it. Produces round, symmetrical objects: bowls, spindles, axles, wheels.

Simple lathe: mount the workpiece between two points. Wrap a cord around it, attach one end to a springy pole above and the other to a foot pedal below. Press the pedal — the pole bends, the cord pulls, the workpiece spins. Release — the pole springs back and the workpiece reverses. Hold a sharp tool against the spinning piece to shape it.

## Part 10: Bridges — Crossing the Gap

### Beam Bridge

The simplest bridge: a rigid beam (log, stone slab, plank) placed across a gap, resting on supports at both ends.

Limitations: the span is limited by the strength of the beam. A log can span a stream. It cannot span a river. The longer the beam, the more it sags under weight.

Strengthening: use multiple beams side by side. Add cross-bracing. Use stronger material (stone instead of wood, steel instead of stone).

### Arch Bridge

Build a stone or brick arch across the gap (see Part 4 — The Arch). The arch distributes load downward into the abutments (the supports at each end). An arch bridge can span much wider gaps than a beam bridge.

The Romans built arch bridges that still stand after two thousand years. The shape does the work.

### Suspension Bridge

For wide spans with limited material:

1. Anchor strong ropes (or cables, if you have drawn wire) at both ends of the gap, at high points.
2. Drape the ropes across the gap in a curve (they will form a natural curve under their own weight — a catenary).
3. Hang a walkway below the main ropes using shorter vertical ropes.

The deck hangs from the cables. The cables transmit all weight to the anchor points.

This can span enormous distances with relatively little material, but the deck will sway in wind. Stiffen it with cross-bracing and side ropes.

### Pontoon Bridge

For temporary crossings: float boats or sealed containers side by side across the water. Lash them together. Lay planks across the tops. Walk or drive across.

Not permanent. Not stable in fast-moving water. But buildable in hours with basic materials.

## Part 11: Boats — Moving on Water

### The Raft

The simplest watercraft. Lash logs or bundles of reeds together into a flat platform. Push with a long pole in shallow water. Paddle in deep water.

Rafts are stable but slow and difficult to control. Suitable for calm rivers and lakes.

### The Dugout Canoe

Fell a large, straight-trunked tree. Remove the bark. Hollow out the interior using fire and chisel:

1. Build a small, controlled fire along the top of the log.
2. Scrape out the charred wood with a chisel or adze.
3. Repeat: burn, scrape, burn, scrape.
4. Continue until the hull is thin enough to be light but thick enough to be strong.
5. Shape the exterior to a point at both ends for easy movement through water.

A dugout is far faster and more maneuverable than a raft. It tracks straight, responds to paddle strokes, and can carry significant cargo.

### The Plank Boat

Build a frame (keel, ribs, stem, and stern) from shaped timber. Attach planks to the frame, overlapping them (clinker-built) or butting them edge to edge (carvel-built). Seal the seams with pitch, tar, or caulking (fiber forced into the gaps).

This produces a larger, more seaworthy vessel than a dugout. Combined with a sail (see Section 6), it enables ocean travel.

### The Sail

A mast (vertical pole) is stepped (set) into the boat's keel. A yard (horizontal pole) crosses the mast. A sail (large cloth surface) hangs from the yard.

Wind pushes the sail. The keel prevents the boat from sliding sideways. The boat moves forward.

By angling the sail relative to the wind, a skilled sailor can travel in almost any direction — not just downwind. This is the foundation of all long-distance maritime travel.

## Part 12: Sanitation — Keeping Waste From Killing You

The collapse of sanitation has killed more people throughout history than any war, weapon, or natural disaster. When waste mixes with water supply, epidemics follow. Every time.

### The Latrine

At minimum: a pit, downhill and downwind from living areas and water sources. Cover deposits with earth or ash after each use.

Better: a deep pit with a raised seat (wooden frame with a hole), enclosed in a shelter for weather protection. When the pit fills to within two feet of the surface, fill it the rest of the way with earth, dig a new one, and mark the old location.

**Rules that save lives:**

- Latrines must be at least 30 paces from any well, spring, stream, or food storage.
- Latrines must be DOWNHILL from water sources. Contamination flows downward through soil.
- Wash hands after using the latrine. Every time. With soap if available, with water at minimum.
- Separate latrines from cooking and eating areas completely.

### Waste Water

Water from washing, cooking, and cleaning carries contaminants. Do not dump it near your water supply.

Channel waste water to a soakaway — a pit filled with gravel and covered with earth. The gravel filters the water as it passes through. The earth absorbs and breaks down organic waste.

### Refuse

Food waste attracts vermin (rats, flies) which carry disease.

- Compost vegetable matter (see Section 8).
- Burn or bury animal waste that is not used for compost or fuel.
- Do not allow piles of refuse near living areas.

### The Key Observation

Every epidemic in our record that devastated a settled population was traced to one of two things: contaminated water or accumulated waste. Cities that built sewers survived plagues better than cities that did not. Communities that separated drinking water from waste water had lower death rates than those that did not.

Sanitation is not a luxury. It is the first infrastructure a settlement needs — before walls, before roads, before any other structure. Build the latrine before you build the house.

## Part 13: Knots — Binding, Joining, Securing

Rope is useless without knots. Every bridge, boat, shelter, and lashing in this section depends on knowing how to tie them.

### Stopper Knots — Preventing Rope From Sliding Through

**Overhand knot**: the simplest knot. Make a loop, pass the end through the loop, pull tight. It creates a lump that prevents the rope from sliding through a hole. Hard to untie after loading — use only when permanence is acceptable.

### Joining Knots — Connecting Two Ropes

**Square knot (reef knot)**: for joining two ropes of equal thickness.

1. Cross the left end over the right end and tuck it under.
2. Now cross the right end over the left end and tuck it under.
3. Pull both sides tight.

Memory aid: left over right, then right over left. If you do it wrong (left over right, left over right again), you get a granny knot — it slips under load. The square knot holds.

**Sheet bend**: for joining two ropes of DIFFERENT thickness.

1. Make a loop (bight) in the thicker rope.
2. Pass the thinner rope up through the loop from below.
3. Wrap the thinner rope around behind both legs of the loop.
4. Tuck the thinner rope under itself (under its own standing part).
5. Pull tight.

This knot holds where the square knot fails — when the ropes are different sizes.

### Loop Knots — Making a Loop That Will Not Slip

**Bowline**: the most important knot in this section. It creates a fixed loop that will not tighten under load and can always be untied afterward.

1. Make a small loop in the standing part of the rope (the long side, not the free end).
2. Pass the free end up through this small loop.
3. Wrap the free end behind the standing part.
4. Pass the free end back down through the small loop.
5. Pull tight.

The bowline is the rescue knot, the mooring knot, the structural knot. It holds under enormous load and releases with a push when the load is removed. Learn this one first.

### Hitches — Attaching Rope to Posts, Poles, or Rings

**Clove hitch**: quick attachment to a post or beam.

1. Wrap the rope around the post.
2. Cross over the first wrap and wrap around again.
3. Tuck the free end under the second wrap.

Fast to tie, holds under steady load, can slip under jerking or variable load. Good for temporary fastening.

**Timber hitch**: for dragging or hoisting logs.

1. Pass the rope around the log.
2. Wrap the free end around itself three or more times.
3. Pull. The wraps tighten against the log. The more you pull, the tighter it grips.

Self-tightening under load. Releases instantly when load is removed.

**Round turn and two half-hitches**: the most reliable hitch for sustained or variable loads.

1. Pass the rope around the post twice (the round turn).
2. Tie two half-hitches around the standing part.

Will not slip. Can be untied even after heavy loading.

### Tensioning — Getting Rope Tight

**Trucker's hitch**: creates mechanical advantage for tensioning.

1. Tie one end of the rope to a fixed point.
2. Partway along the rope, make a simple loop (slip knot or figure-eight on a bight).
3. Pass the free end around the far anchor point.
4. Thread the free end back through the loop.
5. Pull. The loop acts as a pulley — you get roughly 2:1 mechanical advantage.
6. Secure with half-hitches.

This knot lets one person tension a rope tighter than they could by pulling alone. Used for securing loads, tent lines, and rigging.

### Lashing — Binding Poles Together

**Square lashing**: joins two poles at right angles (90 degrees).

1. Start with a clove hitch on one pole.
2. Weave the rope alternately over and under the two poles in a square pattern, going around the crossing point.
3. After 3-4 passes each way, wrap the rope between the poles (frapping turns) to tighten the lashing.
4. Finish with a clove hitch.

**Diagonal lashing**: joins two poles that cross at an angle other than 90 degrees. Same principle but the initial wraps go diagonally across the crossing.

**Shear lashing**: joins two poles side by side (parallel). Used for making A-frames and extending pole length.

With rope and lashing, you can build frames, shelters, rafts, ladders, scaffolding, bridges, and towers from poles — no nails, no metal, no joinery.

## Part 14: The Wheel

The wheel appears throughout this vault — water wheels, pulleys, potter's wheels, cart wheels, lathes — but its construction is never explained. Here it is.

### The Solid Disc Wheel (Simplest)

1. Take three flat planks of strong hardwood, wide enough that when placed edge to edge they form a rough square.
2. Lay them flat, edges touching.
3. Cut two cross-battens (shorter planks). Attach them across the three planks using wooden pegs or bolts, holding them together as a single slab.
4. Mark a circle on the slab — tie a cord to a central peg, hold a marking tool at the cord's end, sweep around.
5. Cut along the circle with a saw or adze.
6. Bore a hole through the center for the axle.

This is the oldest wheel design. Heavy but strong. Suitable for carts on rough ground.

### The Spoked Wheel (Lighter, Stronger)

1. **Hub**: a thick disc of hardwood with a hole through the center for the axle. Bore additional holes radiating outward around the center hole — these hold the spokes.
2. **Spokes**: straight, strong rods (usually split and shaped from dense hardwood). One end shaped to fit tightly into the hub holes. The other end shaped to fit into the rim.
3. **Rim (felloe)**: the outer ring. Made from segments of bent wood joined end to end, or steam-bent from a single long strip.
4. Insert spokes into the hub. Insert the outer ends into the rim. The tension between hub and rim holds the wheel rigid.
5. Wrap the rim with an iron band (tire) for durability if metalwork is available. Heat the iron band so it expands, slip it over the rim, and quench with water — it contracts and grips the rim permanently.

A spoked wheel is dramatically lighter than a solid disc wheel while being equally strong. It enables faster travel and larger carts.

### The Axle

A straight, smooth rod running through the wheel's center hole.

**Fixed axle**: the axle is attached to the cart frame and does not turn. The wheels rotate around the axle. Simpler but creates more friction at the contact point.

**Rotating axle**: the axle is attached to the wheels and turns with them. The axle rotates in brackets attached to the frame. Less friction but requires better bearings.

**Bearings**: the contact point where the axle turns. Must be smooth and lubricated. Grease the axle with animal fat, tallow, or oil. Harder bearing surfaces (dense wood, bone, metal sleeves) last longer.

### The Potter's Wheel

A special application: a heavy horizontal disc mounted on a vertical axle.

1. Build a heavy stone or wooden disc (the flywheel) at ground level.
2. Mount it on a vertical axle — the axle passes through a socket in a frame.
3. Kick the flywheel disc to spin it.
4. Place clay on a smaller disc mounted on the same axle, at waist height.
5. The momentum of the heavy flywheel keeps the clay spinning while you shape it with your hands.

The potter's wheel is one of the oldest applications of rotational momentum — a heavy spinning mass resists changes to its motion and provides smooth, sustained rotation.

### The Wheelbarrow

The simplest wheeled vehicle: a single wheel at the front, two handles at the back, a tray in between. One person can move a load that would otherwise require two or three people carrying.

The wheel bears most of the weight. You provide balance and forward motion. This is a lever combined with a wheel — two fundamental machines working together.

*Every building is a conversation with gravity. Gravity pulls down. You push up. The better you understand the forces, the less material you need and the longer it stands.*
