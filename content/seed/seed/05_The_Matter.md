# 5. The Matter

Everything you can touch is made of a small number of base materials combined in different ways.

> *Fold connection: The ratios in this section — mixing proportions, material compositions — are exact fractions. They sit at specific points on the fold's domain. Section 4 (The Count) shows why these positions matter.*

You do not need to understand WHY materials behave as they do. You need to know THAT they do — reliably, every time. Heat copper and tin together, you get bronze. Every time. Heat iron with charcoal long enough, you get steel. Every time.

This section tells you how to find materials, identify them, test them, extract them, and work them.

---

## Part 1: The Fundamental Observation

Dig up a rock. Crush it. Heat it. Dissolve it in water or acid. You can often separate it into simpler parts.

Keep separating. Eventually you reach materials that cannot be broken down further by heat, water, or physical force. These are the base materials — the building blocks. Everything else is a combination.

You can identify base materials by their properties — what they do when you heat them, hit them, scratch them, dissolve them, or hold a lodestone near them. Each base material has a unique set of properties. Once you learn them, you can identify any material you encounter.

---

## Part 2: Identifying Materials — Tests You Can Do

You need no instruments beyond your hands, a fire, water, a knife, a lodestone, and a piece of rough stone.

### Hardness — What Scratches What

The most reliable identification test. Line up your materials from softest to hardest by testing which ones scratch which.

A rough ranking you can verify yourself:

1. **Talc**: so soft you can scratch it with a fingernail. Feels greasy. White or grey.
2. **Gypsum**: a fingernail scratches it with effort. Soft white stone.
3. **Calcium stone**: a copper coin scratches it. Fizzes when you drop vinegar on it. Light-colored, often contains tiny shell shapes.
4. **Copper**: can be scratched with an iron knife.
5. **Iron/Steel**: scratches copper. A steel knife scratches most things.
6. **Quartz**: scratches steel. Hard, glassy, often clear or milky white. Common in river beds.
7. **Diamond**: scratches everything. Nothing scratches it. Extremely rare.

If a material scratches another, it is harder. This ranking never changes.

### Color

What color is the material itself? And — more useful — what color powder does it leave when you scrape it across a rough white stone (the streak test)?

- Iron ore: red-brown streak, even if the rock looks black or silver.
- Copper ore: green or blue-green streak.
- Gold: yellow streak. Gold is one of the few materials where the streak matches the surface color.
- Charcoal: black streak.

The streak is more reliable than the surface color. Surface color can be misleading; streak rarely is.

### Weight-for-Size (Density)

Hold an iron nail in one hand and a wooden peg the same size in the other. The iron is heavier. Iron is denser than wood.

Density helps identify materials. If someone gives you a yellow metal claiming it is gold, check its weight against its volume (using water displacement from Section 3). Gold is extremely dense — far heavier for its size than brass or copper. If it feels too light, it is not gold.

### Heat Response

- Does it melt? At what temperature? Tin melts over a cooking fire. Copper requires a forced-air forge. Iron requires sustained high heat with bellows.
- Does it burn? Wood burns. Stone does not. Coal burns slowly with intense heat.
- Does it change color? Iron glows red, then orange, then yellow, then white as it gets hotter.

### Water Response

- Does it dissolve? Salt dissolves in water. Stone does not.
- Does it float? Wood floats. Iron sinks. Pumice (volcanic stone) floats — it is stone full of trapped air bubbles.

### Magnetism

Hold a lodestone near the material. Does it pull?

- Iron and steel: pulled strongly.
- Most other materials: not pulled at all.

This is the simplest yes/no test for iron content.

### Malleability vs. Brittleness

Hit it with a hammer on a hard surface.

- **Malleable**: it flattens and spreads without cracking. Copper, gold, lead, and hot iron are malleable.
- **Brittle**: it shatters or cracks. Glass, cold cast iron, and many stones are brittle.

Malleability tells you what can be shaped by hammering. Brittleness tells you what must be cast (poured liquid into a mold).

### Conductivity

Heat one end of a material. Does the other end get hot quickly?

- Metals conduct heat quickly. Hold an iron rod in a fire and the far end burns your hand.
- Wood conducts heat poorly. A wooden handle stays cool even when the tool end is in the fire.

Materials that conduct heat usually also conduct electrical current (Section 6). This matters for building circuits and tools.

## Part 3: The Workable Metals

These are the metals you can find, extract, and use. Listed roughly in the order humans historically discovered and used them.

### Gold

- **Where to find it**: in river beds and streams as flakes or small nuggets (alluvial gold). Also in quartz veins in rock, but harder to extract.
- **What it looks like raw**: yellow, shiny, does not tarnish. Looks the same after years in water or air.
- **How to extract**: pan river gravel — swirl water and sediment in a shallow pan; gold settles to the bottom because it is far denser than sand or gravel. Pick it out.
- **Properties**: extremely soft (you can dent it with a fingernail). Extremely dense. Melts at high heat. Does not corrode — ever. Does not react with water, air, or most acids.
- **Good for**: storing value (it lasts forever and is rare), thin wire, ornament. Too soft for tools.

### Copper

- **Where to find it**: green or blue-green stained rock. Sometimes found as raw copper nuggets, but usually in ore (rock containing copper mixed with other materials).
- **What it looks like raw**: the ore is greenish stone. Extracted copper is reddish-orange, shiny when fresh.
- **How to extract**: crush the ore. Build a hot fire with charcoal and forced air (bellows). Place the crushed ore in a clay crucible (a heat-resistant cup) in the fire. The copper melts out and pools at the bottom. Pour it into a mold. This is smelting.
- **Properties**: soft enough to hammer into shape. Conducts heat and electricity very well — the best common conductor. Turns green over time (patina) when exposed to air and moisture, but does not weaken like iron rust.
- **Good for**: tools (when hardened into bronze), cooking vessels, wire for electrical circuits, water pipes.

### Tin

- **Where to find it**: as cassiterite — dark, heavy stones found in stream beds and alluvial deposits.
- **How to extract**: smelt like copper but at lower temperature. Tin melts easily over a charcoal fire.
- **Properties**: very soft. Silvery-white. Low melting point. Does not corrode easily.
- **Good for**: almost nothing by itself. Its value is in combination with copper.

### Bronze (Copper + Tin)

- **How to make it**: melt copper. Add roughly one part tin to nine parts copper. Stir. Pour into a mold. Let it cool. *(That ratio — 1/10 tin — is an exact fraction, a specific rational position in the fold's domain.)*
- **Properties**: much harder than copper alone. Holds a sharp edge. Resists corrosion. Can be cast into complex shapes.
- **Good for**: tools, weapons, bells, bearings, sculptures. The first great alloy. It enabled the first complex civilizations.
- **Key observation**: mixing two soft metals produced a hard one. The combination has properties neither ingredient has alone. This principle — that mixtures can exceed their parts — is fundamental to material science.

### Iron

- **Where to find it**: extremely common. Iron ore is red, brown, or black rock — often heavy, sometimes with a reddish streak. Look for red or orange staining in soil and rock.
- **How to extract**: this requires more heat than copper. Build a furnace — a tall clay chimney filled with layers of charcoal and crushed ore. Force air in from the bottom with bellows. Maintain for hours. The iron separates from the rock and collects as a spongy mass (bloom) at the bottom. Remove it while hot and hammer it repeatedly to squeeze out impurities (slag). This is wrought iron.
- **Properties**: strong. Harder than bronze. Magnetic. Rusts — iron exposed to water and air slowly crumbles into red powder (iron oxide). This is its great weakness.
- **Good for**: tools, structural supports, nails, hinges, weapons, plows. The backbone of construction and agriculture.
- **Weakness**: it rusts. Keep iron dry and oiled, or it will be destroyed.

### Steel (Iron + Carbon)

- **How to make it**: heat iron in direct contact with charcoal (which is mostly carbon) for extended periods. The carbon absorbs into the iron. Then quench it — plunge the hot metal into water or oil. This hardens it dramatically.
- **Properties**: much harder and springier than iron. Holds a sharper edge longer. Still rusts, but more slowly if the carbon content is right.
- **Good for**: blades, springs, tools, structural beams. The most useful metal for any civilization. A steel knife outperforms every other material for cutting.
- **Tempering**: after hardening (quenching), steel is brittle. Reheat it gently (to a straw-yellow or blue color on the surface) and let it cool slowly. This reduces brittleness while keeping hardness. The exact color tells you the hardness level — observe and experiment.

### Lead

- **Where to find it**: galena ore — dark, shiny, heavy cubic crystals. Often found near silver deposits.
- **How to extract**: smelt at low temperature. Lead melts very easily.
- **Properties**: extremely soft. Very heavy. Dull grey. Melts at low heat.
- **DANGER**: lead is poisonous. It accumulates in the body over time. Do NOT eat from lead vessels. Do NOT drink water stored in lead containers. Do NOT let lead dust or fumes enter your mouth or lungs. Previous civilizations poisoned themselves with lead pipes and cooking vessels. Learn from this.
- **Limited uses**: weights for fishing and balance scales (sealed in cloth so you do not touch it), solder (joining metals), waterproofing roofs (handle with protection).

### Silver

- **Where to find it**: in galena ore (with lead), or occasionally as native silver in veins.
- **How to extract**: smelt galena. Separate silver from lead by a process called cupellation — heat the lead-silver mixture in a shallow cup with forced air. The lead oxidizes and is absorbed by the cup material. Silver remains.
- **Properties**: soft, shiny white, tarnishes black in air (but does not corrode structurally). Conducts heat and electricity better than almost any material. Antimicrobial — water stored in silver vessels stays cleaner longer than in other containers.
- **Good for**: water purification, mirrors (polished), electrical contacts, medicine (silver-infused bandages resist infection).

### Aluminum

- **Where it is**: the most common metal in the earth's crust. Found in clay, in bauxite ore, in many common rocks.
- **The problem**: it bonds so tightly to other materials that it is nearly impossible to extract without large amounts of electricity. You cannot smelt it over a fire.
- **If you have electricity** (from Section 6): dissolve aluminum-bearing minerals in a molten salt bath and pass strong electrical current through it. The aluminum separates and collects at the bottom.
- **Properties**: light, strong for its weight, does not rust (it forms a thin protective layer that prevents further corrosion). Excellent conductor.
- **Good for**: lightweight structures, cooking vessels, electrical wire (where copper is unavailable). But only accessible to a civilization with reliable electrical power.

## Part 4: Stone and Earth

### Calcium Stone

The most important building stone you will encounter.

- Light-colored, relatively soft stone. Often contains the shells or skeletons of ancient sea creatures (visible if you look closely).
- **Acid test**: drop vinegar on it. If it fizzes, it is calcium stone.
- **Why it matters**: burn it hot enough (sustained high heat, hotter than a normal wood fire — use a kiln with charcoal or coal) and it transforms into a white powder — burned calcium. This powder is reactive and dangerous to handle with bare skin.
- **Burned calcium + water**: reacts violently, generating intense heat. Produces calcium paste — a thick white slurry.
- **Calcium paste + sand + water**: produces mortar. Mortar hardens like stone over time as it absorbs gas from the air. This is the basis of all masonry — the material that holds bricks and stones together.
- **Calcium paste + sand + gravel + water**: produces concrete. Concrete can be poured into molds and hardens into artificial stone.

Mortar and concrete enabled every large structure ever built. This is the most important chemical transformation in construction.

### Clay

- Fine-grained earth that becomes plastic (shapeable) when wet and rigid when dry.
- **Raw**: dig it from river banks, lake beds, or deposits. It feels smooth and sticky when wet.
- **Working**: add water until pliable. Shape by hand, coil, or wheel (a spinning platform). Let dry partially. Then fire it in a kiln — sustained high heat transforms the clay permanently. It becomes hard, waterproof pottery.
- **Higher temperature firing**: produces stoneware and porcelain — harder, more waterproof, more durable.
- **Uses**: vessels for water, cooking, and storage. Bricks for building. Tiles for roofing. Crucibles for smelting metal.

### Sand

- Tiny fragments of rock, mostly quartz.
- **Glass**: heat sand to extreme temperatures (hotter than metal smelting) and it melts into a clear liquid. Cool it and it becomes glass — hard, transparent, brittle.
- **Adding soda ash** (from burned seaweed or certain desert plants): lowers the melting point, making glass production easier.
- **Uses of glass**: windows, vessels, lenses (for magnification and fire-starting), mirrors (coat one side with thin metal).

### Charcoal

Not a mineral — a processed material. But without it, you cannot smelt iron, make steel, filter water, or produce ink. Charcoal is as fundamental as fire itself.

**What it is**: wood that has been heated to extreme temperature with almost no air. The process drives off water, sap, and volatile compounds, leaving nearly pure carbon. It burns far hotter and far cleaner than raw wood.

**How to make it — the mound method:**

1. Select dry hardwood. Softwoods (pine, fir) produce inferior charcoal — too crumbly, too fast-burning. Oak, beech, maple, and hickory are ideal.
2. Cut the wood into pieces roughly as long as your forearm.
3. Build a mound: stack the pieces vertically around a central stake on level ground, leaning them together to form a dome shape. Pack tightly.
4. Pull out the central stake, leaving a vertical channel in the middle — this is your ignition chimney.
5. Cover the entire mound with a thick layer of damp leaves or grass, then a layer of earth or clay. Pack it tight. Leave the chimney open at the top. Poke three or four small air holes around the base.
6. Drop hot coals or a burning brand down the chimney. The mound ignites from the center.
7. Watch the smoke. At first it is white and thick (water and sap burning off). When it turns thin and bluish, the conversion to charcoal is happening.
8. Seal the chimney. Reduce the air holes. The fire must smolder, not blaze. If flames break through the covering, patch the hole with wet earth immediately.
9. Maintain for 24 to 48 hours depending on the size of the mound. The mound shrinks as the wood converts.
10. When smoke stops entirely, seal all remaining holes. Let the mound cool completely — this takes another full day. Opening a hot mound lets air in and the charcoal ignites and is destroyed.
11. Break open the cooled mound. Good charcoal is black, light, and rings when you tap pieces together. It breaks cleanly. If pieces are brown or still woody inside, the burn was incomplete.

**How to make it — the pit method** (simpler, smaller batches):

1. Dig a pit in the ground, roughly waist-deep and arm-span across.
2. Fill with dry hardwood.
3. Light the wood and let it burn until the flames die down and you have a bed of hot, glowing coals.
4. Cover the pit tightly with a metal sheet, flat stones, or earth. Seal the edges.
5. Wait until cool (12-24 hours).
6. Uncover. Charcoal.

**Uses**: fuel for forges (essential for iron and steel), water filtration (crushed charcoal in a vessel filters impurities from water), ingredient for ink (ground to fine powder), ingredient for black powder, soil amendment (crushed charcoal improves soil drainage and nutrient retention).

### Salt

- Found in: sea water (evaporate in shallow pans in sun), underground rock deposits, dried lake beds, springs.
- **Essential for**: preserving food (packing in salt prevents rot), the human body (you die without salt), curing leather, making soap (combined with animal fat and an alkali).

## Part 5: Dangerous Materials

Some materials will kill you. Learn to recognise them.

### Lead
Soft grey metal. Very heavy. See above. Poisons slowly through skin contact and ingestion. Accumulates — your body cannot remove it. Causes madness, organ failure, death. Previous civilizations used it for pipes and cookware and paid with shortened lives and damaged minds.

### Mercury
A silvery liquid metal at room temperature — the only metal that is liquid when cool. Extremely poisonous. Do not touch. Do not breathe near it. It attacks the brain and nervous system. Found in cinnabar ore (bright red stone).

### Arsenic
A brittle grey material that produces a white, tasteless, odorless powder. Deadly poison. Historically used for murder because it is difficult to detect. If people die without visible cause after eating or drinking, suspect arsenic in the food or water source.

### Asbestos
A fibrous mineral that can be pulled apart into fine threads. Resists fire — it does not burn. BUT: breathing the fine dust destroys lungs slowly, over years. The damage accumulates and is not reversible. If you find fibrous stone that does not burn, do not crush it, do not breathe near it, do not use it in enclosed spaces.

### Radioactive Minerals
Some very heavy ores — particularly certain dark, dense stones — emit invisible energy that sickens and kills. You cannot see it, smell it, or taste it. But the effects are observable:

- People who work near these deposits sicken: nausea, hair loss, bleeding, slow death.
- Animals avoid certain mineral-rich areas.
- The sickness worsens with closer proximity and longer exposure.

If you observe this pattern — unexplained sickness concentrated around a mineral deposit, worsening with proximity — LEAVE. Mark the area with warnings. Do not dig there. Do not carry the stones. Do not use them for building.

## Part 6: Combining Materials — Observable Reactions

Mixing materials changes them. Some changes are reversible (dissolving salt in water — you can get the salt back by evaporating). Some are permanent (burning wood — you cannot un-burn it).

### Iron + Water + Air = Rust
Iron left in wet conditions slowly crumbles into red powder. The iron is consumed. This is permanent. Protect iron by keeping it dry, coating it with oil, or painting it.

### Copper + Tin + Heat = Bronze
Melt both metals together. The result is harder than either. This is an alloy — a deliberate mixture of metals to produce superior properties.

### Iron + Charcoal + Sustained High Heat = Steel
The carbon from charcoal enters the iron. Steel is harder, springier, holds a sharper edge. The transformation requires hours of high heat with the iron in direct contact with charcoal.

### Calcium Stone + High Heat = Burned Calcium
Burning calcium stone drives off gas and leaves a reactive powder. Adding water to burned calcium produces violent heat and a paste that hardens into stone-like mortar.

### Wood + Sealed Low-Oxygen Fire = Charcoal
Wood heated without enough air to fully burn converts to nearly pure carbon. Burns hotter and cleaner. Essential for metalwork.

### Salt Water + Evaporation = Salt
Evaporate sea water in shallow pans. The water leaves. Salt crystals remain. Simple, reliable, essential.

### Sand + Extreme Heat = Glass
Sand melts into a clear liquid at very high temperatures. Cooled, it becomes a transparent solid. Adding ash from burned plants (soda) lowers the melting point.

### Vinegar + Calcium Stone = Fizzing Gas + Dissolved Calcium
The acid in vinegar reacts with the calcium. Gas bubbles form. The stone dissolves. This is your test for calcium stone.

### Yeast + Sugar + Water = Alcohol + Gas
Mix sugar (from fruit, honey, or grain) with water. Add yeast (from the air, from fruit skins, or saved from a previous batch). Seal loosely. Over days, the liquid bubbles as gas escapes and alcohol forms. This is fermentation.

- The gas produced is the same gas that fizzes when vinegar meets calcium stone.
- The alcohol preserves the liquid (wine, beer, mead).
- If you let alcohol continue to ferment with air exposure, it becomes vinegar.
- Bread rises because yeast produces gas inside the dough, creating bubbles.

### Animal Fat + Alkali (Ash Water) + Heat = Soap
Collect ash from a hardwood fire. Pour water through the ash to produce a harsh liquid (lye). Mix lye with rendered animal fat and heat gently. The mixture thickens and hardens into soap. Soap cleans skin, tools, and wounds far better than water alone.

## Part 7: Fiber and Rope

Rope is referenced in nearly every section of this vault — measurement, construction, bridges, boats, pulleys, knots, the bow drill for making fire. This section teaches you how to make it.

### Plant Fibers

Many plants produce strong fibers in their stems, bark, or leaves:

- **Inner bark** of lime (linden), willow, elm, cedar — peel strips of bark, soak in water for days until the soft outer layers rot away, leaving long tough fibers.
- **Nettle stems** — harvest mature nettles (handle with gloves or wrap your hand in cloth — the stinging hairs lose their sting when dried). Dry the stems. Crush and peel them to extract the inner fibers.
- **Hemp and flax** — the classic rope and textile plants. Harvest stems. Soak in water for one to two weeks (retting) — this rots the soft material and loosens the fibers. Beat the retted stems with a wooden mallet. Comb out the long fibers.
- **Palm and yucca leaves** — strip leaves into strands. Dry. The fibers are already accessible.
- **Animal sinew** — tendon from deer, cattle, or other large animals. Dried sinew is extremely strong for its thickness. Good for binding, bowstrings, and fine cordage.

### Making Cord — The Twist

1. Take a bundle of fibers as long as you can manage (forearm length is a good start).
2. Divide the bundle roughly in half.
3. Twist each half tightly in the SAME direction (clockwise, for example).
4. While keeping each half twisted, wrap the two halves around each other in the OPPOSITE direction (counter-clockwise).
5. The opposing twists lock against each other. The cord holds itself together under tension. Pull on it — it tightens rather than unraveling.

This is the fundamental principle of all rope. Every rope ever made uses this opposing-twist structure.

### Making It Longer

To extend beyond the length of your starting fibers:

When you are halfway through twisting a section, lay a new bundle of fibers alongside the thinning ends, overlapping by several finger-widths. Continue twisting. The new fibers are locked in by the twist. The join is nearly as strong as the continuous sections.

### Making It Stronger — Plying

Take two finished cords. Twist them together (same opposing-twist principle — each cord twists one way, the pair wraps the other). This is two-ply rope. Stronger than either cord alone.

Three cords twisted together = three-ply. Ship rigging and heavy load-bearing rope is many plies of many cords.

### Quick Cordage

In an emergency, you can twist usable cord from:
- Long grass (weak but fast)
- Strips of bark (moderate strength)
- Strips of cloth or hide (strong)
- Hair (surprisingly strong when twisted into cord)

## Part 8: Adhesives — Making Things Stick

### Hide Glue

The strongest natural adhesive.

1. Collect animal hide scraps, tendons, sinew, or hooves.
2. Chop into small pieces. Cover with water. Soak overnight.
3. Heat gently (do not boil) for hours until the material dissolves into a thick, sticky liquid.
4. Strain out any solid pieces.
5. Use hot — it sets as it cools. Reheat to make it liquid again.

Hide glue bonds wood to wood, leather to leather, and many other surfaces. It has been used for thousands of years in furniture, instrument-making, and tool construction. It is water-soluble — a joint can be undone by soaking in hot water, which is useful for repairs.

### Tree Resin

Many trees (pine, spruce, fir) produce sticky sap that hardens when cool.

- Collect resin from wounds in the bark.
- Heat gently to make it liquid.
- Mix with crushed charcoal (for strength) to produce pitch — a waterproof sealant and adhesive.
- Use for: sealing boat seams, waterproofing baskets and vessels, attaching stone or bone points to shafts (hafting).

### Birch Bark Tar

Heat birch bark in a sealed container (no air). A dark, sticky tar collects at the bottom. This tar is one of the oldest known adhesives — used for hafting stone tools tens of thousands of years ago. Extremely strong when cool. Waterproof.

## Part 9: Glass — From Sand to Window

Glass was mentioned briefly in Part 4 (Sand). Here is how to actually make it.

### What You Need

- **Sand** — as pure as possible. White or light-colored sand is best (low iron content). Iron in the sand turns glass green.
- **Flux** — something to lower the melting point. Burn seaweed, kelp, or certain desert plants to ash. This ash contains soda or potash, which acts as flux. Without flux, sand requires impossible temperatures.
- **A furnace** — hotter than a metalworking forge. Glass-melting heat must be sustained for hours. A well-drafted kiln with bellows and charcoal fuel can achieve this.

### The Process

1. Mix sand and ash in a ratio of roughly 3 parts sand to 1 part ash. *(3/4 sand — another exact fraction the fold can locate precisely.)*
2. Place in a clay crucible inside the furnace.
3. Maintain extreme heat for several hours. The mixture melts into a glowing orange liquid.
4. The liquid is glass. Work it while hot — it becomes unworkable once cool.

### Shaping

- **Casting**: pour molten glass into a flat stone mold. This produces flat sheets (window glass).
- **Blowing**: dip the end of a long, hollow metal or clay tube into the molten glass. Gather a blob. Blow gently through the tube. The glass inflates into a bubble. Shape by rolling on a flat surface, pinching with tools, and blowing further. This produces bottles, cups, and vessels.
- **Drawing**: pull a strand of molten glass to make rods or fibers.

### Why Glass Matters

Glass gives you:
- **Lenses** — curved glass bends light. Convex lenses magnify (magnifying glass, spectacles, microscope, telescope). Concave lenses reduce. Without glass, Section 11's optics are impossible.
- **Vessels** — glass does not react with acids, alkalis, or most chemicals. Essential for any advanced chemistry.
- **Windows** — light without wind. Warmth without exposure.
- **Thermometers** — a thin glass tube with colored liquid (Section 3).

## Summary

You now know how to:

| Skill | Materials |
|-------|-----------|
| Identify any material | Hardness, streak, density, heat, magnetism tests |
| Extract copper from ore | Furnace, charcoal, bellows, crucible |
| Make bronze | Copper + tin |
| Extract iron from ore | Tall furnace, charcoal, bellows, hours of heat |
| Make steel | Iron + charcoal + quench in water |
| Make mortar and concrete | Burn calcium stone + add water + sand |
| Make glass | Sand + ash + extreme heat + blowing or casting |
| Make charcoal | Wood + sealed slow burn |
| Make soap | Animal fat + ash water + heat |
| Make rope | Plant fiber + opposing twist |
| Make glue | Animal hide + water + gentle heat |
| Make pitch | Tree resin + crushed charcoal |
| Recognize and avoid poisons | Lead, mercury, arsenic, asbestos, radioactive ores |

Every material in the ground is a tool waiting to be understood. Test it. Heat it. Hit it. Dissolve it. Watch what happens. The answer is always in the reaction, never in a claim about the reaction.
