# 11. The Signal

> *Every signal you send travels outward and weakens by exact ratios. The fold is in every one of them: distance doubles, strength quarters, and the mathematics never flinch.*

The ability to send information across distance is what separates a village from a civilization.

This section covers every method of communication that can be built from basic materials — from shouting to electrical transmission.

---

## Part 1: Sound

Sound travels through air. You hear it because vibrations in the air reach your ears.

### Observations

- Sound is slower than light. You see lightning before you hear thunder. You see an axe hit before you hear the chop. The gap tells you the distance — roughly three heartbeats per thousand paces.
- Sound travels faster through solid material than through air. Press your ear to the ground and you can hear distant footsteps or hoofbeats before they are audible through the air.
- Sound travels through water faster than through air.
- Sound gets weaker with distance. Twice as far away, roughly four times weaker (inverse square — see Section 4). Each doubling of distance applies the fold twice: double once, quarter the strength. The ratio is exact.
- Sound reflects off hard surfaces (echo). Stand near a cliff face and shout. You hear your voice returned to you.

### Amplifying Sound

- **Cupped hands**: curving your hands around your mouth directs more sound forward. Simple, always available.
- **Horn or cone**: a funnel shape concentrates sound in one direction. A hollow animal horn or a rolled cone of bark or metal makes a megaphone. The larger the cone, the greater the projection.
- **Listening cone**: the same shape held to the ear amplifies incoming sound. Useful for hearing distant sounds or faint signals.
- **Tube**: sound travels efficiently through a tube (pipe, hollow reed) with less dissipation than through open air. Two people with a tube between them can communicate clearly over distances where normal speech would be inaudible.

### Signaling with Sound

- **Drums**: a stretched skin over a hollow frame, struck to produce a loud, low-frequency sound that travels farther than the human voice. Different rhythms encode different messages. Drum signals can relay information between villages faster than a runner.
- **Bells**: a metal shell struck to ring. Carries over long distances. Different sizes produce different tones — you can distinguish bells.
- **Horns**: a blast from a horn carries far and indicates direction (the listener can tell where it came from). Used for warnings, gatherings, military signals.
- **Whistles**: high-pitched sound carries in conditions where low-pitched sound does not (wind, dense vegetation). A whistle from a small pipe of wood or metal is audible at surprising distances.

## Part 2: Light

Light travels in straight lines. It is much faster than sound — for all practical purposes, instantaneous at visible distances.

### Signaling with Light

- **Fire**: visible at great distances at night. A fire on a hilltop can be seen from many leagues away. A chain of hilltop fires can relay a signal across an entire territory faster than any horse.
- **Smoke**: visible during the day. A fire with damp material produces thick smoke. Covering and uncovering the fire produces puffs of smoke — these puffs can encode messages (one puff = attention, two puffs = danger, three puffs = all clear, or any code you agree upon).
- **Mirror / polished metal**: reflecting sunlight toward a distant observer produces a flash visible at extreme distances. Angling the reflector produces flashes and pauses that can encode messages. This works only in direct sunlight.
- **Lantern with shutter**: a light source covered and uncovered by a hinged panel produces flashes. The same encoding system as mirror signaling, but works at night and without sunlight.

### Encoding Messages with Flashes

Any sequence of short and long flashes can represent letters or words.

A simple system: assign each letter of your alphabet a unique combination of short flashes (dots) and long flashes (dashes). Separate letters with a pause. Separate words with a longer pause. This is a binary system — two symbols, two states, on or off, short or long. Like the fold's two generators (2 and 3), two elements are enough to build everything.

For example:
- A = short, long
- B = long, short, short, short
- S = short, short, short
- O = long, long, long

"SOS" (help) = short short short, pause, long long long, pause, short short short.

Any two people who agree on the code can communicate over any distance where they can see each other's light.

## Part 3: Written Language

Sound and light signals are instantaneous but temporary. Writing persists.

### The Principle

Assign a mark (symbol) to each distinct sound your language uses. Roughly 20-40 symbols cover most languages. Arrange symbols in sequence to represent spoken words.

This is an alphabet. It is a technology — perhaps the most important in this entire vault.

### Materials for Writing

- **Wet clay tablet**: press marks into wet clay with a stylus (a pointed stick or reed). Let the clay dry. It becomes permanent. Fire the tablet for extreme durability.
- **Ink on prepared skin (parchment)**: animal skin scraped thin, stretched, dried. Write on it with ink (soot mixed with water and a binding agent like egg white or tree sap). Lasts centuries if kept dry.
- **Ink on bark**: birch bark and certain other barks accept ink and last for years.
- **Ink on paper**: paper is made from plant fibers (wood pulp, cotton, hemp) beaten to a mush, spread thin on a screen, and dried flat. Lighter than parchment and easier to produce in quantity. Less durable.
- **Charcoal on stone**: immediate and durable. Charcoal from a fire marks stone surfaces clearly.
- **Carved stone**: the most permanent medium. Chisel letters into stone. Lasts thousands of years. Slow to produce but indestructible by weather, fire, or neglect.

### Numbers as Writing

Use the counting system from Section 4. Consistent symbols for 0 through 9, with place value (ones, tens, hundreds). This is a written language specifically for quantities. It transmits precise information that spoken language handles clumsily.

### How to Make Paper

1. Gather plant fiber: inner bark of certain trees (mulberry is ideal), hemp, flax, cotton rags, or wood pulp.
2. Soak the fiber in water for days until it softens.
3. Boil it with ash water (lye) — this breaks down the tough parts and separates the fibers.
4. Beat the softened fiber with a wooden mallet in water until it becomes a thin, even pulp — a slurry with no clumps.
5. Build a frame: a flat wooden rectangle with a fine screen of woven wire or cloth stretched across it (the mold).
6. Dip the mold into the pulp slurry. Lift it out level. A thin layer of fiber settles evenly across the screen. Let water drain through.
7. Flip the wet sheet onto a smooth, flat surface (a polished board or felt cloth). Press gently to remove more water.
8. Stack sheets between felts. Press the stack under heavy weight to squeeze out remaining water.
9. Hang sheets to dry in air, or press them against a heated surface.

The result is paper — thin, flexible, smooth, and ready for writing. The finer the pulp, the smoother the paper. The thicker the sheet, the more durable.

### How to Make Ink

**Basic black ink**: collect soot (the black deposit from a smoky fire — burn resinous wood or oil for thick soot). Mix soot with water. Add a binding agent — egg white, tree sap (gum from acacia or cherry trees), or boiled animal hide (gelatin). The binder makes the soot stick to the paper and resist smudging.

**Iron ink** (more permanent): soak crushed oak galls (the round growths found on oak trees, caused by wasp larvae) in water. Add iron filings or a piece of rusty iron. The liquid turns dark. Add gum binder. This ink darkens over time and bonds permanently with paper and parchment. It has lasted centuries on surviving manuscripts.

**Writing tools**: cut a reed or feather (quill) to a point with a small slit at the tip. The slit holds ink by surface tension and releases it smoothly as you write. Dip in ink, write, dip again.

### Printing — Multiplying the Written Word

Writing by hand is slow. One scribe, one copy. Printing produces identical copies at speed.

**Block printing:**

1. Take a flat block of fine-grained wood (pear, cherry, or boxwood).
2. Draw your text or image on the surface IN REVERSE (mirror image).
3. Carve away everything that is NOT part of the text, leaving the letters raised.
4. Roll or brush ink onto the raised surface.
5. Press paper firmly onto the inked block.
6. Peel the paper off. You have a printed page.

One block can produce hundreds of identical copies. A new block must be carved for each page.

**Movable type:**

1. Carve or cast individual letters — one block per character.
2. Arrange the character blocks in a frame to compose a page.
3. Ink the assembled type. Press paper onto it. Print.
4. After printing, disassemble the frame and rearrange the characters for the next page.

This is dramatically faster than carving a full block for each page. A set of a few hundred character blocks can compose any page in any language.

Movable type is the most important information technology between writing and electricity. A single press operator can produce more copies in a day than a roomful of scribes in a year.

## Part 4: Magnetism and the Telegraph

This section bridges primitive communication and electrical communication.

### The Observation (from Section 6)

Current flowing through a wire creates a magnetic field. A compass near the wire deflects when current flows, and returns to north when current stops.

### The Telegraph

This is the simplest long-distance electrical communication device.

Components:
1. A battery (see Section 6 — Voltaic pile).
2. A long wire from the sender to the receiver.
3. A switch (a gap in the wire that can be opened and closed by pressing a lever) at the sender's end.
4. An electromagnet (wire coiled around an iron core) at the receiver's end.
5. A metal clicker or armature that clicks against the electromagnet when current flows, and releases when current stops.

Operation:
1. Sender presses the switch. Current flows through the wire.
2. At the receiver's end, the electromagnet pulls the armature. Click.
3. Sender releases the switch. Current stops. The armature releases. Click.
4. Short press = dot. Long press = dash.
5. Dots and dashes encode letters (same system as light signaling).

This sends messages at the speed of electrical current — effectively instantaneous over any wire length. A hundred leagues of copper wire and a battery, and two people can have a conversation across a territory.

### Building It

- Wire: drawn copper (see Section 5). Stretch copper through progressively smaller holes to make thin, flexible wire. You need enough to reach from sender to receiver.
- Insulation: wrap the wire in cloth, coat with wax or tar, or run it through clay pipes. The wire must not touch wet ground or metal objects along its length, or current leaks away.
- Battery: copper and zinc discs with salt water cloth (see Section 6). Multiple cells for stronger current over longer distances.
- Switch: a simple lever that presses a metal contact against the wire, completing the circuit. Release and the circuit breaks.
- Electromagnet: wrap wire many times around an iron nail or bolt. More turns = stronger magnet = clearer clicks.

## Part 5: The Speaker and the Microphone

Sound can be converted to electrical current and back again.

### The Microphone (Sound → Current)

1. Attach a thin, flexible membrane (metal foil, animal skin stretched tight) near an electromagnet.
2. Attach a small magnet or magnetic material to the membrane.
3. When sound hits the membrane, it vibrates.
4. The vibrations move the magnet near the coil (or the coil near the magnet).
5. The movement generates a tiny, varying electrical current in the coil that mirrors the vibrations of the sound.

### The Speaker (Current → Sound)

Reverse the process:
1. A coil of wire sits inside a magnetic field (between magnets).
2. Varying electrical current flows through the coil.
3. The varying current creates varying magnetic fields that push and pull the coil.
4. The coil is attached to a cone or membrane.
5. The membrane vibrates, pushing air, producing sound that mirrors the original.

### Connecting Them

Run wire from a microphone to a speaker. Speak into the microphone. The speaker reproduces your voice at the other end.

This is a telephone — voice communication over any distance you can run wire. It requires no encoding or decoding, unlike the telegraph. The signal IS the sound.

## Part 6: Radio — Signaling Without Wire

### The Observation

A changing electrical current in a wire radiates energy outward through space — invisible waves that travel at the speed of light. Another wire (antenna) at a distance can absorb this energy and convert it back into electrical current.

### How to Verify

1. Create a spark (disconnect a battery circuit sharply — the spark jumps the gap).
2. At a distance, hold a loop of wire near a sensitive detector (a coherer — a tube of loose metal filings between two electrodes; when a radio wave arrives, the filings clump together and conduct; tap the tube to reset).
3. Each spark at the sender causes a response at the detector.

The signal traveled through space without any wire connecting them.

### Building a Simple Receiver

**Crystal radio**: the simplest radio receiver, requiring no battery.

1. **Antenna**: a long wire strung high — the longer and higher, the more signal it captures.
2. **Ground**: a wire connected to a metal rod driven into moist earth.
3. **Coil**: wire wound around a tube. The number of turns determines which frequency you receive. A sliding contact along the coil lets you select different stations.
4. **Crystal detector**: a small piece of galena (lead sulfide crystal) touched by a thin wire (cat's whisker). This junction allows current to flow in only one direction, converting the radio wave into a signal your ear can interpret.
5. **Earpiece**: a tiny speaker (a small magnet and coil with a membrane).

Connect them in series: antenna → coil → crystal → earpiece → ground.

This device receives radio signals from distant transmitters using no power source whatsoever. The radio wave itself provides enough energy to drive the earpiece. It is one of the most elegant devices in this entire vault.

### Building a Transmitter

A transmitter is more demanding:
1. A source of oscillating current (a circuit that switches rapidly back and forth).
2. An antenna to radiate the signal.
3. A way to modulate (vary) the signal to carry information — by turning it on and off (telegraph-style), by varying its strength (amplitude), or by varying its frequency.
4. Power: a battery or generator.

## Part 7: Optics — Seeing Farther

### The Lens

A curved piece of glass bends light passing through it.

- **Convex lens** (thicker in the middle): converges light to a focal point. Use it as a magnifying glass — hold it between your eye and a small object. The object appears larger.
- **Concave lens** (thinner in the middle): diverges light. Makes objects appear smaller.

### The Telescope

Combine two lenses in a tube:
1. A large convex lens at the front (objective) gathers light from a distant object.
2. A smaller lens at the back (eyepiece) magnifies the image.

This makes distant objects appear closer. Stars become clearer. Distant ships become identifiable. The landscape becomes surveyable.

### The Microscope

Combine two convex lenses, both short-focal-length, in a tube. Place a small object at one end. Look through the other. The object appears enormously magnified.

Under a microscope, you can observe:
- Tiny creatures in water that are invisible to the naked eye.
- The structure of plant and animal tissue.
- The organisms that cause infection in wounds (confirming the observations in Section 7 about clean versus dirty conditions).

### The Mirror

A polished flat metal surface reflects light. A curved metal surface focuses light (concave) or spreads it (convex).

Flat mirrors: communication (signal flashing), personal use, redirecting light into dark spaces.
Curved mirrors: concentrating sunlight (see Section 6 — solar heating), building reflecting telescopes.

## Part 8: Maps — Storing Space on a Surface

A map is a signal about geography. It records what is where — so that anyone who reads it can navigate territory they have never seen.

### The Principle of Scale

A map represents a large area on a small surface. Everything is shrunk by the same ratio. If one pace on the ground equals one finger-width on the map, then 1,000 paces equals 1,000 finger-widths (roughly 10 arm-spans on the map surface). That ratio — one pace to one finger-width — is a constant rational scaling factor, a single fraction applied uniformly. The mathematics behind it are in Section 4.

Choose a scale that fits your purpose. A map of your settlement and its surrounding fields needs a large scale (high detail, small area). A map of a region or a coastline needs a small scale (less detail, large area).

**Always mark the scale on the map.** Without it, a future reader cannot translate map distances to real distances.

### Orientation

Mark north on every map. Use a consistent symbol (an arrow, a star). Without this, the map is unreadable to anyone who was not present when it was drawn.

### Surveying — How to Measure a Territory

1. **Baseline**: measure a straight line between two visible points using the pacing method from Section 3. This is your starting line. Make it as long and accurate as possible — every other measurement depends on it.
2. **Triangulation**: from each end of the baseline, measure the angle (Section 3, Part 8) to a visible landmark (a hilltop, a tree, a building). Draw those angles on your map from the ends of your scaled baseline. Where the lines cross is the position of the landmark.
3. **Repeat**: from each established point, sight new landmarks. Each new triangle fixes another position.
4. **Fill in detail**: walk the terrain between fixed points. Record rivers, paths, coastline, elevation changes, forest, clearings, structures.

### Symbols

Create consistent symbols for features that repeat:
- Water (river, lake, sea)
- Elevation (hills, mountains — use contour lines or shading)
- Vegetation (forest, grassland, cultivated)
- Structures (settlements, bridges, roads)
- Hazards (cliffs, swamps, rapids)

**Mark your symbols in a legend on the map.** Without a legend, the symbols are noise.

### Why Maps Matter

A map lets one person's exploration benefit everyone who comes after. It is knowledge about space that survives the explorer. It enables trade, navigation, resource planning, and defence. A community with maps of its territory is a community that can plan. One without maps is one that must rediscover its own land every generation.

## Part 9: Information Storage

The ultimate challenge: making information survive time.

### Durability Ranking of Media

| Medium | Lifespan | Weakness |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Speech | Seconds | Gone when the air stops vibrating |
| Ink on paper | Decades to centuries | Fire, water, rot, insects |
| Ink on parchment | Centuries | Fire, water |
| Clay tablets (fired) | Thousands of years | Physical breakage |
| Carved stone | Thousands to tens of thousands of years | Erosion, burial |
| Metal engraving | Thousands of years | Corrosion (depends on metal) |

If you want information to outlast your civilization, carve it in stone or engrave it in non-corroding metal (gold, platinum, stainless steel alloys). Do not rely on any medium that requires electricity, machinery, or environmental control to read.

### What to Preserve

If you must choose — if you can only preserve a limited amount — prioritize:
1. The language decoder (Section 2 — The Rosetta).
2. Mathematics (Section 4 — The Count).
3. The fire and electricity instructions (Section 6).
4. The warning (Section 13).

These four give a future mind the tools to rediscover everything else.
