# 9. The Sky

> *The sky is the largest clock you will ever read. Every cycle up there — day, month, year — is a periodic orbit, a process that returns to where it started. The fold lives in every one of them.*

Everything above you is a map, a clock, and a weather report. You do not need instruments. You need eyes, patience, and a stick.

---

## Part 1: The Sun

The sun rises in roughly the east each morning and sets in roughly the west each evening.

The exact position of sunrise shifts through the year. In the northern half of the world:
- Summer: the sun rises north of east and sets north of west. It climbs high. Days are long.
- Winter: the sun rises south of east and sets south of west. It stays low. Days are short.

In the southern half of the world, reverse all directions.

**Midday**: when the sun is at its highest point. Shadows are shortest. This moment divides the day in half.

**NEVER look directly at the sun.** It permanently damages your eyes. Observe it by watching its shadow, its position relative to the horizon, and the light it casts — never by staring at it.

### Why This Matters

The sun's path tells you:
- **Direction**: at midday, the shadow points north (northern half) or south (southern half).
- **Season**: the sun's height at midday tells you where you are in the year.
- **Time of day**: the shadow's angle and length tell you how many hours of light remain.

## Part 2: The Moon

The moon goes through a cycle of shapes approximately every 29.5 days:

1. **New moon**: invisible. The moon is between the earth and the sun (you see its unlit side).
2. **Waxing crescent**: a thin sliver appears, growing.
3. **First quarter**: half the face is lit (the right half in the northern hemisphere).
4. **Waxing gibbous**: more than half lit, still growing.
5. **Full moon**: the entire face is lit.
6. **Waning gibbous**: more than half lit, shrinking.
7. **Last quarter**: half the face is lit (the left half in the northern hemisphere).
8. **Waning crescent**: a thin sliver, shrinking.
9. **New moon**: invisible again. Cycle restarts.

### Moon Observations

- The moon rises roughly 50 minutes later each day.
- A full moon rises at sunset and sets at sunrise — it is visible all night.
- A new moon rises with the sun and is invisible.
- The moon cycle is the origin of the month.
- The moon does not produce its own light. It reflects sunlight. You can verify this: the lit side always faces the sun's position.

### Tides

If you live near a coast, you will observe that the water level rises and falls roughly twice per day.

The tides are linked to the moon:
- **Spring tides** (highest highs, lowest lows): occur at full moon and new moon.
- **Neap tides** (moderate range): occur at quarter moons.

Track this yourself by marking water levels on a post at the shore and noting the moon phase. The correlation is consistent.

## Part 3: The Stars

### The Rotating Dome

At night, the stars appear to be fixed to a great dome that rotates slowly. Over the course of one night, they wheel across the sky as a group, maintaining their positions relative to each other.

In the northern hemisphere, they appear to rotate around a single point in the northern sky. One star sits very near this point — the pole star — and barely moves while everything else circles around it.

In the southern hemisphere, they rotate around a point in the southern sky. There is no bright star exactly at this point, but the pattern of the Southern Cross points toward it.

### Star Patterns (Constellations)

Groups of stars form recognizable patterns. These patterns do not change — the stars maintain the same relationships to each other year after year, century after century.

Different patterns are visible at different times of year:
- Some are visible only in summer nights.
- Some only in winter nights.
- Some (near the pole) are visible all year.

By learning which patterns appear at which season, you can tell the time of year from the stars alone.

### The Wanderers (Planets)

Five objects look like bright stars but behave differently:
- They move slowly against the fixed star patterns over weeks and months.
- They are brighter than most stars.
- They do not twinkle — their light is steady (because they are much closer than the stars).
- They stay near an imaginary band across the sky (the same band the sun and moon travel).

These are planets — objects in the sky that are much closer than stars and orbit the sun as we do. You can track their movement night by night against the background stars.

### Shooting Stars

Brief bright streaks across the sky. They are not stars. They are small rocks from space burning up as they hit the air at high speed. They are not omens. They are physical events.

At certain times of year, they are more frequent — the earth passes through areas with more of these small rocks. Track the dates and you can predict the displays.

## Part 4: Navigation

### Finding North — Pole Star Method

**How to find the pattern**: look toward the northern sky on any clear night. The seven bright stars form a shape like a ladle (a long handle attached to a rectangular cup) or a plow. Four stars form the cup, three form the curving handle.

**When it is easiest to find**: the pattern is visible year-round in the northern hemisphere, but its position in the sky rotates through the seasons. In spring evenings, it is high overhead. In autumn evenings, it is low near the northern horizon. In summer it is to the northwest; in winter to the northeast. The shape is the same regardless of orientation — it may appear upside down or on its side depending on the time of year and night.

**Confirming you found the right stars**: the cup's four stars are roughly equally bright. The two outer stars of the cup (the ones farthest from the handle) are called the pointer stars — they are slightly farther apart than the two on the handle side.

1. Identify the two pointer stars at the outer edge of the cup.
2. Draw an imaginary line through them, extending upward (away from the cup's opening) about five times the distance between them.
3. You arrive at a moderately bright star that sits nearly alone — no other bright stars are close to it. This is the pole star.

Everything else rotates around it. At any time of night, in any season, the pole star is north.

**Verification**: watch for an hour. All stars visibly shift position. The pole star does not. It stays put while the sky wheels around it. This confirms you have found it.

### Finding South — Southern Cross Method

**How to find the pattern**: look toward the southern sky. The Southern Cross is a compact group of four bright stars in a distinct cross (or kite) shape. It is small — much smaller than most people expect. Roughly the width of three fingers held at arm's length.

**Distinguishing it from false crosses**: there is another cross-shaped pattern nearby (the False Cross) that can mislead. The true Southern Cross has a distinctive very bright star at the foot and another at the top, and it is accompanied by two very bright "pointer stars" — two prominent stars close together to the left of the cross. If you see the pointer stars, you have the right cross.

1. Identify the longer axis of the cross (from the bright foot star to the top star).
2. Extend an imaginary line along this axis, away from the top of the cross, about four and a half times its length.
3. That point in the sky (near or at the horizon) is approximately south.

### East-West — Shadow Method

1. Plant a straight stick upright in open ground.
2. In the morning, mark the shadow tip with a stone.
3. Wait several hours until the afternoon.
4. When the shadow is the same length as your morning mark, mark the tip again.
5. A straight line between the two marks runs east-west.
6. Facing the stick from the line: north is to your left (northern hemisphere) or right (southern hemisphere).

### Navigation by the Moon

A crescent moon's "horns" (the two tips) are roughly aligned along a north-south line. A line drawn through both tips and extended to the horizon points approximately south (in the northern hemisphere).

This is less precise than star navigation but works when only the moon is visible.

### Navigation by Compass

A magnetized needle (see Section 6 — The Fire) points roughly north. This works in clouds, in fog, in the dark, at sea — anywhere you cannot see the sky.

The needle points to magnetic north, which is close to but not exactly the same as true north (the pole star direction). The difference varies by location. In your local area, compare compass north to pole star north and note the offset.

## Part 5: Weather — Reading the Sky

### Clouds

- **High, thin, wispy clouds**: usually fair weather for the next day or two.
- **Puffy white clouds with flat bases**: fair weather if they remain small. If they grow tall and dark, storms are developing.
- **Low, thick, grey blanket**: rain or drizzle likely within hours.
- **Very tall clouds, dark at the base, anvil-shaped at top**: severe storms — strong wind, heavy rain, lightning, hail. Seek shelter.

### Wind

- Wind shifting to come from the west or southwest (in northern temperate regions) often brings rain.
- Wind from the north or east often brings dry, cold air.
- A sudden drop in wind after a period of steady wind can precede a storm.
- Observe which direction weather typically arrives from in your area. Track it over months. The pattern becomes predictable.

### Pressure

Air has weight. Its weight changes before weather changes.

**Simple water barometer**: a long, narrow glass tube sealed at one end, filled with water, and inverted into an open dish of water. The water level in the tube rises and falls as air pressure changes.

- Falling water level = dropping pressure = incoming storm or rain.
- Rising water level = rising pressure = clearing weather.

### Natural Indicators

- **Red sky at evening**: fair weather likely the next day (in regions where weather moves west to east).
- **Red sky at morning**: rain likely later that day.
- **Heavy morning dew**: usually fair weather — clear skies allowed the ground to cool enough for dew to form.
- **No dew**: clouds or wind overnight, suggesting incoming change.
- **Morning fog that burns off by midday**: fair day.
- **Birds flying low**: dropping pressure before a storm. They struggle at altitude when air is thinner.
- **Insects swarming close to ground**: same cause — dropping pressure.
- **Cattle lying down**: often before rain (debated, but observed frequently enough to note).

These are not superstitions. They are correlations that survive because they were correct often enough to be useful.

## Part 6: Timekeeping

### The Day

One complete cycle of light and dark. The most natural time unit.

Subdivide using the shadow stick: mark the shadow position at regular intervals. The marks become your hours. If you make 12 marks from sunrise to sunset and 12 from sunset to sunrise, you have 24 hours — though the day-hours and night-hours will be different lengths except at the equinoxes. At the equinox, day and night are equal — the 1/2 point of the cycle, the fold's most basic division.

### The Week

Seven days. This is an arbitrary human convention. Nothing in nature has a seven-day cycle. But it is a convenient grouping for organizing work and rest.

### The Month

One moon cycle: new moon to new moon, approximately 29.5 days.

Some months are 29 days. Some are 30. Over a year, 12 lunar months total approximately 354 days — about 11 days short of a solar year. This drift is why moon-based calendars and sun-based calendars do not naturally align. This is the rational approximation problem at work: two periods whose ratio isn't a clean fraction, so you can never tile one perfectly with the other.

### The Year

Approximately 365.25 days. The time between two identical solstices (see Section 3).

**The solstice** is the most precise natural year-marker:
- **Summer solstice**: the day the midday shadow is shortest (sun is highest).
- **Winter solstice**: the day the midday shadow is longest (sun is lowest).

Count the days between two summer (or two winter) solstices. That is one year.

### The Quarter-Day

The year is not exactly 365 days. It is approximately 365 and one-quarter days. If you ignore this, your calendar drifts by one day every four years. After 40 years, your planting calendar is 10 days off.

**Fix**: every four years, add one extra day.

More precisely: every 100 years, do NOT add the extra day (correction for over-counting). But every 400 years, DO add it after all. This keeps the calendar aligned to within fractions of a day over centuries. Watch the structure: +1/4, −1/100, +1/400. Each correction refines the previous covering — a closer rational approximation to the true year length, each step a smaller fraction patching the gap left by the last.

### Seasons

Most regions have either two seasons (wet and dry) or four (spring, summer, autumn, winter).

Seasons are caused by the changing angle of sunlight through the year. In summer, sunlight hits your area more directly (more energy per area). In winter, it hits at a lower angle (less energy per area, spread over more ground).

Observable proof: in summer, you feel hotter, days are longer, and the sun is higher. In winter, the reverse. This is not a belief. It is something you feel every year.

## Part 7: Eclipses

### Solar Eclipse

Occasionally the moon passes directly between the earth and the sun, blocking the sun's light. Day becomes dark for minutes.

This is predictable once you track the moon's cycles precisely enough. The moon's path wobbles slightly relative to the sun's apparent path, so eclipses do not happen every new moon — only when the alignments are exact.

An eclipse is a mechanical event. The moon is a solid object casting a shadow on the ground. It is not an omen, not a message, not a sign.

### Lunar Eclipse

Occasionally the earth passes between the sun and the moon. The earth's shadow falls on the moon, turning it dark red for up to an hour.

**Observable proof that the earth is round**: the shadow of the earth on the moon is always curved — always a circular edge, regardless of the angle. A flat object would sometimes cast a straight or elliptical shadow. Only a sphere casts a circular shadow from every direction.

## Part 8: What the Sky Tells You About Where You Are

### Latitude — How Far North or South

The pole star's height above the horizon tells you how far north you are:
- If the pole star is on the horizon: you are at the equator (0 degrees latitude).
- If the pole star is directly overhead: you are at the north pole (90 degrees latitude).
- The angle between the horizon and the pole star equals your latitude. Your latitude is a fraction of the quarter-turn (90°) — your position expressed as an exact ratio between equator and pole.

Measure this angle with a simple tool: a straight stick with a weight on a string (plumb line). Point the stick at the pole star. The angle between the string (vertical) and the stick gives you the latitude.

### Longitude — How Far East or West

This is harder. It requires knowing the exact time at a reference location AND the exact local time simultaneously.

If you know that midday at a reference location occurs at a specific time, and your local midday occurs later, the time difference tells you how far west you are. Each hour of difference equals approximately 15 degrees of longitude.

The difficulty is knowing the time at the distant reference. This requires a very accurate portable clock or a pre-arranged signal. Without either, longitude must be estimated by dead reckoning (tracking distance and direction traveled).

### The Tropical Zone

Between two lines of latitude, the sun passes directly overhead at midday twice per year. This zone stays warm year-round. Outside it, the sun never passes directly overhead.

This boundary occurs at approximately 23.5 degrees north and south of the equator. You can observe it: if the sun is ever directly overhead (a vertical stick casts no shadow at midday), you are within this zone.
