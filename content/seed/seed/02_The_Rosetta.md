## Part 1: Objects — Things You Can Point At

These are the anchors. Every one of them exists in nature. You do not need us to explain them. You need only to recognise them.

### The Sky

- **Sun**: the bright disc that crosses the sky each day. It gives heat and light. You feel it on your skin. Do not look directly at it.
- **Moon**: the pale disc visible most nights. It changes shape over roughly thirty days, from invisible to full circle and back.
- **Star**: the small points of light visible at night. They do not move relative to each other. They rotate together across the sky.

### The Ground

- **Stone**: hard, cold, heavy. It does not burn. It does not rot. It lasts.
- **Earth/Soil**: the dark ground that plants grow from. Crumbles in your hand. Smells alive when wet.
- **Sand**: tiny fragments of stone. Found along water and in dry places.
- **Mountain**: a large rise of ground made of stone. Visible from great distance.

### Water and Air

- **Water**: the clear liquid that falls from the sky, flows in rivers, fills seas. Essential for all life. It takes the shape of whatever holds it. It freezes solid when cold enough. It becomes invisible gas when hot enough.
- **River**: water that moves across the ground in a channel, always flowing downhill.
- **Sea**: a vast body of water with no visible far edge. Its water is salty and undrinkable without processing.
- **Air**: invisible but present. You feel it as wind. You breathe it. Fire needs it to burn. Without it, you die in minutes.

### Fire

- **Fire**: light and heat produced when fuel is consumed. It needs three things: fuel (wood, grass, oil), air, and enough starting heat. Remove any one and the fire dies. Fire is a tool and a danger.

### How to Make Fire

You need fire before anything else in this vault becomes useful. Three methods, simplest first:

**Friction — The Bow Drill**

1. Find a flat, dry piece of soft wood (the fireboard). Carve a small notch near one edge.
2. Find a straight, dry stick as long as your forearm (the spindle). Sharpen one end slightly.
3. Find a curved stick (the bow). Tie a cord tightly between its ends.
4. Find a small stone or hardwood piece with a shallow dent (the handhold). This sits on top of the spindle.
5. Wrap the bow cord once around the spindle.
6. Place the sharp end of the spindle into the fireboard notch. Press the handhold down on the spindle's top.
7. Saw the bow back and forth. The spindle spins. Friction generates heat.
8. A dark powder collects in the notch. When it begins to smoke on its own — when you can remove the spindle and the powder still smokes — you have an ember.
9. Tip the ember onto a bundle of the driest, finest material you can find: shredded bark, dried grass, bird down, fungus. This is your tinder nest.
10. Cup the nest gently. Blow on the ember. Slow, steady breath. The tinder catches. Flame.
11. Feed the flame with small dry twigs first, then larger sticks, then logs.

**Flint and Steel**

Strike a hard, sharp-edged stone (flint, quartz, jasper) against a piece of high-carbon steel. Sparks fly — tiny pieces of steel burning in the air. Catch a spark on charred cloth (cloth that has been partly burned in a sealed container until black). The cloth glows. Transfer to tinder nest. Blow gently. Flame.

**Focused Sunlight**

Hold a clear, curved piece of glass (a lens) or a polished concave reflector between the sun and a piece of dark tinder. A bright point of concentrated light appears. Hold steady. The tinder smokes, then glows. Transfer to nest. Blow. Flame.

**What Makes Good Tinder**: dry shredded bark (cedar, birch), dry grass, dried fungus (bracket fungus from dead trees), charred cloth, seed fluff (cattail, thistle), bird down.

**What Makes Good Kindling**: dry twigs thinner than your finger, split wood, dry pine needles, small dry bark strips.

Fire is the foundation of everything that follows — cooking, warmth, light, metalwork, pottery, water purification. Do not proceed until you can make fire reliably.

### Life

- **Human**: an upright being with two arms, two legs, a head. You are one.
- **Man**: adult human, typically larger frame, produces seed for reproduction.
- **Woman**: adult human, typically smaller frame, carries and births offspring.
- **Child**: a small human, still growing. They cannot survive alone.
- **Tree**: a tall plant with a hard central column (trunk) and branches. Lives for many years. Some produce food.
- **Seed**: a small object produced by a plant. Place it in soil with water and light, and a new plant grows. This is the engine of all food.
- **Root**: the part of a plant that grows downward into soil. It anchors the plant and draws water upward.
- **Leaf**: the flat, thin part of a plant that faces the sun. Plants need leaves to live.
- **Animal (four-legged)**: a living creature that walks on four legs. Many can be eaten. Some can be tamed for work.
- **Bird**: a living creature with two wings, two legs, and feathers. Most can fly.
- **Fish**: a living creature that lives in water and cannot survive in air.

### The Human Body

- **Eye**: the organ of seeing. Two of them, in the front of the head.
- **Hand**: at the end of each arm. Five fingers. The primary tool for gripping, making, and testing.
- **Mouth**: opening in the face. For eating, drinking, breathing, and making sound.
- **Ear**: one on each side of the head. The organ of hearing.

## Part 2: Counting — Numbers From Fingers

You have ten fingers. This is where counting begins.

### The Marks

One thing: |
Two things: ||
Three things: |||
Four things: ||||
Five things: ||||  (draw a line through the group of four to mark five — a closed hand)

This is a tally. One mark per thing counted. Group every five marks together.

### Building Larger Numbers

- Five tallies of five = twenty-five.
- But tallying gets unwieldy. So we use positions.

Think of it as piles of stones.

- **Ones pile**: holds single stones. When you reach ten, move them all out and add one stone to the next pile.
- **Tens pile**: each stone here represents ten. When you reach ten stones in this pile, move them all out and add one stone to the next pile.
- **Hundreds pile**: each stone here represents one hundred.

So: 3 stones in the hundreds pile, 4 in the tens pile, 7 in the ones pile = three hundred and forty-seven things.

This works for any size number. Just keep adding piles to the left.

### Zero — The Empty Pile

If the tens pile is empty, you must mark it. Otherwise you cannot tell apart "thirty-seven" (3-7) from "three hundred and seven" (3-0-7).

Zero is the symbol for an empty pile. It is not "nothing." It is "this pile has nothing in it." It holds the place.

Zero is one of the most important ideas in this entire collection. Do not skip it.

### Operations — What You Can Do With Piles

**Combining (Addition)**
Put two piles together. Count the total. Three stones plus five stones equals eight stones.

**Removing (Subtraction)**
Take stones away from a pile. Eight stones, remove three, five remain.

**Equal Groups (Multiplication)**
Make identical piles. Four piles of three stones each. Count them all: twelve stones.
Multiplication is repeated addition. 4 × 3 means "add three, four times."

**Splitting (Division)**
Take twelve stones and split them into equal piles. If you make three piles, each has four. If you make four piles, each has three.
Sometimes it does not divide evenly. Thirteen stones into three piles gives four in each pile with one left over. The leftover is called the remainder.

> *Notice: halving — 1/2, 1/4, 1/8 — is the fold's doubling run played in reverse. And that remainder from division? That is modular arithmetic — the heartbeat of the fold. Section 4 has the full picture.*

## Part 3: Logic — The Operators

These are the rules for thinking precisely. They are not opinions. They are relationships that hold true regardless of who is observing.

### IS (Identity)
This thing is that thing. Water is water. A stone is a stone.
If two things behave identically in every test, they are the same material.

### IS NOT (Negation)
This thing is not that thing. Water is not stone. Fire is not air.

### AND (Both Together)
Water AND heat produces steam. Both must be present.

### OR (One or the Other)
Fuel can be wood OR charcoal OR dried grass. Any one of them works.

### IF — THEN (Consequence)
IF you remove air from fire, THEN the fire dies.
IF you plant a seed in soil with water and sun, THEN a plant grows.
This is the most powerful operator. It describes cause and consequence. Every practical instruction in this vault uses it.

### MORE THAN / LESS THAN / EQUAL TO
Five stones is MORE THAN three stones.
Two stones is LESS THAN four stones.
Three stones is EQUAL TO three stones.
These comparisons let you make decisions.

### CAUSE (This Made That Happen)
Dropping a stone CAUSES it to fall. The drop is the cause. The fall is the effect.
Every effect has a cause. This is not philosophy. This is directly observable in every interaction you witness.

### CONTAINS (This Is Inside That)
Water CONTAINS salt (when dissolved). The bag CONTAINS stones.
Many materials contain other materials. Separating them is a primary skill.

## Part 4: Actions — What Things Do

These describe change. Every useful instruction involves at least one action.

### Physical Actions
- **Heat**: increase the temperature of something. Put it near fire or in sunlight.
- **Cool**: decrease the temperature. Remove from heat. Submerge in cold water. Wait.
- **Cut**: divide a thing into parts using a sharp edge.
- **Join**: attach two things together. By tying, pressing, melting, or sticking.
- **Pour**: cause a liquid to flow from one container to another.
- **Press**: apply force to compress or flatten.
- **Pull**: apply force toward you.
- **Push**: apply force away from you.
- **Lift**: move something upward against the pull of the ground.
- **Drop**: release something and let the ground pull it down.
- **Dig**: remove earth from below the surface.
- **Fill**: put material into a container until full.
- **Build**: assemble parts into a structure.
- **Break**: destroy the structure of a thing.

### Life Actions
- **Grow**: increase in size over time. Seeds grow into plants. Children grow into adults.
- **Die**: stop living. All living things do this eventually.
- **Eat**: take food into the body through the mouth.
- **Drink**: take liquid into the body through the mouth.
- **Breathe**: draw air into the lungs and push it out. Continuous. If it stops, death follows in minutes.
- **Bleed**: lose blood from a wound. If too much is lost, death follows.
- **Sleep**: a state of unconsciousness the body requires. Without it, the mind breaks down.

### Movement Actions
- **Move**: change position.
- **Stop**: cease moving.
- **Turn**: change direction.
- **Rise**: move upward.
- **Fall**: move downward (usually involuntarily — the ground pulls everything down).

## Part 5: Properties — What Things Are Like

Every material and object has properties. Properties are how you identify, compare, and choose materials.

- **Hot / Cold**: how much heat something holds. Fire is hot. Ice is cold. Everything else is somewhere between.
- **Hard / Soft**: resistance to pressure. Stone is hard. Clay is soft.
- **Heavy / Light**: how strongly the ground pulls it. Lead is heavy. Feather is light.
- **Wet / Dry**: presence or absence of water.
- **Bright / Dark**: amount of light. The sun is bright. A cave is dark.
- **Rough / Smooth**: surface texture. Bark is rough. River stone is smooth.
- **Sharp / Dull**: edge quality. A fresh-cut stone edge is sharp. A worn one is dull.
- **Full / Empty**: how much a container holds relative to its capacity.
- **Big / Small**: relative size.
- **Fast / Slow**: rate of movement or change.
- **Dense / Sparse**: how much material is packed into a given space. Iron is dense. Wood is sparse.

## Part 6: Building Sentences — Combining Everything

Objects, actions, properties, and logic combine to form instructions. This is how every section that follows is written.

### Structure
**Object** + **Action** + **Object** = a description of something happening.

### Examples

1. **Water + Heat + Fire = Steam**
   IF you heat water over fire, THEN it becomes invisible gas (steam). The steam has force. It pushes.

2. **Seed + Earth + Water + Sun = Plant**
   IF you place a seed in earth AND add water AND provide sunlight, THEN a plant grows.

3. **Copper + Tin + Heat = Bronze**
   IF you melt copper AND tin together, THEN the result is a harder material called bronze.

4. **Iron + Water + Air = Rust**
   IF iron is exposed to water AND air, THEN it slowly crumbles into red powder. The iron is destroyed.

5. **Fire − Air = No Fire**
   IF you remove air from fire, THEN the fire dies.

6. **Human − Water (three days) = Death**
   IF a human has no water for three days, THEN the human dies.

7. **Wound + Dirt = Infection**
   IF a wound is contaminated with dirt, THEN invisible creatures multiply and the flesh swells, heats, produces pus, and may kill.

8. **Wound + Clean Water + Heat (boiled tools) = Healing**
   IF a wound is cleaned with boiled water and treated with clean tools, THEN the chance of infection drops dramatically.

9. **Lodestone + Thread + Hang = North**
   IF you hang a lodestone on a thread freely, THEN it points roughly north-south. Always.

10. **Shadow + Stick + Sun = Time and Direction**
    IF you plant a straight stick upright in the ground, THEN its shadow marks time through the day. The shortest shadow marks midday. The direction of the shortest shadow marks south (in the northern half of the world) or north (in the southern half).

## How To Use This Decoder

Every section that follows uses these objects, actions, properties, and logic operators.

If you encounter a word you do not know, return here. Find the nearest object or action. Work outward from what you can touch to what you cannot see.

The physical world does not lie. Drop a stone. It falls. Heat water. It boils. Plant a seed. It grows.

Start with what you can hold. Build from there.
