# 7. The Body

This is a field manual. Someone is bleeding in front of you and this is the only reference you have.

> *Fold connection: The body runs on exact fractions — thresholds of blood loss, compression depths, breathing ratios. These are critical positions on the fold's interval (0,1]. Section 4 (The Count) shows why the fractions are not approximate.*

Everything here was learned by observing living and dead human bodies, by trial and error across generations, and by noting what works and what kills. Nothing here requires trust in an institution. It requires a willingness to look closely at what is in front of you.

---

## Part 1: The Outside — What You Can See and Feel

The human body is bilaterally symmetrical — the left side roughly mirrors the right.

- **Head**: contains the brain (inside the skull), the eyes, ears, nose, and mouth. The command center.
- **Torso**: the central trunk. Contains all major organs. Protected by the rib cage (upper) and the abdominal wall (lower).
- **Arms**: two. Shoulder, upper arm, elbow, forearm, wrist, hand. Five fingers per hand. The hands are the primary tools.
- **Legs**: two. Hip, thigh, knee, lower leg, ankle, foot. Five toes per foot. The legs carry the body's weight.

### Skin

The outermost barrier. It keeps the inside in and the outside out. When skin is breached — by a cut, burn, or puncture — the inside is exposed to the outside. Infection follows if the breach is not cleaned and closed.

Skin also regulates temperature. When hot, it sweats (water evaporates and cools). When cold, it constricts blood flow to the surface (conserving heat in the core). Hair on the skin traps a thin layer of warm air.

### Bones

The rigid frame. You can feel bones under the skin — along the forearm, the shin, the skull, the ribs. There are roughly 206 bones in an adult.

Bones can break. A broken bone hurts intensely, the area swells, and the limb cannot be used normally. Bones heal themselves if the broken ends are held still and aligned. Healing takes many weeks.

### Muscles

Muscles contract (shorten) to pull on bones, creating movement. They work in opposing pairs:
- One muscle bends a joint (the bicep bends the elbow).
- The opposing muscle straightens it (the tricep straightens the elbow).

You can feel muscles contracting under your skin by flexing and relaxing.

### Joints

Where bones meet. Different joints allow different movements:
- **Ball-and-socket** (shoulder, hip): wide range of rotation.
- **Hinge** (elbow, knee): movement in one plane, like a door.
- **Pivot** (neck): rotation around an axis.
- **Fixed** (skull plates): no movement. The bones are fused.

## Part 2: The Inside — What Dissection Reveals

Everything in this section is observable by opening a body — human or large animal. The same organs exist in most mammals, in similar positions.

### The Heart

A fist-sized muscular pump in the chest, slightly left of center. It beats continuously — roughly 60-100 times per minute at rest. You can feel it by placing your hand on your chest, or feel the pulse at the wrist or neck where blood vessels pass close to the surface.

The heart pumps blood through a network of tubes (vessels) to every part of the body. Blood leaves the heart through thick-walled vessels and returns through thinner-walled ones.

If the heart stops, blood stops flowing, and death follows within minutes. The brain dies first — it requires constant blood flow.

### The Lungs

Two spongy organs filling most of the chest cavity, one on each side of the heart. They expand when you breathe in and compress when you breathe out.

The lungs transfer something from the air into the blood — this is directly observable because blood that enters the lungs is dark red and blood that leaves is bright red. The color change is visible during surgery or butchery.

If breathing stops, death follows within minutes. The heart continues beating briefly but without whatever the lungs provide, the body shuts down.

### Blood

A red fluid that circulates through the entire body. It carries what the lungs add from air and what the gut absorbs from food to every tissue.

Blood also carries waste products away from tissues to the kidneys (for removal in urine) and to the lungs (for removal in breath).

**Blood loss kills.** A healthy adult has roughly 5 liters (about 10 cups) of blood. Losing more than about one-third of this is life-threatening. Losing more than half is almost certainly fatal. *(1/3 and 1/2 — these are not rough guidelines, they are critical threshold fractions on the fold's interval. The body crosses them and the rules change.)*

Blood clots — it thickens and seals small wounds naturally. But large wounds overwhelm this process.

### The Gut

A continuous tube from mouth to anus, roughly 9 meters (30 feet) long in an adult when stretched out.

- **Mouth**: chews food, mixes it with saliva.
- **Throat → Stomach**: food passes down to an acid-filled pouch that breaks it down.
- **Small intestine**: a long, narrow, coiled tube where nutrients are absorbed into the blood. The inner surface is covered in tiny folds that increase contact area.
- **Large intestine**: a wider, shorter tube where water is reabsorbed. What remains is waste.
- **Rectum → Anus**: waste is expelled.

If the gut is punctured (by a stab wound, for example), its contents leak into the body cavity. This causes severe infection and death without intervention.

### The Liver

A large, dark red organ below the right side of the rib cage. The largest internal organ.

It processes blood from the gut — breaking down substances, storing energy reserves, and removing poisons. If the liver fails, the skin and eyes turn yellow (the body cannot process a pigment from old blood cells), and death follows.

Observable: animals with damaged livers turn yellow. Humans who drink excessive alcohol over years develop liver failure and turn yellow. This is a direct observation, not a theory.

### The Kidneys

Two bean-shaped organs in the lower back, one on each side of the spine. They filter blood and produce urine.

Urine should be pale yellow and relatively clear. Dark, concentrated urine indicates dehydration. Bloody or foul-smelling urine indicates damage or infection.

If both kidneys fail, waste accumulates in the blood. Death follows within days.

### The Brain

A soft, folded organ filling the skull. It controls everything — movement, sensation, thought, memory, breathing, heartbeat.

Observable proof that the brain is the seat of the self:
- Injury to the left side of the brain often paralyzes the right side of the body (and vice versa).
- Injury to the back of the brain affects vision.
- Injury to specific areas affects speech, personality, memory, or coordination.
- Severe brain injury produces unconsciousness or death even when the body is otherwise unharmed.

Protect the head. Brain damage is rarely reversible.

## Part 3: What Kills

In order of urgency:

### Immediate (Minutes)

1. **Airway obstruction**: if you cannot breathe, you die in 3-5 minutes. Clear the throat. Remove blockages. Tilt the head back and lift the chin to open the airway.
2. **Massive bleeding**: a severed major vessel empties the body in minutes. Apply direct, firm pressure to the wound with the cleanest cloth available. If the wound is on a limb and pressure is not stopping the blood, tie a tight band above the wound (between the wound and the heart). This cuts off blood flow to the limb. The limb may be sacrificed to save the life.
3. **Heart stops**: if the heart stops beating and the person collapses, press hard and fast on the center of the chest — roughly 100 pushes per minute, compressing the chest about one-third of its depth. This manually pumps blood. Continue until the heart restarts or help arrives. Also breathe into their mouth (pinch the nose, seal your mouth over theirs, blow two breaths, then resume chest pushes). *(1/3 depth, 30:2 ratio — exact fractions governing life. Not estimates. The fold holds these positions precisely.)*

### Hours to Days

4. **Shock**: after severe injury or blood loss, the body diverts blood to vital organs. The person becomes pale, cold, sweaty, confused, with a rapid weak pulse. Lay them flat. Elevate the legs. Keep them warm. Do not give food or drink if abdominal injury is suspected.
5. **Dehydration**: no water for 3 days kills in moderate conditions. Less in heat or with exertion.
6. **Hypothermia**: prolonged cold exposure. The body's core temperature drops. Shivering stops (a bad sign — the body has given up generating heat). Confusion, sleepiness, death. Remove wet clothing. Insulate. Apply gentle warmth. Do NOT heat rapidly.
7. **Hyperthermia**: prolonged heat exposure. The body overheats. Confusion, hot dry skin (sweating has stopped), seizures. Cool rapidly — immerse in cool water, apply wet cloths.

### Days to Weeks

8. **Infection**: the invisible killer. See Part 5 below.
9. **Starvation**: the body consumes its own reserves. Fat first, then muscle. Death follows in weeks to months depending on reserves.

## Part 4: How to Fix Common Injuries

### Wounds

1. **Stop the bleeding**: direct pressure with the cleanest available cloth.
2. **Clean the wound**: wash with water that has been boiled and then cooled. Remove any debris — dirt, fabric, splinters.
3. **Close the wound**: if deep, sew it shut with a needle and clean thread. Push the needle through the skin on one side, across the gap, and out through the skin on the other side. Tie each stitch individually. Space them evenly.
4. **Cover**: wrap with clean cloth. Change the covering daily. Watch for signs of infection — redness spreading from the wound, swelling, heat, pus, fever.

### Burns

1. **Cool immediately**: hold under clean running water or submerge in cool water for as long as possible. Minutes, not seconds.
2. **Do NOT apply grease, butter, or oil.** These trap heat and worsen the burn.
3. **Do NOT break blisters.** They protect the raw skin beneath.
4. **Cover loosely** with clean cloth. Keep clean. Burns are extremely prone to infection.
5. **Severe burns** (large area, white or charred skin, no pain in the burned area): life-threatening. The danger is fluid loss and infection. Give plenty of water with a pinch of salt dissolved in it.

### Broken Bones

You know a bone is broken when:
- The limb is deformed (bent where it should not be).
- Severe pain at a specific point.
- Swelling and bruising.
- Inability to use the limb.
- Grating sound or sensation when moved.

**Treatment:**
1. **Align**: if the bone is visibly displaced, pull the limb gently but firmly to straighten it. This is painful. It is necessary. Bones that heal crooked are useless.
2. **Splint**: place rigid supports (straight sticks, boards) along both sides of the break. Pad with cloth between the splint and the skin. Tie firmly but not so tight that blood flow is cut off. Check fingers/toes beyond the splint — they should be warm and have normal color.
3. **Immobilize**: do not allow the limb to move. Movement disrupts healing.
4. **Wait**: bones heal in 6-12 weeks depending on the bone and the person's nutrition.

### Dislocated Joints

The bone has slipped out of its socket. The joint looks wrong — asymmetrical, swollen, immobile.

**Most common**: shoulder. The arm hangs oddly and cannot be raised.

**Relocation**: requires firm, steady pulling in the right direction to guide the bone back into the socket. This varies by joint. Practice on animals first. A successfully relocated joint moves normally again almost immediately, though it will be sore and swollen.

### Choking

The airway is blocked by food or an object.

1. **If the person can cough**: let them cough. Do not interfere.
2. **If they cannot cough, speak, or breathe**: stand behind them. Place the heel of your hand against their back between the shoulder blades. Strike firmly, five times.
3. **If that fails**: wrap your arms around their waist from behind. Make a fist just above the navel, below the rib cage. Grasp your fist with your other hand. Pull sharply inward and upward. Repeat until the blockage is expelled or the person loses consciousness.
4. **If they lose consciousness**: lay them on their back. Look in the mouth and remove any visible object. Begin chest pushes (as in heart-stopped procedure) — these can expel the obstruction.

## Part 5: Infection — The Invisible Killer

You cannot see what causes infection with your naked eye. But you can observe its rules with perfect consistency.

### The Rules

**Clean = less infection.** This has been observed for thousands of years:
- Wounds washed with clean water heal more often than unwashed wounds.
- Wounds treated by hands that have been washed heal more often than wounds treated by dirty hands.
- Tools (knives, needles) boiled in water before use cause dramatically less infection than unboiled tools.
- Cloth boiled before being used as bandages causes less infection.

**Dirty = more infection.**
- Wounds contaminated with soil, feces, or dirty water become infected far more often.
- Surgical procedures performed in dirty conditions kill far more patients than those performed in clean conditions.
- Crowded, filthy living conditions produce epidemics. Clean water and waste removal prevent them.

### What Infection Looks Like

1. Redness spreading outward from a wound.
2. Swelling and heat at the wound site.
3. Pus — thick, yellowish-white fluid draining from the wound.
4. Fever — the body temperature rises.
5. Red streaks running from the wound toward the heart (along blood vessels). **This is extremely dangerous** — the infection has entered the blood.

### What Fights Infection

**Honey**: applied directly to a wound, honey prevents or slows infection. This has been observed for thousands of years across unconnected civilizations. Honey creates conditions that infections cannot thrive in. It also draws moisture out of the wound, which helps.

**Boiled water**: always clean wounds with water that has been boiled. Boiling kills whatever causes infection in the water.

**Alcohol**: strong spirits (distilled drink) applied to wounds reduce infection. It stings intensely but the wound heals cleaner.

**Vinegar**: diluted vinegar can be used to clean wounds when nothing else is available.

**Certain molds**: this is a critical observation. Blue-green mold that grows on bread, fruit, or in damp places produces a substance that kills infection. If you place moldy bread near an infected wound or prepare a wash from mold-contaminated water, the infection retreats in some cases. This observation was made many times across history. The substance in the mold can be extracted and concentrated, but even in crude form, it has observable effect.

**Maggots**: fly larvae placed on infected wounds eat dead and infected tissue while leaving healthy tissue alone. The wound cleans itself. This is disgusting and effective. It has been used in field medicine for centuries.

**Silver**: water stored in silver vessels stays cleaner longer than water in other containers. Silver surfaces resist the growth of whatever causes infection. Silver-infused bandages reduce wound infection.

**Garlic**: crushed raw garlic applied to wounds or consumed during illness has observable antiseptic and antimicrobial effects. This has been recorded across civilizations that never contacted each other.

## Part 6: Childbirth

Most births proceed without intervention. The mother's body does the work. Your role is to keep conditions clean and to intervene only when necessary.

### Normal Birth

1. Labor: rhythmic contractions of the abdomen, increasing in frequency and intensity over hours.
2. The waters break: a gush of fluid from the birth canal. This is normal.
3. Delivery: the child emerges head-first, face down. Support the head as it appears. Do not pull. The body follows.
4. **The cord**: a rope-like structure connecting the child's belly to the afterbirth (placenta) inside the mother. After the child is delivered and breathing, tie the cord tightly in two places — a few finger-widths from the child's belly, and another a few finger-widths further along. Cut between the ties with a clean, sharp tool. The stump on the child dries and falls off naturally in days.
5. **First breath**: the child should cry and breathe within seconds of delivery. If not, clear the mouth and nose of fluid. Rub the back firmly. Blow gently into the mouth and nose.
6. **The afterbirth**: within 30 minutes after delivery, the placenta is expelled. Inspect it — it should be complete. If pieces remain inside the mother, she is at risk of severe bleeding and infection.

### When Things Go Wrong

- **Breech (feet or bottom first)**: dangerous. Attempt to turn the child before labor advances by gentle manipulation through the mother's belly. If the child cannot be turned and delivery stalls, the danger to both mother and child is extreme.
- **Heavy bleeding after delivery**: the uterus should contract and close the blood vessels where the placenta was attached. Firm massage of the lower abdomen encourages this. If bleeding does not slow, the situation is critical.
- **Cord around the neck**: if the cord is wrapped around the child's neck during delivery, slip it gently over the head before the body delivers. If it is too tight to slip, clamp and cut it immediately.

### The Critical Rule

**Clean conditions during birth dramatically reduce death of both mother and child.** Clean hands, clean tools, clean cloths, clean surface. Boil everything that will touch the mother or child. Wash hands thoroughly before and during the process.

Historically, the single greatest improvement in childbirth survival was handwashing. Nothing else comes close.

## Part 7: Common Diseases and What Helps

### Fever

The body is fighting something. Do not panic at fever — it is a defense mechanism.

- Cool with damp cloths on the forehead, neck, and wrists.
- Give water frequently.
- Rest.
- If fever is extremely high (the person is delirious or seizing), cool more aggressively — wet the entire body.

### Diarrhea and Vomiting

The body is expelling something. The danger is not the diarrhea itself — it is the water loss.

**Rehydration solution**: dissolve a pinch of salt and a small amount of sugar or honey in clean water. Give small sips continuously. This replaces the water AND the salts the body is losing. Without both, the body cannot absorb the water effectively.

This simple mixture saves more lives than almost any other intervention in this entire section.

### Scurvy

Bleeding gums. Loose teeth. Weakness and fatigue. Old wounds reopen. Bruising.

**Cause**: lack of something found in fresh fruit and vegetables. Observable because the disease appears only in populations eating preserved or grain-only diets for months.

**Cure**: fresh citrus fruit (lemons, limes, oranges), fresh greens, rose hips, raw potatoes. Symptoms reverse within days of eating these foods.

### Malaria

Recurring cycles of high fever, intense chills, drenching sweats, exhaustion. The cycle repeats every 2-3 days.

**Observation**: malaria occurs where certain mosquitoes are common. Regions without these mosquitoes do not have malaria. Sleeping under fine nets that prevent mosquito bites reduces malaria dramatically.

**Treatment**: bark of the cinchona tree, brewed into a bitter tea (quinine). This has been observed to suppress the fever cycles.

**Prevention**: drain standing water where mosquitoes breed. Sleep under nets. Burn smoky fires near sleeping areas (smoke repels mosquitoes).

### Tetanus

Muscle stiffness and spasms, especially in the jaw (the jaw locks shut), following a dirty wound — particularly deep punctures.

**Prevention**: clean ALL wounds thoroughly, especially deep ones. Remove all debris.

**Once symptoms begin**: there is no reliable treatment with basic materials. Prevention is everything.

### Wound Infections — Gangrene

If an infected wound turns black and the dead tissue spreads, the limb is dying. The dead tissue produces poison that enters the blood and kills.

The only reliable treatment is removal of the dead tissue. In severe cases, this means amputation of the limb above the infection. This is brutal and necessary. A person can survive without a limb. They cannot survive gangrene spreading to the torso.

## Part 8: Nutrition — What the Body Needs

### Water

The most critical substance. An adult needs roughly 2-3 liters per day, more in heat or during exertion. Without water, death follows in approximately three days.

Always prioritize clean water. Boil water from unknown sources before drinking. Moving water from streams is generally safer than standing water from ponds.

### Salt

Small amounts daily. Found in sea water (evaporate to collect), rock deposits, and animal blood. Without salt, the body's muscles cramp, the mind becomes confused, and eventually the heart fails.

### Food Variety

Bodies that eat many different foods stay healthier than bodies that eat only one thing.

- **Grain alone is not enough.** People who eat only grain develop deficiency diseases.
- **Meat alone is not enough.** People who eat only meat develop different deficiency diseases.
- **Variety provides balance.** Combine grains, vegetables, fruit, fat, and protein.

### Fat

Essential. The body requires it for warmth, energy storage, brain function, and organ protection. Sources: animal fat, nuts, seeds, oily fish, dairy, olive oil.

People who eat no fat weaken, lose weight uncontrollably, and die. Fat is not optional.

### Protein

The building material. The body uses it to repair damage and build new tissue. Sources: meat, fish, eggs, beans, lentils, dairy, nuts.

Growing children need more protein relative to their size than adults.

## Part 9: Teeth

A tooth infection can kill you. This is not an exaggeration. Before modern intervention, tooth abscess was a leading cause of death. The infection enters the bone, then the blood, then the whole body.

### Prevention

- **Clean your teeth daily.** Rub them with a frayed twig (chew the end of a fresh stick until the fibers separate — this is a brush). The mechanical action of rubbing removes the film that causes decay.
- **Salt water rinse.** Dissolve a pinch of salt in warm water. Swish it through the mouth and spit. Do this daily or after eating. Salt suppresses the growth of whatever causes tooth decay.
- **Avoid prolonged sugar contact.** Honey, dried fruit, and sweet foods accelerate decay if residue sits on the teeth.
- **Chew fibrous foods.** Raw vegetables, tough meat, and fibrous plants clean the teeth naturally.

### Signs of Infection

- A tooth that aches constantly, especially throbbing pain that worsens at night.
- Swelling in the gum around the tooth, or in the jaw or cheek.
- Foul taste or pus draining near the tooth.
- Fever accompanying tooth pain.

### Treatment

**If an abscess forms** (swollen area of pus at the gum line): lance it. Pierce the swelling with a sharp, boiled needle or blade. The pus drains. Rinse with warm salt water several times a day. This may resolve the infection.

**If the tooth is destroyed** (cracked, loose, blackened, or the source of recurring infection): extract it. This is painful and necessary.

Extraction method:
1. Have the person bite on a cloth soaked in strong spirits (alcohol) to numb the area slightly.
2. Grip the tooth firmly with pliers or a forged extraction tool — grip as close to the root as possible, not just the crown.
3. Rock the tooth back and forth gently to loosen it in the socket.
4. Pull firmly along the axis of the root. Do not yank sideways — this breaks the root and leaves fragments.
5. Once removed, have the person bite on a clean cloth pad to control bleeding.
6. Rinse with salt water. Watch for infection over the following days.

A pulled tooth is better than a dead patient. Remove the source of infection before it spreads.

## Part 10: The Mind

The body includes the mind. Ignoring this section kills as surely as ignoring infection, just slower and less visibly.

### Observable Facts

**Prolonged isolation damages the mind.** Humans kept alone for extended periods — weeks to months — develop confusion, hallucinations, inability to concentrate, and despair. Humans are social animals. Solitary confinement is a form of torture, and the damage is directly observable.

**Grief is physical.** The loss of someone close produces chest pain, appetite loss, disrupted sleep, fatigue, and difficulty thinking. These are not weakness. They are observable physiological responses. They ease with time, but the process cannot be skipped.

**Trauma changes behavior.** A person who has survived violence, injury, disaster, or extreme fear may experience: repeated intrusive memories, startling at sounds or sudden movements, difficulty sleeping, emotional numbness, avoidance of situations that resemble the original event. These responses are predictable and consistent across individuals and cultures. They are the mind's equivalent of a wound — observable, real, and requiring care.

**Sustained stress breaks the body.** A person under constant threat, constant overwork, or constant fear develops: digestive problems, muscle tension, sleep disruption, weakened immunity (they get sick more often), and eventually collapse. Stress is not imaginary. Its effects on the body are as measurable as a fever.

### What Helps

- **Being heard.** A person in distress who is listened to — without judgment, without advice, without being told to stop — recovers faster than one who is told to be strong and move on. This is consistently observable.
- **Physical work.** Movement, labor, building, walking — these reduce the grip of intrusive thoughts and restore a sense of agency. The body doing something useful helps the mind.
- **Routine.** Regular meals, regular sleep, predictable daily structure. A mind in chaos stabilizes faster when the body is given a rhythm.
- **Sleep.** Sleep deprivation causes hallucinations, impaired judgment, emotional volatility, and eventually psychosis. The mind requires sleep as absolutely as the body requires water. Protect sleep.
- **Community.** Shared purpose, shared meals, shared work, shared grief. Humans heal in groups. Isolated healing is slower and less reliable.
- **Time.** Most acute mental distress eases with time if the conditions above are provided. Do not mistake slow recovery for permanent damage.

### What Does Not Help

- Telling someone to "get over it."
- Shaming someone for fear, grief, or confusion.
- Isolation as punishment.
- Denial of what happened.

These make the damage worse and extend recovery. Every community that has survived catastrophe learned this through observation.

## Part 11: Poisoning, Bites, and Drowning

### Poisoning — Something Swallowed

**If recently swallowed (within minutes) and the substance is NOT caustic** (does not burn the lips, mouth, or throat):

Induce vomiting. Push a finger to the back of the throat, or have the person drink a large quantity of warm salt water. Expelling the substance reduces the dose absorbed.

**If the substance IS caustic** (burns on contact — strong acids, lye, certain cleaning materials):

Do NOT induce vomiting. It will burn the throat and mouth a second time on the way up. Instead, dilute: have the person drink water or milk to reduce the concentration in the stomach.

**Activated charcoal**: crush charcoal (from Section 5) to a fine powder. Mix with water. Have the person drink it. Charcoal absorbs many poisons in the stomach, reducing the amount that enters the blood. This is not a cure — it is a reduction of dose. It works for many plant and mineral poisons. It does NOT work for strong acids, alkalis, or alcohols.

**General**: keep the person calm, hydrated, and conscious. Watch breathing. If breathing stops, perform rescue breaths (see Part 3). If the person is unconscious, lay them on their side so vomit does not block the airway.

### Snake and Insect Bites

Not all bites are venomous. Many snakes and most insects deliver bites that are painful but not dangerous. The dangerous ones are identifiable by what happens next.

**Signs of dangerous venom:**
- Rapid swelling that spreads beyond the bite area.
- Difficulty breathing or swallowing.
- Vision changes (blurred, double).
- Numbness or tingling spreading from the bite.
- Nausea, vomiting, dizziness within minutes.
- Uncontrolled bleeding from the bite (blood that will not clot).

**What to do:**
1. Keep the person still and calm. Movement spreads venom faster through the body.
2. Immobilise the bitten limb. Splint it (see Part 5) if possible.
3. Keep the bite at or below heart level.
4. Remove rings, bracelets, or tight clothing near the bite — swelling will make them dangerous.
5. Clean the bite gently. Do not scrub.
6. Mark the edge of any swelling with charcoal and note the time. This tracks how fast the venom is spreading.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do NOT cut the bite open.
- Do NOT try to suck the venom out.
- Do NOT apply a tourniquet. Cutting off blood flow to a limb causes more damage than the venom in most cases.
- Do NOT apply ice or cold packs directly.
- Do NOT give alcohol.

Many venomous bites are survivable with rest, immobilisation, and time. The body can process small doses of many venoms. The key is reducing the speed at which venom circulates.

### Drowning

**Rescue**: do NOT swim directly toward a drowning person face-on. They will grab you in panic and pull you under. Approach from behind. Grab them by the hair, collar, or under the arms. Tow them to safety while keeping your own airway clear.

If you cannot swim to them: throw a rope, a log, a branch — anything that floats. Extend a pole or stick from shore.

**Once out of the water:**

1. Check breathing. If the person is not breathing, begin rescue breaths immediately (see Part 3).
2. Clear the airway: turn the head to the side, let water drain from the mouth. Remove any visible obstructions.
3. Begin chest compressions if there is no heartbeat.
4. Even if the person appears recovered, watch them closely for hours. Water in the lungs can cause delayed breathing failure.

### Poisonous Plants — What to Avoid

Teach every child: do not eat any plant, berry, or mushroom unless an experienced adult has confirmed it is safe. The following patterns indicate danger:

- **White berries**: most are poisonous. Avoid.
- **Plants with milky sap**: many are toxic. Avoid eating unless the specific plant is known to be safe.
- **Mushrooms**: see Section 8 (Fungi) for identification rules. When in doubt, do not eat.
- **Bitter or burning taste**: if a plant tastes extremely bitter or burns the mouth, spit it out and rinse. Most edible plants taste mild or slightly sweet/earthy.

## Part 12: Pain and Fever — Relief from Plants

### Willow Bark

The inner bark of willow trees contains a substance that reduces pain and fever. This is directly observable and has been used across unconnected cultures for thousands of years. The same substance was eventually synthesised into a tablet — but the bark works on its own.

**Preparation:**
1. Strip fresh inner bark (the moist layer under the outer bark) from young willow branches.
2. Chop or shred the bark.
3. Steep in hot water for 10-15 minutes.
4. Drink the tea. It is bitter.

**Effects**: reduces headache, muscle pain, joint pain, fever, and tooth pain. Takes effect within an hour. Repeat as needed, but do not consume in large quantities — excessive use irritates the stomach.

**Caution**: do not give to children under 12 with fever. Do not give to anyone with stomach bleeding.

### Clove

Dried cloves (the flower buds of the clove tree) contain a powerful numbing agent.

**For toothache**: crush a dried clove. Apply the crushed material directly to the aching tooth and surrounding gum. Hold in place. The area numbs within minutes. This does not fix the underlying problem — see Part 9 (Teeth) — but it provides relief while you prepare for treatment.

Clove oil (if you can press or distill it) is more concentrated. A drop on a cloth pressed against the tooth works faster.

### Peppermint and Chamomile

**Peppermint tea**: relieves nausea, stomach cramps, and digestive distress. Steep fresh or dried peppermint leaves in hot water. Drink slowly. The effect is mild but consistent.

**Chamomile tea**: calms anxiety, aids sleep, soothes mild stomach upset. Steep dried chamomile flowers in hot water. Drink before sleep for rest. Drink during the day for calm.

These are not powerful medicines. They are mild, safe, observable aids with no significant danger in normal doses.

### Poppy

**EXTREME CAUTION.** The sap of the unripe seed pod of the opium poppy is the most powerful natural pain reliever known. It is also one of the most dangerous and addictive substances in nature.

- **For severe, acute pain only**: surgery, crushed limbs, major burns, dying patients.
- **Never for chronic pain.** The body adapts — you need more and more for the same effect. Withdrawal produces severe physical illness. Addiction destroys individuals and communities.
- **Dose matters.** The difference between a pain-relieving dose and a lethal dose is small. A slight excess stops breathing.

We document this because the knowledge exists and cannot be un-known. Use it only when the alternative is worse. Guard access to it like a weapon — because it is one.

The body is resilient. It heals itself if you give it clean conditions, water, food, and time. Your job is to not make things worse, to keep things clean, and to intervene only when the body cannot handle it alone.
