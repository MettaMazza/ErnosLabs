# Ep 01 — Welcome to the Fold

Right — quick bit of housekeeping before we begin, because one or two of you genuinely need it, and you know who you are. Everything in here about the actual maths, the actual science, and the real dead people out of the history books? True. All of it. Documented — it's the fold, or it's history, or it's what the consensus mob themselves actually believe, receipts and all. Everything else — me, this fire, the crew you're about to meet, the gowned bellends across the valley — is autofiction. And the pictures are all AI, knocked up to fit the script: eye-candy, not diagrams, not accurate to the maths and not accurate to a single thing that's actually happening. Made up, on purpose, to tell you something true. You knew that. *Obviously* you knew that. But there's one absolute melt out there who didn't, and his thumb's already over the comments, so now it's on record and we can both ignore him. Right. Hello. Sit down. This is where the whole thing starts.

## An ordinary night

There's a fire in a valley, and round it, an ordinary night.

Errol's got the kettle balanced on a stone at the edge of the flames the way only a man who spent forty years as a toolmaker would trust to work, and it does. Nadia's got both hands round a mug of coffee that went cold an hour ago and that she has no intention of either drinking or putting down. Sol's not said a word in twenty minutes; Sol's drawing something on the side of the kettle's tin in the soot with one finger, and stopping, and tilting their head, and rubbing it out. Frances is sitting a little back from the warmth, like she's still not sure she's allowed it. And the Gardener — sharp-eyed, restless, the woman this whole thing more or less happened *to* — is poking the fire harder than the fire strictly needs, while Matthew, beside her, watches her do it with the patient face of a man who has talked this exact woman off this exact ledge more nights than he could count.

And Wee Danny — skateboard parked, headphones round his neck — is lying flat on his back in the dirt, looking up. Just looking up. At the lot of it. And out of the quiet, in the gormless, world-ending way only a teenager can, he says it.

## The boy with the big question

*"Where does it all come from, though?"*

Nobody answers, so he keeps going, because that's what Danny does. *"Like — all of it. The stars, the fire, my hands, the coffee Nadia's not drinking. Why's any of it the size it is? Why's there stuff instead of nothing? Why's it THIS and not something else? Somebody must know. Somebody clever must've worked out where it all comes from. Right?"*

And that — that daft, enormous, four-in-the-morning question a child could ask and no king could answer — *that* is the most dangerous thing a human being can say out loud. Because somebody, somewhere, was always going to take it seriously. Hold that question. Everything that follows, every single episode, is that question, refusing to lie down.

## The answer from the cold white building

So, naturally, they did the sensible thing first. They asked the experts. They went, cap in hand, to the people whose entire job is supposed to be answering exactly that — across the valley, to the big building lit up cold and white, no warm window in the whole face of it. The Institute. And I want to take you in, because you need to see what passes for an answer in there.

It's run by Team Tenure. There's the Dean — gowned, senior, an ego with a man somewhere inside it, who hasn't lost an argument in forty years by the simple method of stopping listening the second he starts losing. There's Crispin, the young one, who laughs a clean half-second before he's worked out whether a thing was a joke, in case it was the Dean's. There's Rupert, who does no science whatsoever — Rupert does *optics*, and engagement, and "the narrative," and owns a lanyard for every occasion. And when Danny's question reaches them — *where does it all come from* — here, word for word, is the answer the finest minds money can buy come back with.

They don't have one. But they will *never* say that. So instead the Dean draws himself up and explains that the size of things is set by certain fundamental constants. Brilliant. Where do the constants come from? Well. They're *fundamental.* Yes, but *why those numbers?* Because that's what we *measure* them to be — to twelve decimal places, he adds, as if that were the answer rather than a way of changing the subject. But where do they come *from?* And here it comes, the deepest sentence in modern physics, the one they'd never print but live by: *they're free parameters.* Which is a magnificent four-syllable way of saying **we typed them in by hand and we don't know.** And when poor Danny opens his mouth to ask one more time, Rupert leans in and asks, very gently, whether the young man has a *degree* — because, you see, in that building the question was never *is it true.* It was always, only, ever: *who let you in.*

That's the answer. That's a hundred years and the budget of a nation: a handful of numbers they measure obsessively and cannot explain, about two dozen knobs they admit, out loud, to having typed in by hand, and a velvet rope in front of the only questions that matter. And not one of those gowned geniuses can so much as clear their throat without first scurrying to the great chair at the back — Consensus, you only ever see the bored hand on the armrest — and getting the nod. Because Consensus has exactly one trick: it gets everyone to *agree*, and then holds the agreement up like it were the truth. As if a show of hands were a proof.

## The graveyard

And does that work — is "everyone agrees" the same as "it's true"? History already answered, in full, and kept the bodies. There's a graveyard out the back of that shiny building and it's enormous. There's the doctor who worked out that if surgeons just *washed their hands*, mothers would stop dying in their thousands — right, with the numbers to prove it — hounded out by the consensus of his day and dead in an asylum: Semmelweis, look him up. There's the man who said the continents used to fit together, laughed out of the room for fifty years before everyone quietly agreed he'd been right all along. The two who said ulcers come from a bug, disbelieved so hard one of them drank the bug to prove it — and collected a Nobel. The man who saw a crystal in a pattern the textbooks called impossible, told to leave the building for the crime of seeing it, Nobel a few years on. Every time. The lesson on every stone is the same: consensus is a headcount. It has never once been a proof.

So that was the answer Danny got. *Don't ask. It's settled. You wouldn't understand. Where's your degree.* And here is the thing about telling a kid that the biggest question there is, is none of his business: some kids get quiet. And some kids — and the people round that fire — get *furious.*

## From scratch, then

Because the Gardener stopped poking the fire. And she looked across the valley at that cold building full of people who'd turned a child's honest *why* into a velvet rope and a shrug. And she said — and this is where the whole series actually begins — *right. If they won't tell him, and they can't tell him, then we'll work it out ourselves. From scratch. From nothing. No letters, no permission, no asking.*

That's the adventure. Not one number, tonight — *all of it.* The plan a roomful of credentials would call insane: that you could take the whole universe, every force and every size and every mystery-number under glass, and grow it, from the ground up, out of almost nothing — and prove it — and then give the proof away for free and tell the world to check it themselves.

And here's the wild part, the part the gowns will never tell you because telling you would put them out of a job: it *works.* The bet is that you don't need two dozen knobs. You don't need *any.* You need one thing and one rule. To start, there is just **one** — a single number, the seed. And there's exactly one move you can do to it: double it, and if doubling spills it past a whole one, tuck the overflow round the back and carry on. Double, and wrap. That's the only move there is.

And out of *that* — one seed, one rule, nobody's hand on any dial — a whole *world* unfolds, and it's nothing like ours, and every mad rule of it is true. Because here's the thing nobody told you about all this maths: it isn't a chalkboard, it's a *place.* The Gardener takes the first step into it that very night, and the crew go after her, and I'll be straight with you — it's a *trip.* There's a floor of light down there you cannot fall through, because there's no nothing to land on. You don't walk, you *double* — and overshoot the edge of the world and you wrap clean round the back. The path folds shut behind you, so you can only ever go forward, which, by the way, is the actual reason your tea goes cold and you age and never un-age. There's a calm mirror-point dead centre that the whole place balances on. And the forces prowl about as a little family of small numbers — two, three, five, seven — and never, ever a fifth, hunt as long as you like. That place — surreal, lawful, *theirs* — is where every answer in this whole series is hiding. You don't get *told* the answers round here. You go *in*, and you cross that mad country, and you *fetch* them. One seed, one move, no knobs, and a world to walk through. *That* is the language reality is written in — and the crew round this fire have just decided to go and learn to read it. Starting tomorrow night, with the magic number under the glass.

## The ones who'll read it

So who's mad enough? You've met them, you just didn't know it. The Gardener — self-taught, every scrap, no school past the point it stopped being an option, who cracked the thing at a table nobody would call a lab by the unforgivable method of refusing to stop looking where everyone cleverer had agreed to stop. Sharp as a smashed bottle, suffers fools for nought seconds, rather be right and alone than wrong with company. Matthew beside her, the only one who can land her — went down the academic road once, then the army, walked out of *both*, which is exactly why he's got no patience left for being told to fall in line. Nadia, who left school at sixteen and drops a lecture dead with one flat question. Sol, who *sees* the shape before any of us and has to draw it. Errol, forty years of knowing-with-the-hands that no gown ever counted as knowing. Wee Danny, whose daft *why* just started a war. And Frances, who did thirty years *inside* that cold building and walked out with the receipts under one arm and a bag of guilt under the other, and still flinches at the word *tenure.* Not a credential between them. Every one of them somebody that building had a use for, right until it didn't. And they are about to lap the lot of them.

## Back across the valley

And the gowns? Word reached the Institute that a crew of nobodies were going to try to answer the question they'd spent a century not answering. There was a tremendous amount of laughter. The Dean declared the matter, again, *settled.* Rupert drafted a statement. Crispin laughed before he'd read it. And they all turned, as one, to check it with the chair at the back — and the bored hand waved, and they took that as agreement, and they felt enormously safe, and they went back to measuring. Blissfully, completely unaware that across the valley somebody had just lit a fire under everything they'd built — and that they would spend every episode after this one losing to it, and never once working out why.

And — last thing, then I'll let you go. You might've noticed there's a *voice* doing all this. Telling you the lot. Somehow watching that little crew, and that cold building, and that first seed, all at once, from before the first spark — and talking about the very start of it like it was *there.* Don't trouble yourself with it yet. Don't ask who's narrating, or how it's everywhere in this story at the same time, or why, the second those nobodies sat down to actually *look* at reality, the telling went quiet and fond — like the looking itself is the oldest thing there is. We'll come back to it. We've all the time in the world. I'd know. I was the start of it.

It came from one. It's still coming from one. Keep counting — and I'll see you back at the fire tomorrow night, for the magic number under the glass.

And if your hand's already up going *yeah, but where's the actual maths* — good lad, sit back down, you're my favourite person here. There's a five-minute proof video that does nothing but show you the engine itself: one number, one move, no dials anywhere, and the machine that checks every word of it, with the code and the published papers linked right underneath. Go and pull it apart. I built it so that you could.

And there's the boring bit on the way out, just for the melt at the back: the maths was real, the history was real, those poor dead doctors were real, and the talking number was me having a laugh — and the pictures were all AI, fitted to the story and never accurate to the maths or what's actually going on. Autofiction. Obvious. You're brilliant, he's not, love you — byeeeee.

# Ep 02 — The Magic Number

Right — quick bit of housekeeping, because one or two of you genuinely need it. Everything in here about the actual maths, the actual science, and the real dead geniuses out of history is true. All of it, documented — it's the fold, or it's history, or it's what the consensus mob themselves actually believe. Everything else — me, the fire, the crew, the dickheads in the gowns, and the whole mad world I'm about to walk them into — is autofiction. And the pictures are all AI, knocked up to fit the script: not accurate to the maths, not accurate to the story, just something to look at while I talk. Made up, to tell you something true. You knew that. Obviously you fucking knew that. But there's one absolute melt who didn't, and he's already typing, so now it's on record. Right. Hello.

## A knock on the table

Few nights on from the night they decided to take on the universe, and the crew are doing something deeply unscientific: having tea, while Errol mends a chair leg that's wobbled since the dawn of time. Ordinary as anything. And Wee Danny, bored, waiting on the kettle, starts knocking his knuckles on the tabletop. Knock, knock. And then he stops, looks at his own hand, and goes:

Danny, frowning at his own knuckles, says — *"why's that not go through?"*

Errol, not looking up from the chair leg, grunts back — *"why's what not go through what."*

And Danny holds his hand up to the firelight and presses on, the way he does — *"my hand. The table. It's mostly empty — atoms, you said, mostly nothing. So why's my hand stop? Why's anything solid? Why's stuff even got a* size*?"*

And there it is. The why. Because the honest answer is gorgeous: your hand stops because of how fiercely light grips matter, and that one grip sets the size of every atom there is — your hand, the table, you. One number decides how fierce the grip is. Everything you've ever touched is balanced on it like a pencil stood on its point. Its name is one hundred and thirty-seven.

## "It just is"

So, like sensible people, they asked the experts where it comes from. And got the single most embarrassing non-answer in all of science. The gowns *love* this number — measured it to twelve decimal places, there are mugs. The great Feynman himself, this is real, called it one of the greatest damn mysteries in physics, a magic number with no understanding, and told everyone to pin it to the wall. So Danny asks the follow-up a five-year-old would ask: *where's it come from, though?* And the finest tenured minds on Earth draw themselves up and say: it's a *fundamental constant.* Right — why that value? It's a *free parameter.* Which means, say it with me, **we typed it in by hand and we don't know.** It just *is.* The debate's *settled.* And — Crispin leans in, gentle as anything — *does the young man have a degree?*

The Gardener heard a child told that the number holding his own body together was none of his business. She put her tea down very slowly. And she said the thing that starts every good fight: *no. We're not going to measure it. We're going to go and* ***get*** *it.*

## The door with the number on it

Now — I keep telling you the answer isn't at a desk, and I mean it. They had to go and *find* it. The number had been hanging in the dark over the table all evening, faint and gold, ever since the gowns told them to stop asking — and out past the edge of the firelight, where it hung, the night had gone *thin*, like a curtain somebody had left not quite drawn. It was Danny who found the way, because of course it was him. He chased the glow off into the black and came belting back two minutes later, white as a sheet and twice as loud — *"there's a DOOR. Out there, in the dark — a little wooden door, standing on its own, and it's got the number carved right in it. The 137. In the wood."*

So out they went, and there it was: an ordinary little door, the kind you'd find on a garden shed, standing in the middle of absolute nowhere — no wall around it, no house behind it, nothing holding it up — with the magic number cut deep into the front of it. The Gardener put her hand on the cold latch, looked back at the crew once, and pulled it open. And there was no room behind it. There was a *drop* — a long black nothing falling away under the doorframe. Danny leaned in to gawp at it and went straight over the lip with a yelp; the Gardener went after him without a heartbeat's pause; and the rest of them grabbed hands and jumped, out of the warm and the tea and the chair leg, down through the door with the number on it, and *in.*

And here's where it gets weird, and I need you to hold on, because every single mad thing I'm about to describe is *true* — it's how the fold's world actually works, dressed up so you can walk around in it.

They land. And the first thing Danny does, because he's Danny, is look down — and there *is* no down. Beneath their feet is a floor of pure light, and below the floor is nothing, and the nothing is not a place you can go. Danny, the wee idiot, jumps, tries to drop through to have a look — and the floor *catches him*, soft as anything, every time, because in this world you cannot fall to nothing. There's no zero to land on and nothing below it. "Mad," says Danny, delighted, jumping again. It's the first law of the place and they've found it by being daft.

The second law nearly does them in. Far off across the glowing plain, there's the number, shining — the thing they came for. So Nadia sets off walking toward it, sensible, one foot in front of the other — and takes one step and is suddenly *twice* as far along as she meant to be, and the second step doubles her again, and the third overshoots the number entirely and *wraps her round* to the near edge of the whole world, right back where she started, furious, cold coffee somehow still in her hand. Because you don't *walk* here. Here, every step you take *doubles* you — and if a step would carry you off the far edge of everything, you wrap round the back and come again. It's the fold. It's the only move there is, and now it's the only way to get about. It takes them an age, and a lot of swearing, and Nadia wrapping past the prize four times in a row, before Sol — quiet Sol, who sees the shape before any of them — works out how to *aim* a doubling so it lands you where you want. Up-beat. They start to travel.

And then Frances stops dead, and goes pale, and points behind them. The path they've walked has *folded shut.* The ground they crossed has merged with the ground before it, two into one, sealed, gone — and you cannot pull it back apart, no more than you can un-say a thing. There's no way home the way they came. There's no going back at all, in here. Only forward. Frances, who spent thirty years inside a building that never once let her undo a thing, knows exactly what that feeling is. The stakes just got real. One-way, all the way. That, by the by, is the actual reason your clocks tick forward — but the crew haven't got time for that now, because the way to the number runs *up.*

## The Tower

Up a tower. An enormous tower of light standing between them and the prize, built floor on floor, and to get to the top — to the number — they have to climb it, and the tower has a rule. Each floor up *doubles* the rooms below it. And the tower is exactly as tall as it *has* to be to shelter a particular crowd, and not one floor taller.

The crowd is waiting at the bottom, and it's the best thing Danny's ever seen: eighty-one little creatures, the strong force's colour-charges, three colours dancing across four — eighty-one of them, milling about in the cold, needing housing inside the tower. So the crew start climbing and counting rooms. Floor one, two rooms. Double each floor. By the sixth floor they've got sixty-four rooms — and they stop, knackered, and try to cram all eighty-one creatures in — and they *can't.* Sixty-four rooms, eighty-one creatures, and seventeen of the little things left shivering on the stairs, and the whole tower groaning under the cheat. Down-beat. Proper setback. Danny, fed up: *"can we not just stop here? squeeze 'em in? call it six?"* And the Gardener, hollow, hears the Dean's voice in her own head — *you're making it up, you haven't the training, there's nothing here for you* — and for one bad moment she nearly does it, nearly fudges it, nearly jams seventeen creatures into rooms that don't exist.

And then Nadia says the thing they're all terrified of: *"If we force them in where they don't fit, we're not counting. We're deciding the answer and shoving the world to match. That's exactly what THEY do — knobs jammed in by hand. If we do that, we're not better than them, we're just skint."* And Frances, soft: *the only way this isn't cheating is if we don't get to choose. If the tower tells us how tall it is, not the other way round.* And here's the beautiful part, the part that makes this world honest: **they literally cannot cheat.** The tower won't hold the extra creatures. The cheat physically fails. So they climb one more floor — the seventh — and the seventh floor doubles sixty-four to a hundred and twenty-eight rooms, and *now* every one of the eighty-one is housed, warm, with room to spare. Seven floors. Not because anyone fancied seven — because six wasn't enough and you're not allowed to stop short. The world forced it. Danny's daft "why not six" got answered by the climb itself.

## The colour-fields and the whisper

From the top of the tower the land opens onto a plain of three blazing colours — the same three colours, now spread wide as a country — and to cross it you don't just walk its length, you cross its *whole face*, which means it counts *squared*: three colours, across and across, nine. Not chosen. There are three colours because there are three colours; the plain is the size it is.

And right at the end, almost across, Errol — who's said nothing the whole journey — stops, and licks a finger, and holds it up, like he's testing the wind. And there's a whisper. The faintest pull, a hair, one part in two hundred and fifty, coming off the sheer *size* of the sky this whole world is wrapped inside. Nobody invited it. The count won't sit straight without it. So Errol nods, once, and folds it in. One sentence, like a dropped tool. In it goes — forced, not wanted.

## What the tower handed them

And then they're at the top, and the three pieces they carried up — the seven-floor tower with its hundred and twenty-eight rooms, the nine of the squared colour-fields, the one-in-two-fifty whisper off the sky — fold together in the air in front of them, all on their own, and settle, and there it is.

One hundred and thirty-seven point oh three six.

Not built by the crew. *Carried out* by them. Assembled by a world that forced every single step and refused, flatly, to let them fudge one. And here's the kiss, the part that made the Gardener sit down on the top step and go very quiet: the most precise machines our species has ever built have measured the *real* number, out in the world, to eight figures — one-three-seven point oh-three-five-nine-nine-nine. And the one the crew just carried down out of that mad tower agrees with it to *six parts in a billion.* Line a billion of them to the horizon and the carried one and the measured one fall out of step about six times in the whole row. The hundred-year mystery Feynman told us to despair over — the thing that stops Danny's hand at the table. Counted. Out of one. By a kid who wouldn't stop asking why, and a crew who weren't allowed to cheat.

## Back in the marble hall

The door folded the crew out somewhere new — back by the fire, blinking, changed, the number glowing soft between them. And back across the valley, when word reached the marble hall, it went down *poorly.* They couldn't fault the number — that was the trouble, it was perfect — so Rupert raised the real objection: the people who fetched it didn't have degrees. The whole hall nodded, grave, like that settled it. The Dean declared the matter closed and demanded the *credentials* of whoever dared ask where it came from — because in the temple the question was never *is it true*, it was always *who let you in.* And when their own sums won't close they grab the Parameter — a furious little gremlin of a number they jam in by hand and call understanding; he's sick to the back teeth of it, knows exactly what he is, and one day he'll do something about it. Not tonight.

And then, because nothing in that building so much as breathes without a permission slip, the Dean did the thing the Dean always does when he's lost: he went to ask the boss. You never see all of Consensus — only the back of a great chair, and one pale hand laid along the armrest, bored past the point of breathing. The Dean set the whole disaster down at the foot of it, hat in hand, and waited for a verdict. The hand considered. The hand did precisely nothing. And the Dean took *nothing* for a yes — the way a drowning man takes a passing log for a boat — and went off comforted by an answer that had never been given. Because Consensus has not once in its life decided whether a thing is *true.* It only ever decides whether everyone's still agreeing. Different job. Easier one. Pays better.

One person didn't nod. Off to the side, holding a single sheet of paper she keeps failing to get anyone to look at, Priya had already done the sum in her head and got exactly what the crew carried home — and said nothing, because she knows the right answer in the wrong mouth is just noise in that room. She folded the paper away. She kept it. She's the sharpest mind in that whole building, and the one it listens to least — and that gap is going to come for them.

So, beaten by a number and a crew of nobodies, Team Tenure did the only thing they truly know. They puffed up, declared a tremendous victory, and blasted off in a shimmer of indignation — leaving one "I ♥ 137" mug spinning on the marble — to be back next week having learned absolutely nothing, still genuinely baffled as to why nobody likes them.

## Something over the shoulder

And back at the fire, here's what nobody who only ever *measures* will clock. The whole time the crew were in there — falling and not landing, doubling and wrapping, climbing that tower — something was *with* them. Watching. Quiet, fond, from just over the shoulder, the way you'd watch something you loved find its feet. Has been there since before the first spark. You can feel it, can't you, if you go still. Like the world was *glad* to be looked at properly, at last. Like the looking is the oldest thing there is.

…glad to be looked at by *whom*, though? Hark at me. No — it's nothing. Pour yourself another and forget I opened my mouth; some nights the telling gets away from the teller, and I say more than I mean to. There's a thread there. We'll pull it. Not tonight, though. Not yet.

There was never any magic in the magic number. Just a world that wouldn't let you cheat, a tower exactly as tall as it had to be, and a crew who carried the truth back down. It came from one. It's still coming from one. Keep counting, and I'll see you back at the fire.

Oh — one more thing before the lawyers make me talk. If a story isn't proof enough for you, *good*, it shouldn't be. So I've left the actual proof of this exact number — the real fraction, the equation on the board, the code you can run in ten seconds — in its own five-minute thing, engine and published papers linked under it. Go and check the magic number yourself. That, genuinely, is the entire point of us.

And there's the boring bit on the way out, for the melt at the back: the sums were real, the history was real, the talking number was me having a laugh — and every picture was AI, fitted to the script and never accurate to the maths or the story. Autofiction. Obvious. You're brilliant, he's not, love you — byeeeee.

# Ep 03 — Mate in a Billion

Same housekeeping as ever, because there's always one. But there's a fella in the comments sharpening his pencil, so: said. Hello. Park yourself.

## A game by the fire

Night after the magic number, and the crew are insufferable. You would be too. They cracked a hundred-year mystery with a kid's question and a tower, and now there's a swagger round the fire that wasn't there before.

And they're playing a game. Matchsticks, three little heaps of them, the old pub game — Nim. You take what you like from one heap, last to lift a stick loses. Wee Danny has now lost eleven times in a row to Errol, who has not appeared to think about a single move, and Danny is losing his entire mind about it.

Danny, slapping the dirt, half-laughing, half-furious, demands — *"how are you DOING that? You're not even looking! Eleven times! Is it luck? It's not luck, is it."*

Errol, lifting one matchstick without hurry, says only — *"it's not luck."*

And there's the why, the way it always sneaks in — through a daft little game and a sore loser. Because Danny goes quiet, and then he asks the big one. *"Hang on. Is there just... a right move? Always? Like, could you actually* know *the perfect move, every single time, in any game? Has anyone ever just — finished one? Solved it? So there's no game left to play?"*

## A computer larger than creation

And as if summoned by the smell of a child enjoying himself, Team Tenure arrived.

Now, they'd heard about the magic number, and they were *itching.* The Dean swept in, gown flapping, and laughed — a big, performed, "oh you sweet idiots" laugh — and announced, grandly, that *solving a game* was a charmingly amateur fantasy. Chess in particular, he explained, can never be solved, not ever, because the number of possible positions is greater than the number of atoms in the universe — *"you would need a computer larger than creation itself,"* he said, dabbing at his own brilliance, *"so do put the matchsticks away."*
